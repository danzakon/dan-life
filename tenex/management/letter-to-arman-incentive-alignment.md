# A Note on Incentive Alignment

Arman,

I'm writing this down because I'd rather be thoughtful about how I say it than try to cover everything in a conversation where things can get lost or smoothed over in the moment.

I think you know me well enough to know that I'm writing this because I care about the company and I want it to succeed. I also think I'm in a unique position to tell you what I'm seeing and feeling, both from my own experience and from what I've heard from the rest of the team. A lot of people hold in their concerns or talk about them with each other but don't bring them to you directly. I think you deserve to hear it clearly from someone willing to say it, and as I look around I feel like I may be the only one willing.

---

## What I'm Observing

The concerns people have about incentive alignment are more significant than I think you realize. I'm saying this because I think there's a gap between how you perceive the team's sentiment and what the reality actually is.

When you talk to people and walk away feeling like things are smoothed over, there is a lot that doesn't get said in those conversations. People feel better in the moment because you're genuinely good at that, but the underlying concerns don't resolve.

Every early person I've talked to has expressed, in their own way, that they feel disconnected from the success of the company. We went from nothing to $15 million in ARR in about a year. Everyone recognizes how extraordinary that is. But the experience of that growth from where I sit and from what I'm hearing doesn't feel like winning. 

Someone said to me recently that it feels like the company is a rocket ship launching and everyone except you and Alex is standing on the ground looking up. That's painful to hear but I think it reflects how most people feel.

People didn't start feeling this way yesterday. Everyone who was here in 2025 felt like December was the moment it needed to be addressed because the next phase was predictable.

When this many of the core early people feel the same way independently, I think that points to something systemic rather than a bunch of individuals being unreasonable. The fact that people don't feel like they're winning when the company is clearly winning says a lot about the underlying structure.

---

## The Structural Issue

I think it's a structural problem with how incentives work right now, not a case of everyone being ungrateful or entitled. Even if you don't believe that to be the case, I want you to consider it honestly.

The original deal was high cash compensation, uncapped, based on output, no equity. But the reality of that deal hasn't matched the promise. You've referenced CJ making $750K as evidence that the comp model works, but that isn't what he actually earns annually. That was a short burst that wasn't sustainable, and he scaled back significantly. In practice, nobody has consistently earned more than $400K or $500K. That's a good salary, but it's not the $800K uncapped opportunity that was presented, and it's not a substitute for equity in a company growing this fast. Strategists don't have the same output-based incentive structure so it's even more acute for them. Everyone is capable of understanding the difference between cash and leverage.

The fundamental issue is that in any high-growth company, there has to be some mechanism that ties the team's success to the company's success. Every company that reaches this stage has one, whether that's equity, options, profit-sharing, or something else. You chose not to use the traditional version of that mechanism, and that's your right, but there are serious trade-offs.

You've mentioned not wanting to distribute equity because you don't want to be controlled by VCs and have had stressful experiences with them in the past. I fear that the stress you refer to (lack of control and misaligned incentives) has been absorbed by everyone else. More than anything, if there aren't any clear structures or rules of the road, people have no choice but to imagine the worst.

What that means in practice is that when new people come in and the company grows, the people who built the foundation don't benefit from that growth. New hires succeeding doesn't help the people who were here first. It creates competition for clients and story points. 

It's an incentive design problem, I think it creates a foreseeable crisis in the future, I think it's being underestimated, and I think it's urgent. People aren't rooting for each other or the company the way they should be and everyone can feel it.

---

## Trust and Communication

I need to be honest that trust has meaningfully eroded.

At the start of last quarter, you communicated that a leveraged upside program was coming. You said the charter was almost ready, that it was "on track" at every all-hands. People heard that and took it not as a vague aspiration but as a real timeline and commitment. Then communication about it stopped entirely with no update, no revised timeline, and no acknowledgment. In your mind that may have just been a priority that shifted. In everyone else's mind, it was a promise that was broken and then never addressed. I understand that priorities evolve, but the pattern of setting ambitious goals around compensation, missing them, and then never discussing it again creates a very specific kind of disillusionment.

That silence did more damage than I think you know because it confirmed a lot of peoples' fears that they are viewed as replaceable units of human capital or pawns.

When we talked on Friday you referred to rewarding people retroactively for being early as a "participation trophy." I get the intent behind that, you don't want to create entitlement, but I want you to understand how that lands with people who built this company from nothing. Those people don't see themselves as having merely participated, they see themselves as having built the thing.

---

## This Moment

The culture and incentive structure that exists right now is going to calcify. Whatever we are at 30 is what we'll be at 150, except harder to change.

I've said before that we're either going to decide the culture proactively or it's going to be decided for us. Right now there are visible cracks. New structural changes layered on top of an unresolved incentive problem intensifies everyone's feelings of replaceability and not being valued. Each wave of new people and new structures makes the early team wonder whether they're being built around or built over.

To put it very bluntly, the longer this goes unaddressed the more people quietly start thinking about what else they could be doing. I'd rather say something alarming right now while there's still time to solve it than wish I had said it six months from now.

---

## My Personal Context

I care about this personally and I'm not going to pretend I don't. I've told you that since day one. I helped build this company and I want that to be recognized, not as a favor but because it's true. 

I joined Tenex as the 3rd employee when we didn't even have a proper website. You asked me to take a bet on you, and I did. You told me to treat this like a partnership, and I took that seriously. I trusted you and I still do, which is why I feel like I can come to you with this. I've also said to you that I want to be here for many years, and I still do.

Since joining I've devoted every ounce of my available energy to this, working almost every night and every weekend. I want to continue doing that for years to come. I believe I've had a large hand in what we've built so far, and I believe I'll have a large hand in what we build next. I've told you many times that I'm willing to accept less short-term compensation for longer-term success, and you've agreed that that makes sense. 

If this were only a me thing, I wouldn't be writing this. I'm writing this because I think it's a structural issue that matters for the future of the company regardless of my individual situation. It's in Tenex's best interest to solve the incentive alignment problem, and it's very solvable. 

We have a growing, profitable business with more than enough success to go around. The only question that matters to people at the end of the day is whether there's a mechanism for sharing it.

For the last several quarters, when people came to me with concerns about shared upside and the future, I've been the one saying "there's a plan, your early contributions will be recognized, Arman is going to figure this out, trust the process." 

I'll continue to be your voice to the rest of the team, but I want to be honest that it's getting harder.

I'm asking you to take this seriously, to hear what I'm saying as someone who genuinely wants this company to succeed, and to recognize that solving this through actions and not words is one of the most important things you can do for Tenex right now.

Dan

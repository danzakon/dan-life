# Write Article

Write a long-form article draft from any input. Research explores. Articles argue.

## Inputs

This skill handles any starting point, with briefs being the preferred path:

- **A brief file** (from `content/briefs/YYYYMMDD-SRC-NNN.md`) — preferred. Contains core insight, your take, selected angle, and draft instructions. Generated by the content-interview step.
- A research report (from `research/reports/`)
- A bookmarked tweet or thread (with source attribution)
- A topic or question raised in conversation
- A link to external content worth responding to

When a brief is available, read it first — it contains the user's actual reaction and chosen angle. The article's perspective should build from the brief's core insight.

Read `content/pipeline/strategy.md` for current content themes and emphasis.

## The Core Principle

An article needs a **singular perspective**. A clear throughline that everything else supports. Multiple angles, examples, and sections all reinforce the main argument from different directions.

Think of it like a legal case: one argument, many pieces of evidence.

If you can't state the article's perspective in one sentence, you're not ready to write it yet.

## Process

### Step 1: Find the Narrative

**Read all sources first.** If the brief has a `## Sources` section, read every local file path before analyzing the narrative. For research reports, focus on synthesis and key findings. For raw files, extract quotes, data points, and concrete examples. For tutorials, pull practical patterns and real-world context. External URLs are noted for attribution only, not fetched.

Build an evidence set from the source material. The article's argument should be grounded in this substance.

Then read whatever other source material is provided. Ask:

- What's the single most interesting conclusion or take?
- What would make someone stop and think "I didn't know that" or "I disagree"?
- What's the throughline that ties the best parts together?
- Can you state the perspective in one sentence?

Write down that one-sentence perspective before proceeding. It goes in the frontmatter.

### Step 2: Structure the Article

Every section should serve the throughline:

```
1. Opening (get to the point fast, no throat-clearing)
2. The perspective stated clearly
3. Evidence and angles supporting it
4. Counterarguments acknowledged honestly
5. Implications or what this means going forward
```

Cut anything that doesn't serve the argument, no matter how interesting. Save those for separate posts.

### Step 3: Write the Draft

Follow the Writing Standards below. Focus on making the argument clearly and persuasively.

### Step 4: Create the File

Save to `content/articles/drafts/` with frontmatter:

```yaml
---
title: {Article Title}
status: draft
platform: x-article
thumbnail: pending
perspective: "{one-sentence perspective}"
sources:
  - {path or URL from brief's ## Sources}
content-id: {ID from brief, if available}
---
```

Naming: `{YYYY}-{MM}-{slug}.md`

### Step 5: Update Pipeline State

If working from a brief with a content ID:

```bash
sqlite3 content/pipeline/index.db \
  "UPDATE items SET status = 'draft', draft_file = 'content/articles/drafts/{filename}'
   WHERE id = '{ID}';"
```

### Step 6: Assess Content Tree

After writing, always assess what else this article unlocks. Develop all applicable formats now:

- **Post excerpts**: 2-3 sections that work as standalone posts to promote the article. Draft them inline.
- **Thread**: Could the article's argument be compressed into a tweetstorm?
- **Series**: Does this article fit an active series in `content/pipeline/series.md`? Flag the connection.

### Step 7: Suggest Thumbnail Concept

Suggest 1-2 image concepts for the thumbnail. Don't generate the image. Just describe the visual concept so the user can invoke `article-image` when ready.

### Step 8: Write Post Excerpts and Content Tree

At the bottom of the article file:

```markdown
---

## Content Tree

### Post Excerpts (draft these — ready for write-post)

1. {excerpt angle 1 — include the actual draft text, not just a description}
2. {excerpt angle 2}

### Thread Potential
{Yes/No — if yes, outline the thread structure}

### Series Connection
{Series name if applicable, which episode this would be}
```

## Writing Standards

These apply to every article this skill produces.

### Voice

Write like someone who knows what they're talking about and has a point of view. The reader should come away thinking this was written by a person with experience and opinions, not generated by a tool.

**Have a perspective and commit to it.** Research can be balanced. Articles should take stances. "Both approaches have merit" is boring. "Approach A is better for most teams, and here's why" is interesting. You can acknowledge nuance without being noncommittal.

**Use first person.** "I've seen this fail" hits harder than "this often fails." "We shipped this and it cut our costs 40%" is something people remember. Don't fake experience, but when you have it, use it.

**Be specific.** Vague claims are forgettable. Concrete examples, numbers, and named technologies make writing credible and memorable. Not "teams struggle with X" but "I watched three teams struggle with X last year."

**Write to be understood.** Use plain language for plain ideas and precise language for precise ideas. The goal is clarity and substance, not sounding impressive. A well-explained complex idea is better than a simple idea dressed up in complex vocabulary.

**Acknowledge uncertainty honestly.** "I'm not sure about this, but here's what I think" is more human than hedging with weasel words. Admit what you don't know directly.

### Readability

Good writing is easy to read but not dumbed down. It respects the reader's intelligence while respecting their time.

**Vary your sentence structure naturally.** Don't write every sentence the same length, but don't overthink it either. Read it aloud. If it flows when spoken, it flows on the page. If you run out of breath, the sentence is too long. If it sounds like a robot listing bullet points, it needs more flow.

**Get to the point.** Open with something worth reading. Don't warm up with a paragraph of context before saying the interesting thing. The interesting thing is the context.

**Use structure.** Headers, short paragraphs, and whitespace make long-form content scannable. Walls of text lose readers. But don't over-structure to the point where the article reads like an outline with prose filling in the gaps.

**End sections with something worth remembering.** The last line of a section is what sticks. Don't let it be a throwaway.

### Anti-AI Patterns

AI-generated text has recognizable tells. These make writing feel synthetic even when the ideas are good.

**Em-dashes.** AI overuses them constantly. This has become such a well-known tell that readers pattern-match "em-dash heavy" to "AI-generated." Use periods, commas, or "but" instead.

```
BAD:  The problem isn't technical—it's organizational.
GOOD: The problem isn't technical. It's organizational.

BAD:  We tried everything—caching, indexing, sharding—nothing worked.
GOOD: We tried caching, indexing, sharding. Nothing worked.
```

**The contrast cliche.** "It's not X, it's Y" is now a dead giveaway. Find other ways to draw contrasts.

```
BAD:  The real story isn't the agent. It's what the approach reveals.
GOOD: The agent itself matters less than what the approach reveals.
```

**Overused AI vocabulary:**

| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|-------------|
| delve | dig into, explore, examine |
| tapestry | mix, web, combination |
| landscape | space, world, field |
| unlock | enable, open up, make possible |
| unveil | show, reveal, introduce |
| foster | encourage, build, grow |
| leverage | use |
| realm | area, space, domain |
| multifaceted | complex, complicated, layered |
| underscores | shows, highlights, proves |

**Short-sentence stacking for dramatic effect.** Rapid-fire short fragments feel theatrical, not confident. Collapse them into one clean sentence instead.

```
BAD:  No cloud APIs. No per-query costs. No data leaving your machine.
GOOD: No cloud APIs, per-query costs, or data leaving your machine.

BAD:  Just good. Full stop.
GOOD: Just good.
```

The tell is three or more very short sentences in a row that each make a related point. One sentence with a list, or simply stopping after the statement lands, is almost always stronger.

**Structural tells to avoid:**

- Starting with "In today's [X]" or "In the world of [X]"
- "It's important to note that..."
- "Let's dive in" or "Let's explore"
- Lists of exactly three examples when two or four would be more natural
- Every paragraph being the same length
- Rhetorical questions that answer themselves immediately
- Throat-clearing introductions that delay the actual point

### What Makes Articles Work

**Take a stance someone might push back on.** If everyone already agrees with your perspective, the article doesn't need to exist.

**Include at least one thing the reader can't easily find elsewhere.** Original experience, a novel connection between ideas, a contrarian interpretation of public data.

**Acknowledge counterarguments honestly.** Don't strawman the other side. The best articles engage with the strongest version of the opposing view and still make their case.

**Be honest about what you don't know.** Credibility comes from admitting limits, not from pretending to have all the answers.

### Quality Gate

Before finishing:

1. State the perspective in one sentence. Do all sections support it?
2. Read the opening. Does it hook within the first two sentences?
3. Search for em-dashes. Replace most of them.
4. Search for AI vocabulary. Replace any that appear.
5. Read a random paragraph aloud. Does it sound like a person?
6. Is there at least one thing a reader couldn't find elsewhere?
7. Does the article take a stance someone might disagree with?
8. Is the writing clear and substantial without being bloated?

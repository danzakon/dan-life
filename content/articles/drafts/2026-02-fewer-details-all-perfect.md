Jack Dorsey said something recently that I can't stop thinking about. ([Original clip](https://x.com/saylordocs/status/2027459468469191065)) To have great attention to detail, you have to limit the number of details. Then make every single one perfect.

The natural instinct with any new tool is to build more with it. Vibe coding has made that instinct dangerous. The friction that used to force you to think carefully about what to build is basically gone.

So what happens? You add features because you can. A settings page, an analytics dashboard, a sharing flow, a notification system. Each one takes minutes instead of days, so there's no natural pressure to stop and ask whether any of them are actually good. The result is products that do a lot of things adequately and none of them well.

This is the defining tension of building software right now. The tools make creation trivially easy, which means the bottleneck has shifted entirely to restraint. Can you resist building the next feature long enough to make the current ones feel right?

The engineers and designers who figure this out are going to build the best products of this era. Their edge will be discipline. Taking twenty features down to five and then spending all their energy making those five feel inevitable. Refinement, testing, iteration, quality control. That work is harder than building from scratch, because it requires you to care about the details you chose to keep rather than the ones you could have added.

When creation is free, curation becomes the skill.

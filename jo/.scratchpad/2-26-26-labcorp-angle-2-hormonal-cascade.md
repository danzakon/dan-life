## Summary

This patient's labs tell a single, coherent story: **severely elevated prolactin (121 ng/mL) is the primary driver**, suppressing the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, which cascades into suppressed FSH, likely low bioavailable estrogen, compensatory elevation of SHBG, reduced free hormone activity, and a mild adrenal stress response visible as elevated DHEA. The prolactin level of 121 ng/mL — 3.6x the upper limit of normal — sits in a range that demands investigation for either a prolactin-secreting pituitary adenoma (prolactinoma) or medication-induced hyperprolactinemia. Among the medications this patient has been researching, **risperidone is the most potent prolactin elevator** (causing hyperprolactinemia in up to 91% of patients, with levels commonly reaching 50–150+ ng/mL), followed by fluoxetine (Prozac), which raises prolactin through serotonergic inhibition of dopamine. Vyvanse and marijuana have minimal direct prolactin effects. Treatment should focus on identifying and addressing the root cause of the hyperprolactinemia — the downstream abnormalities (SHBG, FSH, DHEA) are expected to self-correct once prolactin normalizes.

## Key Findings

- **Prolactin at 121 ng/mL is the linchpin** — this level is high enough to consider both prolactinoma and drug-induced causes, particularly risperidone
- **Risperidone is the single most likely pharmacological culprit** — it causes hyperprolactinemia in ~91% of patients by blocking D2 receptors in the anterior pituitary, which sits outside the blood-brain barrier
- **Fluoxetine (Prozac) is a secondary contributor** — SSRIs raise prolactin through serotonin-mediated inhibition of tuberoinfundibular dopamine neurons, though typically to more modest levels (usually <100 ng/mL)
- **SHBG at 154 nmol/L is almost certainly driven by oral contraceptive use or estrogen-related effects** — the Panzer et al. study showed OC users have SHBG levels 4x higher than never-users, and levels remain elevated for months after discontinuation
- **Prolactin paradoxically should *decrease* SHBG** — the Plymate study (1988) showed prolactin inhibits SHBG production in hepatocytes, so the elevated SHBG is more likely from OCs, low BMI, or hepatic estrogen exposure rather than the hyperprolactinemia itself
- **FSH at 2.8 is suppressed by the high prolactin** — prolactin disrupts GnRH pulsatility, leading to hypogonadotropic hypogonadism
- **DHEA elevation (734 vs 701 upper limit) with normal DHEA-S is clinically insignificant** — DHEA has a 15–30 minute half-life and shows significant diurnal and stress-related variation; DHEA-S is the stable reservoir and it's solidly normal
- **The combination of high SHBG + suppressed FSH + high prolactin creates a functional hormone deficiency state** that would produce symptoms of fatigue, low libido, mood disturbance, and potential menstrual irregularity despite "normal" total testosterone and estrogen on paper

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. What Causes Elevated SHBG in Young Women?

SHBG is a glycoprotein produced by the liver that binds testosterone (with high affinity) and estradiol, controlling how much of each hormone is biologically active. In a 24-year-old woman, the major causes of SHBG elevated to 154 nmol/L (26% above the upper limit of 122) include:

**Oral Contraceptives (Most Likely Cause)**

The synthetic ethinyl estradiol in combined oral contraceptives is the single most powerful driver of SHBG elevation in young women. According to [Briggs (1975)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010782475800199), OCs cause a 2–3 fold increase in SHBG levels. The landmark [Panzer et al. (2006)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16409223/) study at Boston University found that women on OCs had SHBG values **four times higher** than never-users (P<0.001), and — critically — women who had discontinued OCs still had SHBG levels nearly **double** those of never-users even 6+ months after stopping. As [MedPage Today reported](https://www.medpagetoday.com/obgyn/hrt/2423), the SHBG elevation from OCs "may be a long-term consequence" that doesn't fully resolve, potentially permanently altering hepatic SHBG gene expression.

**Hyperthyroidism** — Ruled out. TSH at 1.14 is solidly normal.

**Low BMI / Eating Disorders** — Underweight states reduce insulin, which is a negative regulator of SHBG production. Lower insulin = higher SHBG. This should be assessed in a 24-year-old woman, especially given the stimulant medication research (Vyvanse can suppress appetite).

**Hepatic Estrogen Exposure / Liver Disease** — Liver disease (particularly cirrhosis) raises SHBG, but this is uncommon in a 24-year-old. However, any oral estrogen passes through the liver first (first-pass metabolism), directly stimulating hepatic SHBG synthesis. This is why oral contraceptives raise SHBG but transdermal estrogen does not, as noted in the [Biomedicines review (2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12109167/).

**Medications** — Anticonvulsants, estrogen-containing medications, and some antipsychotics can raise SHBG. According to [Dr. Oracle](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/154216/what-does-an-elevated-sex-hormone-binding-globulin-shbg-level), "certain medications like oral contraceptives, estrogen therapy, anti-epileptics, and some anti-psychotics can also increase SHBG levels."

**SSRIs** — There is limited but suggestive evidence that SSRIs may modestly influence SHBG, potentially through their effects on estrogen metabolism. This is not well-established as a primary driver.

**Bottom line for this patient:** If she is on or recently stopped oral contraceptives, that alone explains the SHBG of 154. If she has never used OCs, low BMI (particularly if Vyvanse-related appetite suppression is involved) becomes the leading candidate, with medications as a contributing factor.

### 2. The Relationship Between High Prolactin and Elevated SHBG

This is where the picture gets counterintuitive. You might expect high prolactin to raise SHBG, but the evidence suggests the opposite.

**Prolactin *Inhibits* SHBG Production**

The key study here is [Plymate et al. (1988)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2842359/), which demonstrated in the HepG2 human hepatoma cell line that **both insulin and prolactin inhibit SHBG production**. This means that hyperprolactinemia should, if anything, *lower* SHBG — not raise it. This is an important finding because it tells us the elevated SHBG in this patient is **not caused by the high prolactin** but rather by an independent mechanism (most likely oral contraceptives or low BMI/nutritional status).

**The Indirect Estrogen Connection**

Where prolactin and SHBG do interact is through the HPG axis:

1. High prolactin suppresses GnRH pulsatility
2. Suppressed GnRH reduces FSH and LH secretion
3. Reduced FSH/LH leads to decreased ovarian estrogen production
4. Lower estrogen should theoretically *decrease* SHBG (since estrogen stimulates hepatic SHBG production)

This creates a paradox: if the prolactin is suppressing estrogen, SHBG should be going *down*, not up. The fact that SHBG is going up despite this suggests there's a separate, powerful stimulus for SHBG production — again pointing to exogenous estrogen (OCs) or very low insulin states as the dominant driver.

According to the [Endocrine Society's overview of hyperprolactinemia](https://www.endocrine.org/patient-engagement/endocrine-library/hyperprolactinemia), "Hyperprolactinemia can lead to menstrual disturbances, estrogen deficiency and testosterone deficiency, infertility, and breast milk production." The estrogen deficiency caused by hyperprolactinemia would normally reduce SHBG, so finding elevated SHBG alongside high prolactin suggests **two separate pathological processes** or a single medication (like OCs) that both elevates SHBG and potentially contributes to the hormonal milieu.

### 3. Consequences of Elevated SHBG at 154 nmol/L

At SHBG of 154, this patient is experiencing a **functional hormone deficiency** even if total hormone levels look normal on paper. Here's what elevated SHBG does and what symptoms a 24-year-old woman would experience:

**Mechanism of Harm**

SHBG binds sex hormones with the following affinity hierarchy: DHT > testosterone > estradiol. When SHBG is elevated, it acts as a "hormone sponge" — pulling free, bioavailable hormones out of circulation and rendering them inactive. As [SiPhox Health](https://siphoxhealth.com/articles/how-does-high-shbg-affect-my-hormones) explains: "When hormones are bound to SHBG, they become biologically inactive. Only the free, unbound hormones can enter cells and trigger the effects we associate with these hormones."

**This patient's free testosterone is already at the floor: 1.0 pg/mL.** While technically within some lab ranges, this is the low end and reflects the SHBG mopping up available testosterone.

**Expected Symptoms in a 24-Year-Old Woman:**

According to [Dr. Oracle's review of elevated SHBG and low free testosterone in women](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/242504/what-are-the-symptoms-and-treatment-options-for-women):

- **Decreased libido** — This is one of the most consistent and earliest symptoms. Free testosterone is critical for sexual desire in women.
- **Persistent fatigue and low energy** — Both free testosterone and free estrogen drive mitochondrial function, energy metabolism, and subjective vitality.
- **Mood changes** — Depression, anxiety, irritability. Free testosterone and estradiol both modulate serotonin and dopamine signaling.
- **Reduced muscle mass and strength** — Free testosterone is anabolic; binding it up reduces musculoskeletal support.
- **Decreased bone mineral density** — Long-term risk. Both estrogen and testosterone protect bone, and SHBG-mediated reduction in bioavailability accelerates loss. As noted by [Hertility Health](https://hertilityhealth.com/blog/shbg-levels-in-females), "When our SHBG levels are high, our levels of free unbound testosterone and oestrogen can become abnormally low, resulting in symptoms associated with low oestrogen and testosterone."
- **Vaginal dryness and sexual pain** — Reduced free estrogen impacts vaginal mucosal health.
- **Brain fog and diminished cognitive sharpness** — Estrogen is neuroprotective; reduced bioavailability affects cognition.
- **Potential fertility impacts** — Disrupted free hormone levels can interfere with ovulation.

As [Dr. Jolene Brighten](https://drbrighten.com/symptoms-of-high-or-low-shbg-levels/) notes: "If you've ever felt like your hormone lab results were 'normal,' yet your symptoms — fatigue, acne, low libido, mood swings — screamed otherwise, then you're not imagining things. One key piece that's often missing from the reproductive health conversation is SHBG."

**The "Normal Labs, Real Symptoms" Problem**

This is exactly the clinical trap with this patient. Total testosterone is 33 ng/dL (normal), total estrogen may look fine — but with SHBG at 154, the *bioavailable* fractions are depleted. Free testosterone at 1.0 pg/mL confirms this. Without checking free estradiol, the estrogen side of this equation is invisible on standard panels.

### 4. DHEA vs DHEA-S Discrepancy

- **DHEA Serum: 734 ng/dL** (reference: 31–701) — mildly elevated
- **DHEA-Sulfate: 326.0 ug/dL** (reference: 110.0–431.7) — solidly normal

**Why This Discrepancy Is Clinically Insignificant**

According to [Access Medical Labs](https://blog.accessmedlab.com/differences-between-dhea-and-dhea-s): "DHEA only occurs in the body in short bursts as needed before it is metabolized, so its half-life is 15–20 minutes." DHEA-S, by contrast, has a half-life of 7–10 hours and circulates at concentrations 300–500x higher than DHEA. DHEA-S is the stable reservoir; DHEA is the transient, pulsatile form.

Key factors explaining the discrepancy:

**Diurnal Variation** — DHEA follows a diurnal pattern similar to cortisol, peaking in the morning. As the [study by Oskis et al.](https://westminsterresearch.westminster.ac.uk/item/8z88z/differences-between-the-diurnal-patterns-of-salivary-cortisol-and-dhea-in-healthy-female-adolescents) showed, "cortisol and DHEA share a common secretagogue (ACTH), however, secretion of these hormones can be dissociated suggesting subtle individual regulation at the adrenal gland." A morning blood draw would capture DHEA at or near its peak, potentially pushing it just above the reference range.

**Acute Stress Response** — DHEA is acutely stress-responsive. The blood draw itself, anxiety about results, or general life stress can cause a transient DHEA spike without affecting the more stable DHEA-S pool. The [DHEA and job-related stress study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6779852/) confirmed the association between stress states and DHEA-S levels, noting that DHEA responds more rapidly to acute stressors.

**The DHEA-S Is the True Indicator of Adrenal Androgen Function** — As [Access Medical Labs](https://blog.accessmedlab.com/differences-between-dhea-and-dhea-s) notes: "DHEA-S is found in the blood at much higher levels than DHEA and persists longer, making it a better marker of long-term hormone patterns rather than short-term stress." The solidly normal DHEA-S (326 out of 431.7 upper limit) confirms there is no adrenal hyperandrogenism, adrenal tumor, or significant adrenal pathology.

**Adrenal vs Ovarian Sources** — DHEA is produced primarily by the adrenal cortex (~75%) with small contributions from the ovaries. DHEA-S is almost exclusively adrenal in origin. An isolated mild DHEA elevation with normal DHEA-S does not suggest ovarian pathology or meaningful adrenal overproduction.

**Clinical significance: This is noise, not signal.** A value of 734 vs an upper limit of 701 (a 5% overshoot) on a highly variable, pulsatile hormone with a 15–30 minute half-life is within the expected range of biological variation. No workup or concern is warranted for this finding in isolation.

**What's Causing the Prolactin of 121?**

This is the critical question. A prolactin of 121 ng/mL falls in a diagnostically ambiguous zone:

- **Drug-induced hyperprolactinemia:** Risperidone is the strongest candidate among the medications this patient has been researching. According to [Stojkovic et al. (2022)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9121093/) in Frontiers in Psychiatry: "hyperprolactinemia occurred in 91% of patients treated with risperidone." Risperidone uniquely penetrates the anterior pituitary (which sits outside the blood-brain barrier) at concentrations higher than in the rest of the brain, as noted by [El-Mallakh & Watkins (2019)](https://www.psychiatrist.com/pcc/prolactin-elevations-and-permeability-glycoprotein/): "the levels of these antipsychotics are higher in the anterior pituitary than in parts of the brain protected by the blood-brain barrier." Risperidone-induced prolactin levels commonly reach 50–150+ ng/mL, fully consistent with 121.

- **Fluoxetine (Prozac):** SSRIs raise prolactin through a serotonergic mechanism. According to [Dutta et al. (2026)](https://bjpsychiatry.org/fluoxetine-induced-galatorrhea-a-rare-case-series/) in the Bengal Journal of Psychiatry and the [PMC case series](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3840809/), fluoxetine can cause amenorrhea, hyperprolactinemia, and galactorrhea. The mechanism: serotonin (elevated by SSRIs) inhibits dopamine release from tuberoinfundibular neurons, and since dopamine is the primary inhibitor of prolactin secretion, less dopamine = more prolactin. However, SSRI-induced prolactin elevations are typically more modest — usually <100 ng/mL.

- **Prolactinoma (pituitary adenoma):** The [Pituitary Society Consensus Statement (2023)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-023-00886-5) notes that prolactin levels generally correlate with tumor size. Microprolactinomas (<10mm) typically produce levels of 50–200 ng/mL, which is exactly where 121 falls. The [diagnostic evaluation guidelines](https://www.ovid.com/journals/nendo/pdf/10.1159/000499694~pitfalls-in-the-diagnostic-evaluation-of-hyperprolactinemia) recommend MRI of the pituitary when drug causes are excluded.

- **Vyvanse (lisdexamfetamine):** Amphetamines increase dopamine, which should *suppress* prolactin, not raise it. The [Strajhar et al. (2018)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30381248/) study examined lisdexamfetamine's effects on plasma steroid concentrations and found HPA axis stimulation (cortisol, DHEA elevation) but no prolactin-raising effect. Vyvanse is not a contributor to the hyperprolactinemia.

- **Marijuana (Cannabis/THC):** The evidence is mixed. The [scoping review by Ayoub et al. (2025)](https://www.researchsquare.com/article/rs-7403198/latest) found "In animals, THC exposure often suppressed prolactin, especially during key reproductive stages. Human studies were inconsistent." The [Ranganathan et al. (2008)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2863108/) study on cannabinoids and prolactin in humans showed variable results. Cannabis is unlikely to be the cause of a prolactin of 121.

**Likelihood ranking for the prolactin elevation:**
1. **Risperidone** — overwhelmingly the most likely medication cause (~91% incidence, levels commonly 50–150+)
2. **Prolactinoma** — must be excluded with pituitary MRI if the patient is not on risperidone
3. **Fluoxetine** — possible contributor, especially in combination with risperidone
4. **Cannabis** — unlikely primary cause
5. **Vyvanse** — would lower, not raise prolactin

**The FSH Suppression Confirms the Cascade**

FSH at 2.8 mIU/mL (below the follicular range of 3.5–12.5) is the downstream proof that hyperprolactinemia is disrupting the HPG axis. As the [StatPearls review of hyperprolactinemia](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537331/) explains, excess prolactin suppresses GnRH pulsatile secretion from the hypothalamus, which in turn reduces both FSH and LH. LH at 5.8 is still in the normal follicular range, but FSH is already below it — suggesting the axis is being suppressed but hasn't fully shut down yet. This is consistent with "partial" hypogonadotropic hypogonadism.

### 6. What to Do About Elevated SHBG

The treatment approach depends entirely on addressing the underlying cause(s). SHBG is a downstream marker — you don't treat SHBG, you treat what's driving it.

**Step 1: Identify and Treat the Root Cause of Hyperprolactinemia**

- If the patient is on **risperidone**: Discuss with prescribing psychiatrist about switching to a prolactin-sparing antipsychotic (aripiprazole, quetiapine, or clozapine). Per [Flint et al. (2002)](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ajp.159.1.133), baseline prolactin in patients on olanzapine or clozapine averaged 9 ng/mL vs 27 ng/mL for risperidone.
- If the patient is on **fluoxetine**: Consider whether the SSRI is necessary, or whether switching to a less serotonergic antidepressant (bupropion, which is dopaminergic) might help reduce prolactin.
- If **no medication** explains the level: Pituitary MRI to rule out prolactinoma. If found, dopamine agonist therapy (cabergoline) is first-line treatment.
- Normalizing prolactin will restore GnRH pulsatility → normalize FSH → restore ovarian estrogen production.

**Step 2: Address the SHBG Elevation Independently**

- **If on oral contraceptives**: Discuss discontinuation or switching to a non-oral hormonal method (IUD, patch, ring) to reduce first-pass hepatic estrogen exposure. Per [Panzer et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16409223/), SHBG may take months to normalize after OC discontinuation but should eventually come down.
- **If low BMI / underweight**: Improving caloric intake and reaching a healthy weight increases insulin signaling, which downregulates hepatic SHBG production. According to the [Simó et al. (2015) review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1043276015000831) in Trends in Endocrinology & Metabolism, hepatic SHBG production is regulated by inflammatory cytokines, monosaccharides, and fatty acids — not simply by insulin as previously thought.

**Step 3: Lifestyle and Dietary Modifications**

According to [Dr. Jolene Brighten](https://drbrighten.com/shbg-hormone-levels/) and [Pippa Campbell Health](https://www.pippacampbellhealth.com/blog/how-to-lower-elevated-shbg-levels/):

- **Adequate protein intake** — Protein supports liver function and can modulate SHBG. Ensuring sufficient dietary protein, especially from whole food sources.
- **Healthy fats** — Adequate dietary fat intake supports steroid hormone synthesis. Very low-fat diets can exacerbate SHBG elevation.
- **Strength training** — Resistance exercise has been shown to improve androgen levels and may help modulate SHBG. It also supports bone density (important given the long-term risk from elevated SHBG).
- **Stress management** — Chronic stress drives the HPA axis, affecting the entire endocrine cascade. Reducing stress may lower DHEA spikes and improve overall hormonal balance.
- **Optimize liver health** — SHBG is synthesized in the liver. Supporting liver function through minimizing alcohol, processed food, and hepatotoxins.
- **Adequate sleep** — Sleep deprivation disrupts the HPG axis and can worsen the hormonal cascade.

**Step 4: Specific Supplements (With Caveats)**

Some practitioners recommend:
- **Magnesium** — May support hormone metabolism
- **Zinc** — Has some evidence for modestly reducing SHBG
- **Vitamin D** — Deficiency is associated with altered SHBG; optimization may help
- **Boron** — Some limited evidence suggests it can reduce SHBG

These are adjunctive at best. The root cause treatment (addressing prolactin and OCs if applicable) will have 10–100x the impact of any supplement.

**What NOT to Do**

- Don't try to "treat" the DHEA finding — it's not clinically significant
- Don't focus on SHBG supplementation or exotic protocols before addressing the prolactin
- Don't ignore the prolactin level — 121 ng/mL is not a "slightly elevated" finding, it's a significant abnormality that demands diagnosis

**Expected Timeline for Improvement**

If the prolactin source is identified and treated:
- Prolactin normalization: 2–6 weeks (medication change) or 2–4 weeks (cabergoline for prolactinoma)
- FSH recovery: 4–12 weeks after prolactin normalizes
- SHBG normalization: 3–6 months (faster if OCs are also discontinued)
- Symptom improvement: Most patients notice improvement in energy and mood within 4–8 weeks of prolactin normalization; libido may take longer

# Risperidone: Neurological and Brain Effects

A deep dive into the therapeutic and adverse neurological consequences of risperidone, with emphasis on what the clinical literature actually shows.

---

## 1. Tardive Dyskinesia (TD)

### What It Is

Tardive dyskinesia is a medication-induced hyperkinetic movement disorder — involuntary, repetitive movements primarily affecting the face, tongue, jaw, and extremities. It is associated with chronic exposure to dopamine receptor-blocking agents, including all antipsychotics.

### Incidence Rates: Risperidone Specifically

- **The headline stat:** 1 in 5 patients (20%) on second-generation antipsychotics (SGAs) for a prolonged period develop TD. This figure comes from pharmacoepidemiologic studies and is cited in the *American Journal of Psychiatry* and *Psychiatric News* (Goldberg, 2021).
  - Source: https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.pn.2021.3.10

- **Correll et al. (2004)** conducted a landmark systematic review of 1-year studies: the annualized TD incidence for SGAs was approximately **3.9%** per year, compared to **5.5%** for first-generation antipsychotics (FGAs). While lower, the risk is far from eliminated.
  - Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/5402354_Lower_Risk_for_Tardive_Dyskinesia_Associated_With_Second-Generation_Antipsychotics_A_Systematic_Review_of_1-Year_Studies

- **Carbon et al. (2018) meta-analysis** in *World Psychiatry* pooled data from comparative RCTs: SGAs had a significantly lower TD risk than FGAs, but risk was never zero. The meta-analysis noted significant heterogeneity across individual SGAs.
  - Source: https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6127753/

- **Carbon et al. (2017) prevalence meta-analysis:** TD prevalence was approximately **20.7%** for patients currently receiving SGAs (who may also have prior FGA exposure) vs. **30.0%** for those on FGAs. For patients with only SGA exposure, prevalence was lower but still substantial.
  - Source: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Tardive-Dyskinesia-Prevalence-in-the-Period-of-Use%3A-Carbon-Hsieh/faaf921b87523037fb7fbfce37aaf1b067a3c536

- **Woerner et al. (2011)** — a 2-year prospective study of antipsychotic-naïve elderly patients on risperidone or olanzapine. Even in this vulnerable, naïve population, TD emerged, confirming that SGAs do carry meaningful TD risk.
  - Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21508932/

### Risperidone vs. Other SGAs

- **Stegmayer, Walther & van Harten (2018)** reviewed TD mechanisms with atypical antipsychotics. Risperidone is considered to have a **higher EPS/TD risk** among SGAs, likely due to its relatively potent D2 receptor blockade compared to agents like quetiapine, clozapine, or olanzapine. Among SGAs, the risk hierarchy generally runs: **clozapine (lowest) < quetiapine < olanzapine < aripiprazole ≤ risperidone < ziprasidone** (though exact rankings vary by study).
  - Source: https://cris.maastrichtuniversity.nl/ws/files/78507833/Van_Harten_2018_Tardive_dyskinesia_associated_with_atypical.pdf

### Risk Factors

- **Age** — strongest risk factor; elderly patients are 5-6x more vulnerable
- **Female sex** — especially post-menopausal women
- **Duration of exposure** — cumulative risk increases over years
- **Higher doses** — dose-dependent risk
- **Prior FGA use** — any prior exposure increases baseline vulnerability
- **Diabetes and metabolic syndrome** — comorbid risk factors
- **Substance use disorders** — especially alcohol
- **Affective disorders** — mood disorder patients may be more susceptible
- Source: https://www.uptodate.com/contents/tardive-dyskinesia-etiology-risk-factors-clinical-features-and-diagnosis/print

### Irreversibility

TD is often **partially or fully irreversible**, particularly after prolonged exposure. Early detection and discontinuation improve chances of remission, but many patients have persistent symptoms even years after drug cessation. Younger patients and those with shorter exposure durations have better odds of remission. In the elderly, TD is frequently permanent.

### Time to Onset

- Typically emerges after **months to years** of continuous antipsychotic use (minimum ~3 months, per DSM-5 criteria, or 1 month in patients >60 years)
- Can appear after dose reduction or discontinuation ("withdrawal dyskinesia"), which may unmask an underlying TD
- The "1-in-5" cumulative figure represents risk accrued over **years** of continuous SGA treatment

## 2. Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)

### Overview

EPS encompasses four major categories of movement disorders from antipsychotics:
1. **Acute dystonia** — sustained muscle contractions (hours to days after starting)
2. **Akathisia** — subjective restlessness and inability to sit still (days to weeks)
3. **Drug-induced parkinsonism** — tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (weeks to months)
4. **Tardive dyskinesia** — involuntary movements (months to years; covered above)

Source: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK534115/

### Risperidone and Dose-Dependence: The D2 Occupancy Threshold

The critical insight linking dose to EPS is the **D2 receptor occupancy threshold**:

- **65% D2 occupancy** → threshold for clinical efficacy
- **72% D2 occupancy** → threshold for hyperprolactinemia
- **78-80% D2 occupancy** → threshold for EPS onset

Source: Kapur et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2000 — https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ajp.157.4.514

**PET data for risperidone specifically** (Nyberg et al., 1999, *American Journal of Psychiatry*):
- At **6 mg/day**: mean D2 occupancy was **82%** (range 79-85%). Six of seven patients developed EPS.
- At **3 mg/day**: mean D2 occupancy was **72%** (range 53-78%). Three of seven had EPS.
- **Conclusion:** The commonly prescribed 6 mg/day dose produces D2 occupancy above the EPS threshold in most patients. A dose of 2-4 mg/day may be the minimal effective dose for many patients.

Source: https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/ajp.156.6.869

### Siafis et al. (2023) — Dose-Response Meta-Analysis

A landmark systematic review in *Molecular Psychiatry* examined dose-D2 occupancy-EPS relationships across antipsychotics:
- Confirmed a monotonic or hyperbolic dose-response for EPS with most antipsychotics, including risperidone
- Higher doses carry greater or equal EPS risk — there is no "safe high dose"
- For **akathisia specifically** (Wu et al., 2023), antipsychotics differ significantly in propensity, and risk increases with dose for most agents

Sources:
- https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-023-02203-y
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0924977X23000664

### How Risperidone Compares to Other SGAs for EPS Risk

**Cochrane Review (Komossa et al., 2011)** — risperidone vs. other atypical antipsychotics for schizophrenia:
- Risperidone produces **more EPS than clozapine, olanzapine, quetiapine, and ziprasidone**
- Risperidone produces **roughly comparable EPS to aripiprazole**
- Risperidone EPS risk is **still lower than most FGAs** (especially haloperidol)

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4167865/

**EPS risk hierarchy among SGAs (general consensus):**
- **Lowest:** clozapine, quetiapine
- **Low-moderate:** olanzapine, aripiprazole (though akathisia is common with aripiprazole)
- **Moderate-high:** risperidone, paliperidone, ziprasidone
- **Highest SGA:** risperidone at doses >4 mg/day approaches FGA-like EPS rates

Source: https://www.bcchildrens.ca/sites/g/files/qpdaav156/files/2025-01/treatment-recommendations-extrapyramidal-side-effects-sga-use-children-youth.pdf

### Case Series Evidence

Thomson et al. (2017), *Annals of Neurosciences*, documented a case series of EPS related to risperidone treatment — dystonia, parkinsonism, and akathisia were all represented, even at moderate doses. Notably, some cases involved patients without prior antipsychotic exposure, underscoring that risperidone's D2 affinity creates meaningful EPS liability.

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5566679/

## 3. Dopamine Supersensitivity

### The Core Mechanism

When antipsychotics chronically block D2 receptors, the brain compensates through:

1. **Upregulation of D2 receptor density** — more receptors are expressed on postsynaptic neurons
2. **Increased D2 receptor affinity** — existing receptors shift to a "D2-High" (high-affinity) state
3. **Increased presynaptic dopamine synthesis and release**

This compensatory process is called **dopamine supersensitivity**. The brain is essentially "fighting back" against the chemical blockade by amplifying its dopamine signaling capacity.

Source: Seeman, 2005, *PNAS* — https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0409766102

### Supersensitivity Psychosis

**Dopamine supersensitivity psychosis (DSP)** is a clinical phenomenon where:
- Psychotic symptoms emerge or worsen **despite stable or increasing antipsychotic doses**
- "Rebound psychosis" occurs when antipsychotics are reduced or withdrawn — symptoms that are **more severe than the original illness**
- Patients become **treatment-refractory** over time

Clinical characteristics of DSP (Iyo et al., 2017):
- Psychotic relapse during maintenance treatment
- Tolerance to antipsychotic effects (requiring dose escalation)
- Co-occurrence with tardive dyskinesia (both may reflect D2 supersensitivity)
- Worsening long-term prognosis

Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0269881117728428

### The Paradox: Antipsychotics Potentially Worsening the Underlying Condition

This is perhaps the most disturbing finding in the antipsychotic literature:

**Yin et al. (2017), *Current Neuropharmacology*** — comprehensive review:
- Chronic antipsychotic use can produce neuroadaptations that reduce therapeutic efficacy over time
- Relapse following antipsychotic discontinuation may be partly iatrogenic — caused by the supersensitized dopamine system, not just the re-emergence of the underlying illness
- Some cases of "treatment-resistant schizophrenia" may actually be antipsychotic-induced supersensitivity psychosis

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5327459/

**Servonnet & Bhatt Samaha (2020), *Neuropharmacology*** — animal model review:
- Long-term antipsychotic treatment evokes dopamine supersensitivity in a substantial proportion of subjects
- This supersensitivity is linked to tolerance to antipsychotic effects and can worsen psychosis
- D2-related changes are observed but are imperfect markers — the full molecular cascade is still being mapped
- **Key finding:** Adequate dosing and intermittent treatment kinetics may help **prevent** dopamine supersensitivity development

Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0028390819301571

**Kruyer et al. (2021), *Molecular Psychiatry*** — mechanistic study:
- Identified that D2-medium spiny neuron (D2-MSN) hyperactivity in the nucleus accumbens drives antipsychotic-induced behavioral supersensitivity
- Provides a cellular-level mechanism for why chronic antipsychotic use can paradoxically increase sensitivity to dopamine

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-021-01235-6

### The Clinical Implication

The dopamine supersensitivity literature raises a fundamental question: **Are antipsychotics creating a chronic dependency by altering the very neurobiology they treat?** The evidence suggests that for a subset of patients, chronic D2 blockade makes the brain more vulnerable to dopaminergic perturbation than it was before treatment began. This argues strongly for:
- Using the **lowest effective dose**
- Considering carefully before long-term maintenance in patients where the benefit-risk ratio is marginal
- **Very gradual taper** if discontinuation is attempted (to avoid supersensitivity rebound)

## 4. Brain Structural Changes

### The Andreasen Studies: The Landmark Controversy

**Nancy Andreasen et al.** at the University of Iowa conducted the largest longitudinal brain imaging study of schizophrenia, tracking patients over 7-14+ years with serial MRI scans.

Key publications:
- **Andreasen et al. (2011)**, *Archives of General Psychiatry*: In 211 patients with first-episode schizophrenia followed longitudinally, higher antipsychotic treatment intensity was associated with smaller brain tissue volumes — including gray matter, white matter, and total brain volume. This association persisted after controlling for illness severity, substance use, and other confounders.
  - Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21300943/

- **Andreasen et al. (2013)**, *American Journal of Psychiatry*: Extended follow-up confirmed that **relapse duration** and **antipsychotic treatment intensity** were both independently associated with brain tissue loss. More medication → more volume loss. More time in relapse → more volume loss. A devastating double bind.
  - Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23558429/

Andreasen's own words (New York Times interview, 2008):
> "People with schizophrenia are losing brain tissue at a more rapid rate than healthy people of comparable age. Some are losing as much as 1 percent per year. And then we're trying to figure out why. Another thing we've discovered is that the more drugs you've been given, the more brain tissue you lose."
>
> "What exactly do these drugs do? They block basal ganglia activity. The prefrontal cortex doesn't get the input it needs and is being shut down by drugs."

Source: https://mindfreedom.org/kb/andreasen-antipsychotic-brain-atrophy/

University of Iowa summary: https://psychiatry.medicine.uiowa.edu/news/2016/08/largest-longitudinal-brain-imaging-study-date-carries-critical-implications-treatment

### The Chopra Placebo-Controlled Study: The Gold Standard

**Chopra et al. (2021), *Neuropsychopharmacology*** — the most methodologically rigorous study to date:

- **Design:** Randomized, triple-blind, placebo-controlled MRI study
- **Subjects:** 62 antipsychotic-naïve first-episode psychosis patients randomized to risperidone/paliperidone OR placebo + intensive psychosocial therapy. Plus 27 healthy controls.
- **MRI:** Baseline, 3 months, and 12 months

**Findings at 3 months:**
- Patients receiving antipsychotic medication showed significant **reductions in frontal, temporal, and parietal cortical thickness** compared to placebo
- The placebo group (illness only, no medication) did **not** show these same reductions
- This provides the strongest evidence to date that **antipsychotic medication itself — not the disease — drives at least some of the observed brain volume loss**

**Findings at 12 months:**
- By 12 months, most placebo patients had been switched to antipsychotics (as clinically needed), making group comparisons less clean
- Nevertheless, the early medication-group reductions persisted

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41386-021-00980-0 / https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8209146/

### Cortical Thinning Mechanisms

**Hietala et al. (2025), *Translational Psychiatry***:
- Antipsychotic-related cortical thinning maps onto regions rich in serotonin and dopamine receptors
- The pattern of thinning is associated with neurotransmitter systems — most notably the serotonin system — not just D2
- Findings replicated using ENIGMA consortium data (N ≥ 2,168 patients)
- Suggests the thinning is pharmacologically driven, related to receptor-rich regions being most affected

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-025-03336-0

### Risperidone-Specific MRI Findings

**Zong et al. (2023), *Frontiers in Psychiatry***:
- After just **8 weeks** of risperidone monotherapy in first-episode, drug-naïve schizophrenia patients, significant cortical thickness changes were detected
- These structural changes correlated with transcriptomic and epigenetic changes — suggesting deep biological remodeling in a very short time

Source: https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2023.1127353/pdf

**Li et al. (2025), *Frontiers in Psychiatry*** — MRI review of risperidone's effects:
- Risperidone alters both brain structure and functional connectivity
- Both therapeutic (improved functional connectivity in psychosis-related circuits) and adverse (gray matter reductions) effects are observed
- Advances in MRI are revealing that risperidone's brain effects are more complex and widespread than previously appreciated

Source: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1590886/full

### The Controversial Question: Disease or Drug?

**The answer is: both, but in different ways.**

| Factor | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| Disease contribution | Brain changes observed in antipsychotic-naïve patients; progressive loss correlates with relapse duration |
| Drug contribution | Chopra et al. placebo-controlled trial shows medication-specific volume loss; Andreasen longitudinal data shows dose-response; Hietala shows receptor-distribution-linked thinning |
| Interaction | Relapse itself causes damage, but the medication used to prevent relapse also causes structural changes — a genuine therapeutic dilemma |

**Emsley et al. (2023), *Therapeutic Advances in Psychopharmacology***, specifically asked whether treatment could contribute to brain changes:
- Reviewed evidence for cortical thickness and volume reductions, basal ganglia changes, and white matter changes
- Concluded that time spent in psychotic relapse is harmful, but treatment intensity is independently associated with tissue loss
- Called for further research into lowest effective doses and treatment intermittency

Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/20451253231195258

## 5. Cognitive Effects

### The Central Paradox

Risperidone's cognitive effects present a paradox: some evidence suggests modest **short-term cognitive improvement** (particularly vs. untreated psychosis or vs. FGAs), while other evidence reveals **adverse cognitive effects**, especially on working memory.

### Adverse Effects on Working Memory

**Reilly et al. (2006), *JAMA Psychiatry***:
- Found **adverse effects of risperidone on spatial working memory** in first-episode schizophrenia patients
- This is not a subtle finding — working memory is among the most functionally important cognitive domains, affecting daily life, work capability, and decision-making
- The deficit was specifically linked to risperidone treatment, not just the illness

Source: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/668233

### The Placebo-Controlled Cognitive Data

**Allott et al. (2023), *Translational Psychiatry*** — secondary analysis of the Chopra placebo-controlled trial:
- Antipsychotic-naïve first-episode psychosis patients were randomized to risperidone/paliperidone or placebo + psychosocial therapy
- At 6 months, both groups showed similar cognitive trajectories
- Antipsychotic medication **did not improve cognition** relative to placebo
- This challenges the notion that antipsychotics provide cognitive enhancement — the improvement seen in open-label studies may be attributable to practice effects, regression to the mean, or resolution of acute psychosis, rather than direct cognitive benefit

Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02501-7

### Systematic Review Evidence

**Meta-analysis in *Journal of Advanced Trends in Medical Research* (2024):**
- Reviewed efficacy of risperidone for cognitive enhancement across psychiatric disorders
- Found evidence for **modest improvements in some cognitive domains** (attention, verbal memory) relative to FGAs
- But effects were **inconsistent across studies** and **small in magnitude**
- No evidence that risperidone normalizes cognition to healthy control levels

Source: https://journals.lww.com/atmr/fulltext/2024/04000/efficacy_of_risperidone_in_enhancing_cognitive.10.aspx

**Houthoofd et al. (2008), *Clinical Therapeutics*** — review of double-blind RCTs:
- Risperidone showed mixed effects across cognitive domains
- Improvements in some areas (attention, verbal fluency) but not others
- Processing speed effects were variable
- Sedation is a significant confound in cognitive testing

Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S014929180800307X

### Sedation vs. Cognitive Enhancement

Risperidone causes dose-dependent sedation through:
- H1 histamine receptor antagonism
- Alpha-1 adrenergic antagonism
- Partial serotonin blockade

Sedation directly impairs:
- Processing speed
- Sustained attention
- Reaction time
- Executive function

The question of whether risperidone produces "net" cognitive benefit requires disentangling:
1. Relief from psychotic disorganization (cognitive benefit from treating illness)
2. Direct pharmacological cognitive enhancement (minimal evidence)
3. Sedation and D2-mediated cognitive impairment (cognitive cost of treatment)

### Long-Term Cognitive Trajectory

The long-term picture is concerning:
- Brain volume loss (see Section 4) is associated with cognitive decline
- Dopamine supersensitivity (see Section 3) may impair prefrontal cortex function
- The Andreasen data suggests that prefrontal cortex — critical for executive function — is "shut down" by chronic antipsychotic-induced basal ganglia suppression
- No long-term study has demonstrated sustained cognitive enhancement from risperidone over years of treatment

## 6. Reward System Dampening

### The Mesolimbic Dopamine System

The mesolimbic pathway — projecting from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens (ventral striatum) — is the brain's core reward and motivation circuit. Dopamine release in this pathway signals:
- Reward prediction
- Motivational salience
- Incentive to pursue goals
- Hedonic pleasure (partially)

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2238694/

All antipsychotics block D2 receptors. The mesolimbic pathway is D2-rich. Therefore, **all antipsychotics dampen reward signaling to some degree** — this is an inherent pharmacological consequence, not an off-target side effect.

### fMRI Evidence: Reward System Suppression

**Juckel et al. (2006, 2008, 2016), *Psychopharmacology* and *Dialogues in Clinical Neuroscience***:

Using the Monetary Incentive Delay (MID) task during fMRI:
- **Healthy controls:** robust bilateral ventral striatum activation during reward anticipation
- **Schizophrenia patients on FGAs:** virtually **no** ventral striatum activation — the reward system was functionally silenced
- **Schizophrenia patients on SGAs (including risperidone):** partial activation observed — significantly better than FGAs but still reduced vs. controls
- **Switch studies:** Switching from FGAs to SGAs (including risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole) **reactivated** ventral striatal signaling, suggesting the suppression was drug-induced, not disease-inherent

Key finding: D2 receptor blockade causes **clinical depression in 20-40% of patients** taking FGAs. SGAs partially mitigate this via less complete D2 blockade and serotonin interactions, but do not eliminate it.

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4826766/

### Neuroleptic Dysphoria

**Voruganti & Awad (2004), *Psychopharmacology***:
- "Neuroleptic dysphoria" is a distinct, under-recognized syndrome: unpleasant subjective changes in arousal, mood, thinking, and motivation induced by antipsychotics
- Manifests early in treatment as **drug aversiveness** — patients dislike how the medication makes them feel
- When persistent, leads to: non-adherence, substance abuse, poor outcomes, increased suicidality, compromised quality of life
- Direct neuroimaging evidence confirms that dopamine blockade in reward circuits underlies the phenomenon
- SGAs produce dysphoria less than FGAs but do not eliminate it

Source: https://biopsychiatry.com/neuroleptics.htm

**Awad & Voruganti (2005), *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica***:
- Neuroleptic dysphoria was recognized from the very first days of chlorpromazine use in the 1950s
- Took decades to receive adequate research attention
- Directly linked to dopamine pathway interference

Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/7862661_Neuroleptic_dysphoria_Revisiting_the_concept_50_years_later

### Antipsychotics and Amotivation: The Nuanced Picture

**Fervaha et al. (2015), *Neuropsychopharmacology*** — the largest study on this topic (N=520, CATIE trial data):
- Examined whether antipsychotic dose predicted motivational deficits across five medications (olanzapine, perphenazine, quetiapine, risperidone, ziprasidone)
- **Finding:** Antipsychotic dose was **not** significantly related to level of amotivation for any medication examined
- Sedation severity was also not significantly related to amotivation
- 121 antipsychotic-free patients showed **no change in motivation** after 6 months of antipsychotic treatment
- **Conclusion:** "Chronic treatment with antipsychotics does not necessarily impede or enhance goal-directed motivation in patients with schizophrenia"

**However**, the authors note important caveats:
- The CATIE population was chronically treated (average 15 years of illness) — baseline motivational deficits may already be maximal
- The study measured **general motivation**, not hedonic response or anticipatory pleasure specifically
- SGAs cause less reward dampening than FGAs, and the CATIE study primarily used SGAs
- Amotivation from the illness itself is severe and may mask drug-related effects

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4397414/

### Anhedonia and Emotional Blunting

The distinction between illness-related and drug-related anhedonia is critical:

- **Schizophrenia itself** produces profound anhedonia, particularly anticipatory anhedonia (inability to look forward to pleasurable activities), which is a core negative symptom predating any medication use
- **Antipsychotic D2 blockade** adds a layer of pharmacological reward dampening on top of illness-related deficits
- The net result for many patients is a **compounded motivational deficit** where illness and treatment both suppress the reward system

Source: https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4759314/

### Clinical Impact

The reward dampening effects of antipsychotics (including risperidone) have real-world consequences:
- **Reduced drive for social engagement** — contributing to social isolation
- **Diminished capacity for pleasure** — making life feel flat and unrewarding
- **Impaired motivation for work, self-care, and recovery activities**
- **Treatment non-adherence** — patients stop medications because they feel subjectively worse
- **Substance use** — patients may self-medicate with substances that activate reward circuits (alcohol, cannabis, stimulants)

## Summary: The Neurological Cost-Benefit Framework

| Domain | Short-Term (weeks-months) | Long-Term (years) |
|--------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **Psychosis control** | Strong efficacy; D2 blockade reduces positive symptoms | Diminishing returns; supersensitivity may develop |
| **Movement disorders** | Dose-dependent EPS; usually manageable | 20% cumulative TD risk; potentially irreversible |
| **Brain structure** | Cortical thinning detectable within 8 weeks | Progressive volume loss proportional to dose/duration |
| **Cognition** | Mixed — some improvement from psychosis resolution, some impairment | No demonstrated long-term enhancement; possible decline |
| **Reward/motivation** | Partial suppression of reward circuitry; dysphoria common | Compounded with illness-related anhedonia; quality of life impact |
| **Dopamine system** | Therapeutic D2 blockade | D2 upregulation, supersensitivity, potential treatment resistance |

### The Fundamental Tension

The evidence reveals an uncomfortable truth: risperidone (and antipsychotics as a class) are genuinely effective for acute psychosis, but carry neurological costs that accumulate with duration and dose. The brain is not a passive recipient of pharmacological blockade — it adapts, compensates, and is structurally altered by chronic treatment.

The clinical implication is not to avoid antipsychotics when needed, but to:
1. **Use the lowest effective dose** (risperidone 2-4 mg/day rather than 6 mg/day)
2. **Monitor continuously** for EPS, TD, cognitive changes, and motivational deficits
3. **Consider the long-term plan** — indefinite high-dose maintenance carries escalating neurological risk
4. **Taper gradually** if discontinuation is appropriate — never abrupt withdrawal
5. **Weigh alternatives** — is the risk profile of the specific antipsychotic appropriate for this specific patient?

# Hyperprolactinemia Deep Dive: Prolactin at 121 ng/mL

**Category:** Medical Research
**Date Started:** 2-26-26
**Status:** [x] Complete

---

## Summary

A prolactin level of 121 ng/mL in a 24-year-old woman sits in a diagnostically ambiguous zone — too high to dismiss as a trivial medication side effect, but below the ~200 ng/mL threshold that would virtually confirm a prolactinoma. The most likely culprits are risperidone (an antipsychotic notorious for producing the highest prolactin elevations of any psychiatric medication, routinely exceeding 100 ng/mL in women) or a microprolactinoma (<10 mm pituitary adenoma). Fluoxetine, Vyvanse, and marijuana are unlikely to produce elevations of this magnitude on their own. The suppressed FSH at 2.8 mIU/mL confirms active disruption of the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis via prolactin's inhibition of GnRH pulsatility, with real downstream consequences for menstrual regularity, bone density, and fertility. An MRI of the sella turcica and macroprolactin screening are the essential next steps. Treatment depends on the cause: if risperidone-induced, medication switching is first-line; if a prolactinoma, cabergoline is the gold-standard dopamine agonist with >90% efficacy.

## Key Findings

- **Prolactin at 121 ng/mL falls in the overlap zone** between drug-induced hyperprolactinemia (typically <100 ng/mL but risperidone can push well above this) and microprolactinoma (typically 50–200 ng/mL). Levels >200 ng/mL are virtually diagnostic of prolactinoma.
- **Risperidone is the #1 suspect** among this patient's medications — it is the most potent prolactin-elevating antipsychotic, with women on standard doses commonly reaching 80–140+ ng/mL. It causes sustained, dose-dependent hyperprolactinemia because it crosses the blood-brain barrier poorly and saturates pituitary D2 receptors.
- **Fluoxetine produces only mild prolactin elevations** (typically <50 ng/mL), usually via serotonin-mediated stimulation of lactotrophs. It could be additive but is not the primary driver at 121 ng/mL.
- **Vyvanse (lisdexamfetamine) would be expected to *lower* prolactin**, not raise it — amphetamines enhance dopaminergic transmission, and dopamine is prolactin's primary inhibitor. However, d-amphetamine is a weak prolactin suppressor in clinical practice.
- **Cannabis/marijuana has inconsistent effects on prolactin** — acute THC may transiently suppress prolactin via dopaminergic activity, while chronic use and withdrawal may mildly elevate it. It is not a significant driver of hyperprolactinemia at this level.
- **The suppressed FSH (2.8 mIU/mL)** is a direct mechanistic consequence of hyperprolactinemia disrupting GnRH pulsatility via inhibition of kisspeptin neurons. This confirms active HPG axis suppression.
- **Sustained hyperprolactinemia at this level causes** bone density loss (via hypogonadal estrogen deficiency), menstrual irregularities/amenorrhea, anovulatory infertility, possible galactorrhea, and mood/cognitive effects.
- **MRI of the sella turcica is mandatory** — even if medications are the likely cause, imaging is needed to rule out a coexisting pituitary adenoma, especially at levels >100 ng/mL.
- **Cabergoline is superior to bromocriptine** for prolactinoma treatment — better efficacy, fewer side effects, twice-weekly dosing, and faster normalization.

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. Differential Diagnosis: What Causes Prolactin at 121 ng/mL?

The magnitude of prolactin elevation is the single most useful clue for distinguishing etiology. The clinical literature consistently uses the following thresholds:

| Prolactin Level | Most Likely Etiology |
|---|---|
| 25–100 ng/mL | Drug-induced, stalk effect, idiopathic, macroprolactinemia, mild microprolactinoma |
| 100–200 ng/mL | **Overlap zone**: microprolactinoma OR high-potency drug (risperidone/paliperidone) |
| >200 ng/mL | Virtually diagnostic of prolactinoma (specificity approaches 100%) |
| >1,000 ng/mL | Macroprolactinoma |

A level of 121 ng/mL is **not** in the "clean" diagnostic territory. According to a large cohort study of 770 patients by [Vilar et al. (2024)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11460971/), prolactin levels correlate with tumor size in prolactinomas, but significant overlap exists with drug-induced causes in the 100–200 ng/mL range. The [Pituitary Society Consensus Statement (2023)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-023-00886-5) notes that prolactin >250 ng/mL is highly suggestive of prolactinoma, while levels >200 ng/mL have very high specificity for this diagnosis.

At 121 ng/mL, the two leading diagnostic possibilities are:

**A. Microprolactinoma (<10 mm pituitary adenoma)**
- Microprolactinomas typically produce prolactin levels of 50–200 ng/mL, though some may be higher ([Pituitary Network Association](https://pituitary.org/disorders/prolactinomas/))
- Mean prolactin in surgically confirmed prolactinomas is ~858 ng/mL, but this is skewed by macroadenomas ([Dr. Oracle / Clinical Guidelines](https://www.droracle.ai/articles/523972/what-are-typical-prolactin-levels-in-patients-with-prolactinoma))
- Prolactinomas are the most common pituitary tumor (30–40% of all pituitary adenomas) and disproportionately affect women of reproductive age
- A 24-year-old woman is squarely in the highest-risk demographic

**B. Risperidone-Induced Hyperprolactinemia**
- Risperidone is the most prolactin-raising antipsychotic on the market — a fact confirmed by multiple meta-analyses and the [J&J/Risperdal prescribing information](https://www.jnjmedicalconnect.com/products/risperdal/medical-content/adverse-event-of-risperdal-elevated-prolactin-levels-in-adults), which explicitly states "RISPERDAL is associated with higher levels of PRL elevation than other antipsychotic agents"
- Women are more susceptible to risperidone-induced prolactin elevation than men. According to [Stojkovic et al. (2022)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9121093/), risperidone-induced prolactin levels in women commonly range from 40 to 140+ ng/mL, and can exceed 200 ng/mL at higher doses
- The mechanism is unique: risperidone has very high D2 receptor binding affinity but limited blood-brain barrier penetration relative to its pituitary penetration, meaning it saturates pituitary D2 receptors (which lack a blood-brain barrier) more aggressively than central receptors. This produces sustained, non-transient prolactin elevation unlike most other atypical antipsychotics ([Flint, Am J Psychiatry, 2002](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ajp.159.1.133))
- A [dose-response meta-analysis (Lin et al., CNS Drugs 2025)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40263-025-01218-z) confirmed that risperidone's prolactin-elevating effect is dose-dependent and among the highest of all antipsychotics studied
- **Critical point**: The traditional teaching that "drug-induced hyperprolactinemia usually stays <100 ng/mL" has a major exception for risperidone and paliperidone, which routinely exceed this threshold

**C. Fluoxetine (Prozac) — Unlikely Primary Cause**
- SSRIs raise prolactin through serotonin (5-HT) pathways — serotonin stimulates prolactin release from pituitary lactotrophs via 5-HT2A and 5-HT2C receptors
- However, the elevation is typically **mild** (usually <50 ng/mL). A case report by [Peterson (Mayo Clin Proc, 2001)](https://www.e-lactancia.org/media/papers/FluoxetinaBF-MayoClinPro2001.pdf) described fluoxetine-associated galactorrhea with a prolactin of only 37.4 ng/mL
- [Papakostas et al. (J Clin Psychiatry, 2006)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/6936526_Serum_Prolactin_Levels_Among_Outpatients_With_Major_Depressive_Disorder_During_the_Acute_Phase_of_Treatment_With_Fluoxetine) studied 87 patients on fluoxetine and found modest prolactin increases, with relatively few exceeding the hyperprolactinemia threshold
- A [case series by Mondal et al. (Ther Adv Psychopharmacol, 2013)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3840809/) documented fluoxetine-induced amenorrhea and galactorrhea but noted the mechanism involves both direct serotonergic stimulation and possible inhibition of tuberoinfundibular dopaminergic neurons
- **Bottom line**: Fluoxetine could contribute additively (especially combined with risperidone), but alone it would not produce a level of 121 ng/mL

**D. Vyvanse (Lisdexamfetamine) — Would Be Expected to *Lower* Prolactin**
- Amphetamines are dopamine-releasing agents and DAT inhibitors. Since dopamine is the primary tonic inhibitor of prolactin secretion, stimulants would theoretically suppress prolactin
- However, [a classic study on d-amphetamine](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6642414/) found it is actually a "poor PRL suppressor" — at 15 mg IV, it induced only a modest 20% inhibition of prolactin in normoprolactinemic women, and was completely ineffective in women with suspected prolactinomas
- This may be because amphetamine primarily releases dopamine from non-granular neuronal pools rather than the tuberoinfundibular tract that controls pituitary function
- **Bottom line**: Vyvanse is neither raising prolactin nor meaningfully counteracting the elevation. It is essentially neutral in this context.

**E. Marijuana/Cannabis — Minor Player**
- The effects of THC on prolactin are inconsistent and context-dependent. [Ranganathan et al. (Psychopharmacology, 2008)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2863108/) hypothesized THC would decrease prolactin (via dopaminergic activity) but found inconsistent results in controlled IV THC administration studies
- Animal data generally shows THC suppresses prolactin, but human data is mixed ([Scoping Review, 2025](https://sciety-discovery.elifesciences.org/articles/by?article_doi=10.21203/rs.3.rs-7403198/v1))
- Chronic cannabis use does not appear to significantly alter basal prolactin levels ([Vescovi et al., Drug Alcohol Depend, 1992](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/037687169290036C))
- Cannabis withdrawal may mildly elevate prolactin ([Markianos & Stefanis, 1982](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0376871682900503))
- **Bottom line**: Marijuana is not a significant contributor to prolactin at 121 ng/mL

**Diagnostic Summary**: If this patient is currently taking risperidone, that is the most likely cause, possibly with a small additive contribution from fluoxetine. If she is **not** currently taking risperidone (i.e., it was only previously researched/considered), then a microprolactinoma becomes the leading diagnosis. The MRI is essential regardless.

### 2. Consequences of Sustained Hyperprolactinemia

Chronically elevated prolactin at 121 ng/mL — roughly 3.6x the upper limit of normal — produces a cascade of clinical consequences, primarily through hypogonadism caused by HPG axis suppression.

**A. Menstrual Irregularities and Amenorrhea**
- Hyperprolactinemia is one of the most common causes of secondary amenorrhea in premenopausal women. In a retrospective study cited by [UpToDate](https://www.uptodate.com/contents/clinical-manifestations-and-evaluation-of-hyperprolactinemia), the most commonly reported symptoms in women aged 30–44 with hyperprolactinemia were infertility (48%), headache (39%), and oligomenorrhea (29%)
- The degree of menstrual disruption correlates with prolactin level. Mild elevations may cause luteal phase defects and oligomenorrhea; levels above ~60–100 ng/mL commonly cause frank amenorrhea ([Clue/Boutot, 2018](https://helloclue.com/articles/cycle-a-z/hyperprolactinemia-and-the-menstrual-cycle))
- At 121 ng/mL, complete anovulation and amenorrhea are very likely

**B. Infertility**
- Anovulation from hyperprolactinemia-induced GnRH suppression directly causes infertility. According to [Hossa et al. (J Exp Clin Med, 2025)](https://dergipark.org.tr/en/download/article-file/4217053), hyperprolactinemia is an "essential contributory cause" of infertility, affecting approximately 15% of infertile couples
- Even subclinical hyperprolactinemia can impair luteal function and implantation
- The good news: fertility typically normalizes rapidly once prolactin is corrected — this is one of the most treatable causes of female infertility

**C. Bone Density Loss (Osteopenia/Osteoporosis)**
- This is arguably the most serious long-term consequence in a 24-year-old. Hyperprolactinemia → hypogonadism → estrogen deficiency → accelerated bone resorption
- According to [Yun et al. (Int J Mol Sci, 2024)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10855748/), hyperprolactinemia-induced bone loss occurs through two mechanisms: (1) indirect loss via estrogen deficiency from HPG axis suppression, and (2) direct effects of prolactin on osteoblasts and osteoclasts
- At age 24, this patient has not yet reached peak bone mass (typically achieved by age 25–30). Prolonged hyperprolactinemia during this critical window could permanently compromise skeletal health
- The [UK NHS guidance (TEWV, 2025)](https://www.tewv.nhs.uk/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/Guidance-on-managing-the-effects-of-psychotropic-drugs-on-prolactin.pdf) specifically warns to "avoid drugs known to cause hyperprolactinaemia in patients <25 years (who have not attained peak bone mass)"
- A DEXA scan should be considered for bone density assessment

**D. Galactorrhea**
- Milky breast discharge unrelated to pregnancy/nursing occurs in a minority of hyperprolactinemic patients — estimates range from 30–80% depending on the series
- [Columbia Doctors](https://www.columbiadoctors.org/treatments-conditions/hyperprolactinemia-prolaction-disorder) notes that "the majority will not experience this symptom" despite elevated prolactin
- When present, it is distressing but not dangerous and resolves with prolactin normalization

**E. Mood, Cognition, and Sexual Function**
- Hyperprolactinemia is independently associated with reduced libido, anorgasmia, and sexual dysfunction in both sexes
- The relationship with mood is bidirectional: hyperprolactinemia worsens depression and anxiety (partially through hypogonadism), while the psychiatric conditions requiring medications that cause hyperprolactinemia also affect mood
- Some evidence suggests prolactin itself has direct CNS effects on mood regulation beyond its gonadal hormone effects

**F. Cardiovascular and Metabolic Effects**
- According to [Glezer et al. (Front Endocrinol, 2023)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.1018090/full), there is emerging evidence linking hyperprolactinemia to increased cardiovascular disease risk, potentially through metabolic syndrome, altered lipid profiles, and chronic inflammation
- [Kirsch et al. (Front Endocrinol, 2022)](https://public-pages-files-2025.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.1002320/pdf) reviewed prolactin's metabolic effects and noted that elevated prolactin levels are "widely associated with worsening metabolic disease," including effects on adipose tissue, pancreatic function, and insulin sensitivity
- However, the cardiovascular risk data is still evolving and somewhat inconsistent. The most established risks remain those mediated through hypogonadism and estrogen deficiency

### 3. Mechanism: How Hyperprolactinemia Suppresses FSH and Disrupts the HPG Axis

The pathway from elevated prolactin to suppressed FSH (2.8 mIU/mL in this patient) is well-characterized and operates through the following cascade:

**Step 1: Prolactin Inhibits Kisspeptin Neurons**
- Kisspeptin neurons in the arcuate nucleus (ARC) and anteroventral periventricular nucleus (AVPV) of the hypothalamus are the master regulators of GnRH secretion
- These kisspeptin neurons express prolactin receptors. When prolactin binds, it directly inhibits kisspeptin gene expression and peptide release ([Donato & Frazão, Arch Endocrinol Metab, 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10522168/))
- Kisspeptin neurons in the ARC co-express neurokinin B (NKB) and dynorphin — these are called KNDy neurons and serve as the "GnRH pulse generator" ([Xie et al., Front Endocrinol, 2022](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.925206/full))
- A landmark study by [Hoskova et al. (J Clin Endocrinol Metab, 2022)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9282259/) demonstrated that exogenous kisspeptin administration can overcome GnRH neuronal suppression caused by hyperprolactinemia in humans, confirming that kisspeptin inhibition is the key mediator

**Step 2: Kisspeptin Suppression → Reduced GnRH Pulsatility**
- GnRH neurons in the hypothalamus do not express prolactin receptors directly — prolactin's effect on GnRH is mediated through kisspeptin
- Normal GnRH secretion requires pulsatile release at specific frequencies: approximately every 60–90 minutes during the follicular phase
- When kisspeptin input is reduced, GnRH pulse frequency and amplitude decrease
- This is functionally equivalent to the mechanism behind lactational amenorrhea — the same pathway that prevents ovulation during breastfeeding

**Step 3: Reduced GnRH → Suppressed Gonadotropins (FSH and LH)**
- Decreased GnRH stimulation of the anterior pituitary gonadotrophs results in reduced FSH and LH secretion
- In this patient, FSH is clearly suppressed at 2.8 mIU/mL (below the follicular phase reference of 3.5–12.5 mIU/mL)
- LH at 5.8 mIU/mL appears within normal range but may still be functionally suppressed (LH is more pulsatile and a single measurement is less reliable)
- The GnRH pulse frequency matters: slower frequencies favor FSH secretion while faster frequencies favor LH. Hyperprolactinemia tends to reduce overall pulsatility, which can differentially suppress FSH

**Step 4: Suppressed FSH/LH → Ovarian Dysfunction**
- Inadequate FSH prevents proper follicular recruitment and maturation in the ovaries
- Without a dominant follicle, estradiol production drops — leading to a hypoestrogenic state
- The absent LH surge means ovulation cannot occur → anovulatory infertility
- The reduced estrogen production feeds back into the bone density and cardiovascular consequences described above

**Why the Normal TSH Matters**
- TSH at 1.14 uIU/mL effectively rules out primary hypothyroidism, which is an important differential because hypothyroidism causes hyperprolactinemia through TRH-mediated stimulation of lactotrophs
- This narrows the differential to medications vs. prolactinoma vs. other rare causes

### 4. Recommended Diagnostic Workup

Based on the [Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guidelines (Melmed et al., JCEM 2011)](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/96/2/273/2709487), the [Pituitary Society Consensus Statement (Petersenn et al., Nat Rev Endocrinol 2023)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-023-00886-5), and the recent [CMAJ workup algorithm (Mann et al., 2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12007944/):

**A. Confirm the Elevation — Repeat Prolactin**
- A single elevated prolactin should be confirmed with a repeat fasting morning draw (at least 1 hour after waking/eating)
- Stress, exercise, breast stimulation, and recent meals can transiently elevate prolactin
- If the patient was stressed during the blood draw, this could have contributed (though unlikely to push levels to 121 ng/mL from stress alone)

**B. Rule Out Macroprolactinemia**
- Macroprolactin is a biologically inactive complex of prolactin bound to IgG antibodies. It is detected by standard immunoassays and can account for up to 40% of unexplained hyperprolactinemia cases ([Vilar, Neuroendocrinology, 2019](https://virtual.fasen.org.ar/docs/1.-Pitfalls-in-the-diagnostic-evaluation-of-hyperprolactinemia.-Neurocndocrinology-2019.pdf))
- Macroprolactin screening (via polyethylene glycol (PEG) precipitation) should be requested
- If macroprolactin predominates, the patient may not actually have true hyperprolactinemia, and further workup may be unnecessary

**C. Medication Review — Critical Step**
- Determine which of the researched medications the patient is **actually taking** vs. merely investigating
- If currently on risperidone: this is the most likely cause. The guideline recommendation is to either:
  1. Discontinue or switch the offending medication for 3 days (if psychiatrically safe) and recheck prolactin, OR
  2. If switching is not safe, proceed directly to MRI to rule out a coexisting adenoma
- If NOT on risperidone: the suspicion for prolactinoma rises substantially

**D. MRI of the Sella Turcica (Pituitary MRI with Gadolinium)**
- This is **mandatory** at a prolactin level of 121 ng/mL
- Gadolinium-enhanced MRI with dedicated thin-cut (2–3 mm) sequences through the sella is the gold standard for detecting pituitary microadenomas
- Even if medication-induced hyperprolactinemia is suspected, MRI is recommended at levels >100 ng/mL to exclude a coexisting adenoma (per [Endocrine Society guidelines](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/96/2/273/2709487))
- If a macroadenoma (>10 mm) is found with prolactin of "only" 121 ng/mL, consider the "hook effect" — request serial dilutions to ensure the assay isn't falsely reporting a low value (this is rare but clinically important)

**E. Additional Labs to Request**
- **Estradiol**: To quantify the degree of hypogonadism — likely to be low given the suppressed FSH
- **Pregnancy test (β-hCG)**: Always rule out pregnancy in a woman of reproductive age with hyperprolactinemia
- **Comprehensive metabolic panel / renal function**: Chronic kidney disease can cause hyperprolactinemia
- **IGF-1**: If MRI shows a pituitary mass, co-secretion of growth hormone should be evaluated
- **Visual field testing**: Only if MRI reveals a macroadenoma approaching or compressing the optic chiasm
- **DEXA scan**: Consider given the patient's age (<25, not yet at peak bone mass) and likely duration of hyperprolactinemia

### 5. Treatment Options

Treatment depends entirely on the etiology and whether the patient is symptomatic.

**A. If Risperidone-Induced: Medication Switching**

The first-line approach for drug-induced hyperprolactinemia is discontinuing or switching the offending medication, in collaboration with the prescribing psychiatrist:

- **Switch to a prolactin-sparing antipsychotic**: Aripiprazole (a partial D2 agonist that can actually *lower* prolactin), quetiapine, or clozapine are the preferred alternatives ([Gupta et al., BJPsych Advances, 2018](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/bjpsych-advances/article/management-of-antipsychoticinduced-hyperprolactinaemia/6495A80D8850C4233ACDF8F4EDEC1A0C))
- **Adjunctive aripiprazole**: Adding low-dose aripiprazole (5–10 mg) to risperidone can significantly reduce prolactin levels through its partial D2 agonist action, without requiring a full medication switch
- **Timeline**: Prolactin typically normalizes within days to weeks after risperidone discontinuation ([Current Psychiatry, 2018](https://cdn.mdedge.com/files/s3fs-public/Document/February-2018/CP01703042.PDF))
- **Caution**: Never abruptly discontinue an antipsychotic — titrate down and cross-taper with psychiatric supervision

**B. If Prolactinoma: Dopamine Agonist Therapy**

Dopamine agonists are the first-line treatment for prolactinomas of all sizes, per every major guideline:

**Cabergoline (Dostinex) — Preferred Agent**
- Cabergoline is a long-acting ergot-derived D2 agonist, dosed 0.25–1.0 mg twice weekly (can be escalated to 3.5 mg/week in resistant cases)
- Superior to bromocriptine in every major comparison: a [systematic review by dos Santos Nunes et al.](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK85990/) found cabergoline achieves prolactin normalization in ~83–92% of patients vs. ~48–78% for bromocriptine
- A [large meta-analysis by Wang et al. (Systematic Reviews, 2012)](https://systematicreviewsjournal.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/2046-4053-1-33) of 8 RCTs and 178 non-randomized studies (>3,000 patients) confirmed dopamine agonists significantly reduce prolactin levels compared to no treatment (weighted mean difference: -45 ng/mL)
- Tumor shrinkage: Cabergoline shrinks prolactinomas in ~80% of patients with macroadenomas and normalizes prolactin in >90% of microprolactinomas ([Endotext - Prolactinoma Management, Drummond et al. 2025](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK279174/))
- Timeline for normalization:
  - Prolactin levels begin dropping within **days** of starting cabergoline
  - Most patients achieve normalization within **2–4 weeks** at standard doses
  - Tumor shrinkage is detectable on MRI within **3–6 months**
- Side effects: nausea, dizziness, headache, orthostatic hypotension (generally mild and dose-dependent; rare cardiac valve concerns at high doses used for Parkinson's disease are not typically relevant at prolactinoma doses)

**Bromocriptine (Parlodel) — Second-Line**
- Shorter-acting, requires daily dosing (2.5–15 mg/day in divided doses)
- More gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, vomiting, nasal congestion)
- Still effective: normalizes prolactin in ~60–80% of microprolactinomas
- Main advantage: longer safety track record in pregnancy (historical preference for women planning conception, though cabergoline data in pregnancy is increasingly reassuring)

**C. If Both Medication and Adenoma Are Contributing**
- This scenario is more common than typically appreciated
- Approach: Switch/stop the offending medication first, recheck prolactin after washout (3+ days for risperidone), and proceed to MRI
- If prolactin normalizes after medication change → drug-induced was the sole cause
- If prolactin remains elevated → likely adenoma requiring dopamine agonist therapy
- If prolactin drops substantially but doesn't normalize → possible dual etiology

**D. Surgical and Radiation Options**
- Transsphenoidal surgery: Reserved for dopamine agonist-resistant prolactinomas, intolerance to medical therapy, or patient preference. Success rates: ~70–90% for microprolactinomas
- Stereotactic radiosurgery: Third-line for aggressive/resistant tumors
- Neither is likely needed for this patient given the moderate prolactin elevation

**E. Monitoring During Treatment**
- Prolactin levels: Recheck 1 month after starting treatment, then every 3–6 months
- MRI: Repeat at 12 months for microprolactinoma on dopamine agonist; earlier if symptoms worsen
- Menstrual cycle: Return of regular menses is a clinical marker of treatment response
- Bone density: DEXA scan at baseline and follow-up if prolonged hypogonadism was present

## Interpretation for This Patient

The clinical picture assembles as follows:

1. **If on risperidone**: This is the most likely cause. Prolactin at 121 ng/mL is well within the expected range for risperidone in young women. The FSH suppression is a predictable downstream consequence. The priority is a psychiatric medication review — can risperidone be switched to a prolactin-sparing alternative (aripiprazole, quetiapine)? Even so, an MRI is warranted given the level is >100 ng/mL.

2. **If NOT on risperidone**: Fluoxetine alone is insufficient to explain this level. The differential shifts heavily toward microprolactinoma. MRI is urgent, and cabergoline is the likely treatment.

3. **Regardless of cause**: The suppressed FSH confirms active HPG axis disruption. At age 24 — before peak bone mass — prolonged hypoestrogenism from anovulation is actively degrading skeletal health. There is urgency to resolve this, not just for fertility but for lifelong bone health.

4. **The normal TSH eliminates hypothyroidism** as a contributing factor, and the normal testosterone argues against PCOS overlap.

# Prozac: Discontinuation, PSSD, and Persistent Effects

**Category:** Research Sub-report
**Date:** 2-26-26
**Status:** [x] Complete

---

## Summary

Fluoxetine (Prozac) occupies a unique position among SSRIs regarding discontinuation: its exceptionally long half-life (4–6 days for the parent compound, 4–16 days for its active metabolite norfluoxetine) creates a built-in self-taper that makes acute withdrawal far milder than shorter-acting SSRIs like paroxetine or venlafaxine. However, this pharmacokinetic advantage does not eliminate all persistent post-treatment effects. Emerging evidence documents Post-SSRI Sexual Dysfunction (PSSD)—recognized by the EMA in 2019—as a condition where sexual side effects persist indefinitely after cessation, potentially mediated by epigenetic changes and receptor desensitization. Emotional blunting and anhedonia, while typically resolving, may persist in some patients through oppositional tolerance mechanisms. The landmark ASCP deprescribing consensus published February 25, 2026 in JAMA Network Open now provides the first structured clinical framework for when and how to reassess and taper psychotropic medications. Meanwhile, animal studies demonstrate that fluoxetine induces lasting epigenetic modifications—including chromatin remodeling and altered myelination gene expression—that outlast treatment by weeks. The clinical challenge remains balancing these risks against strong evidence that continued antidepressant treatment halves relapse rates in recurrent depression.

## Key Findings

- **Fluoxetine has the mildest discontinuation profile among SSRIs** due to its 4–16 day effective half-life acting as a natural self-taper; it is even used as a bridging agent to help patients discontinue shorter-acting SSRIs
- **~31% of patients experience at least one discontinuation symptom** when stopping antidepressants (vs 17% on placebo), but severe symptoms are relatively rare (~3%)
- **PSSD is now formally recognized** by the EMA (2019) and acknowledged in FDA labeling; it can begin after even brief SSRI exposure and persist indefinitely
- **Emotional blunting affects 40–60% of patients on SSRIs**, with some evidence of persistence post-discontinuation via oppositional tolerance mechanisms
- **The ASCP 45-member task force** (Feb 25, 2026, JAMA Network Open) achieved consensus on 44/50 Delphi statements on deprescribing, emphasizing periodic review, shared decision-making, and distinguishing withdrawal from relapse
- **Slow tapering + psychological support** prevents relapse as effectively as continued medication in many patients (Lancet Psychiatry, Dec 2025 meta-analysis of 17,000+ adults)
- **Fluoxetine causes lasting epigenetic changes** in animal models—chromatin remodeling across 27 brain regions, altered myelination gene expression persisting 40+ days post-treatment, and CaMKIIα promoter modifications in the nucleus accumbens
- **Continued antidepressant treatment reduces relapse by ~50–60%** compared to discontinuation, and some patients with recurrent/severe depression genuinely benefit from indefinite treatment

## 1. Discontinuation Syndrome

### Fluoxetine vs Other SSRIs

Fluoxetine is consistently identified as the SSRI with the **lowest risk of discontinuation syndrome**. This is entirely attributable to pharmacokinetics:

| Drug | Half-life | Discontinuation Risk |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| Paroxetine | 21 hours | Highest among SSRIs |
| Venlafaxine | 5 hours | Very high |
| Sertraline | 26 hours | Moderate |
| Citalopram | 35 hours | Moderate |
| Fluoxetine | 4–6 days (norfluoxetine: 4–16 days) | Lowest |

The active metabolite norfluoxetine effectively creates a **self-taper over weeks**, gradually reducing serotonin reuptake inhibition after the last dose. This is so well-established that fluoxetine is used as a **bridging/substitution strategy** for patients struggling to discontinue shorter-acting SSRIs—a technique described in a 2025 paper by Shapiro in the *Journal of Psychiatry & Neuroscience* as a "technical approach for deprescribing antidepressants."

### Incidence Data

A landmark 2024 *Lancet Psychiatry* meta-analysis (Henssler et al.) pooling 44 RCTs and 35 observational studies found:
- **31% of patients** stopping antidepressants experience at least one discontinuation symptom (vs 17% on placebo)
- The **drug-attributable incidence** is approximately 14 percentage points (8 pp in RCTs with placebo arms)
- **Severe symptoms** occur in roughly **2.8%** of patients
- Desvenlafaxine, venlafaxine, and paroxetine had the highest rates; fluoxetine had among the lowest

A 2025 JAMA Psychiatry systematic review and meta-analysis confirmed these findings and further characterized symptom profiles.

### Common Withdrawal Symptoms

- **Neurological:** "Brain zaps" (electric shock sensations), dizziness, light-headedness, paresthesias, tinnitus
- **Gastrointestinal:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- **Psychological:** Anxiety, irritability, agitation, insomnia, vivid dreams, depersonalization
- **General:** Fatigue, flu-like symptoms, tremor, diaphoresis

For fluoxetine specifically, these are typically **mild and self-limiting** (days to a few weeks), though some patients report protracted courses lasting months.

### "Withdrawal" vs "Discontinuation Syndrome"—The Naming Debate

The term "discontinuation syndrome" was promoted by pharmaceutical companies and psychiatry organizations starting in the 1990s to **differentiate SSRI cessation effects from benzodiazepine/opioid "withdrawal"** and avoid stigma. Critics (notably Fava, Cosci, Moncrieff, and Healy) argue this is:

1. **Pharmaceutical wordplay** designed to minimize the severity and clinical significance of the phenomenon
2. **Clinically misleading**—the underlying mechanism (neuroadaptation to chronic serotonergic stimulation, followed by abrupt removal) is pharmacologically identical to withdrawal from other psychoactive substances
3. **Harmful to patients**—the euphemistic framing led to decades of undertreating and dismissing withdrawal symptoms

The 2024 Henssler meta-analysis and the 2019 Cosci & Fava commentary in *J Clin Psychiatry* explicitly frame these as **withdrawal syndromes**, not merely "discontinuation" effects. The *Lancet* editorial (May 2025, "50 years of SSRIs") now states: "It is now widely accepted that SSRIs can cause a protracted withdrawal syndrome."

A February 25, 2026 article published by *Psychiatric Times*—summarized by CCHR—acknowledged that "SSRI antidepressant withdrawal is more severe and often misdiagnosed than previously acknowledged," and flagged **serious methodological flaws in traditional relapse-prevention trials** that inflate perceptions of benefit while obscuring evidence of physiological dependence.

### The DAWSS Scale (2024)

Moncrieff, Read, and Horowitz (2024, *J Affect Disord Reports*) surveyed 1,148 respondents across 20 peer-led online groups and proposed the **Discriminatory Antidepressant Withdrawal Symptoms Scale (DAWSS)**, assessing 31 commonly reported symptoms. The study found that many symptoms—particularly brain zaps, akathisia, and severe emotional instability—are highly specific to withdrawal and can help clinicians **distinguish withdrawal from relapse**, a critical unmet need.

### Withdrawal in Children and Adolescents

A December 2025 systematic review (Wang & Cosci, *Therapeutic Advances in Psychopharmacology*) found acute and persistent withdrawal syndromes in pediatric populations as well, though data remain limited.

## 2. Post-SSRI Sexual Dysfunction (PSSD)

### Definition and Recognition

PSSD is an **iatrogenic condition** where sexual dysfunction persists **indefinitely** after discontinuation of serotonin reuptake inhibitors. Core symptoms include:

- **Genital anesthesia** (numbness of genitals)
- **Decreased or absent libido**
- **Erectile dysfunction / impaired arousal**
- **Anorgasmia or muted orgasm**
- **Ejaculatory dysfunction**
- **Pleasureless orgasm**
- **Loss of nocturnal erections**

### Regulatory Recognition

- **2019: European Medicines Agency (EMA)** — Conducted a formal EudraVigilance analysis (EMA/132684/2019, dated February 20, 2019) reviewing adverse event reports for SSRIs, SNRIs, and other antidepressants specifically regarding persistent sexual dysfunction. Issued a formal recommendation that product information include warnings that sexual dysfunction can persist after treatment cessation—a landmark regulatory acknowledgment.
- **FDA labeling** — Updated to include language acknowledging persistent sexual side effects post-discontinuation for certain SSRIs. The current fluoxetine label (last major update 2017) includes sexual dysfunction warnings, though the PSSD-specific language varies by formulation.
- **MedDRA coding** — PSSD has a designated code in the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities, enabling systematic pharmacovigilance tracking
- The condition is now referenced in pharmacovigilance databases and by patient advocacy organizations globally

### Prevalence

True prevalence is **unknown and contested**:
- A 2024 paper by David Healy (*Epidemiology and Psychiatric Sciences*) detailed the **barriers to quantifying incidence and prevalence**, including: underreporting, clinician dismissal, lack of prospective studies, and the difficulty of distinguishing PSSD from depression-related sexual dysfunction
- A 2025 preprint (Linke et al.) conducted a large online survey characterizing demographics, risk factors, and symptom profiles of PSSD sufferers, finding the condition is "underdiagnosed and poorly understood"
- Estimates range widely from **<1% to potentially 5–10%** of SSRI users experiencing some degree of persistent sexual changes, but methodological limitations make firm numbers impossible
- Ben-Sheetrit et al. (2023, *Annals of General Psychiatry*) attempted a formal risk estimation, concluding the risk is non-trivial but acknowledging that prospective controlled studies are still lacking
- The RxISK database has collected thousands of patient reports

### Csoka and the Epigenetic Hypothesis

Professor **Antonei Csoka** (Howard University) has been central to PSSD research:
- Published early **case reports** documenting persistent sexual dysfunction after SSRI cessation
- Proposed the **epigenetic hypothesis**: SSRIs may cause lasting modifications to gene expression (DNA methylation, histone modification) that persist after the drug is metabolized
- The PSSD Network announced a 2025 research collaboration between Csoka and Professor Ashley Monks (University of Toronto) investigating underlying mechanisms, alongside continued support for Professor Roberto Melcangi's work (University of Milan)

### Proposed Mechanisms

1. **Epigenetic modifications** — SSRIs alter DNA methylation and histone acetylation patterns in reward and sexual function circuitry. These changes can persist independently of continued drug exposure. Csoka's lab at Howard University focuses specifically on this mechanism.

2. **5-HT1A receptor desensitization** — Chronic serotonin elevation may downregulate or desensitize 5-HT1A receptors, which normally facilitate sexual function. If desensitization becomes persistent, it could explain ongoing dysfunction.

3. **Dopaminergic pathway suppression** — Serotonin-mediated inhibition of dopamine in mesolimbic reward circuitry may not fully reverse, contributing to anhedonia and reduced sexual motivation.

4. **Peripheral nerve changes** — A 2025 case study (Stachelek, *PubMed*) provided insights into "the peripheral nature of persistent sexual dysfunction," suggesting that peripheral nerve or receptor changes (not just central) may contribute to genital anesthesia.

5. **Neurosteroid alterations** — Melcangi's research at University of Milan explores gut microbiome and neurosteroid changes as contributing factors.

6. **Hormonal disruption** — Persistent changes to prolactin, testosterone, or other hormonal axes may play a role, though evidence is mixed.

### Clinical Case Descriptions and Reviews

A 2024 case study in *Current Issues in Personality Psychology* (Gargul, University of Gdansk) documented the psychological and sexual functioning of PSSD sufferers, finding:
- Profound impact on quality of life, relationships, and identity
- Feelings of grief, loss, and anger at medical dismissal
- Comorbid anxiety and depression secondary to the condition itself

Klaas et al. (2023, *Biomedicine & Pharmacotherapy*) published a detailed PSSD pathophysiology case study that mapped the progression of symptoms from 2000 through 2021, distinguishing three types of SSRI-associated sexual dysfunction (on-treatment, withdrawal-onset, and persistent) and examining HPG axis functioning and value proposition design for treatment approaches.

Aftab (2024, *The Carlat Psychiatry Report*) provided a balanced clinical review of PSSD, noting that while "reports of PSSD date to the 1990s, it has received recognition from regulatory agencies only recently" and that patient groups have gathered to support the concept while some medical organizations have endorsed it, though it remains controversial in parts of psychiatry.

## 3. Emotional/Cognitive Persistence After Discontinuation

### Emotional Blunting on SSRIs

Emotional blunting (AIEB) is well-documented during SSRI treatment:
- Affects **40–60% of patients** on SSRIs/SNRIs (Psychopharmacology Institute, 2025)
- Patients describe feeling "numb," detached, unable to cry, or unable to feel joy
- **2023 University of Cambridge study** (published in *Neuropsychopharmacology*) demonstrated that escitalopram reduced reinforcement learning sensitivity—a concrete cognitive mechanism for the "blunting" patients describe
- Involves reduced activation in prefrontal cortex, amygdala, and reward circuits (particularly ventral striatum)

### Evidence for Persistence After Stopping

**The oppositional tolerance model** (Andrews et al., 2011; elaborated in *Pharmacopsychiatry*, 2021) provides a theoretical framework:

1. The brain adapts to chronic serotonin elevation by downregulating receptors and reducing endogenous serotonin production
2. When the SSRI is removed, the brain is in a **hyposerotonergic state** relative to its new set point
3. This can manifest as **rebound depression, anxiety, emotional instability, and cognitive dysfunction** that may be misinterpreted as relapse
4. In some individuals, the neural adaptations may not fully reverse, particularly after prolonged treatment

**Neurochemical evidence for rebound effects:**
A 2024 study in *Neuropsychopharmacology* (Sharp lab) directly measured 5-HT neurochemistry after paroxetine discontinuation in mice and found:
- Brain tissue levels of 5-HT and its metabolite 5-HIAA **increased above control levels** after discontinuation, notably in the hippocampus
- Depolarization-evoked 5-HT release was **reduced during continuous SSRI** but **increased above controls post-discontinuation**—a direct rebound overshoot
- This provides the first mechanistic demonstration that SSRI discontinuation triggers a **rebound activation of serotonin neurons** that may underlie withdrawal symptoms and mood instability

**Fluoxetine's multifaceted cognitive effects:**
Ampuero et al. (2024, *Frontiers in Pharmacology*) published a comprehensive review of fluoxetine's effects on cognition, noting that fluoxetine induces **bidirectional cognitive changes**—improving some domains (fear extinction, cognitive flexibility) while impairing others (memory consolidation, emotional processing)—depending on treatment duration, dose, and developmental stage.

**Evidence for persistent cognitive effects:**
- The oppositional tolerance model predicts that "potential cognitive deficits caused by the discontinuation of antidepressant drugs" could occur, as detailed in the 2021 PMC review
- Some patients report persistent anhedonia, emotional flatness, or cognitive fog months after successful tapering
- However, **controlled longitudinal data are limited**—it is difficult to disentangle residual drug effects from underlying depression, post-withdrawal syndrome, and nocebo effects

**Evidence against persistence:**
- Most patients report emotional range returning within weeks to months of discontinuation
- Formal cognitive testing generally shows normalization after drug clearance
- The Cambridge study showed blunting effects were tied to active drug presence, suggesting reversibility

### The Rebound Problem

The critical clinical issue is that **withdrawal-induced mood instability is frequently misinterpreted as depressive relapse**, leading to reinstatement of medication and potentially indefinite use. This is discussed extensively in the deprescribing literature (see Section 4).

## 4. Deprescribing Challenges

### The ASCP Consensus Statement (February 25, 2026)

**Just published yesterday** in *JAMA Network Open*, this is a landmark:

- **45-member international task force** convened by the American Society of Clinical Psychopharmacology (ASCP)
- Led by Joseph F. Goldberg, MD, MSc (Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai)
- Used a **structured Delphi process** with focused literature review (January–May 2025)
- Evaluated 50 statements; achieved **consensus (≥75% agreement) on 44 of 50**

**Key consensus points:**
1. Components of pharmacotherapy should undergo **periodic review** to ensure treatments target relevant symptoms with favorable risk-benefit ratios
2. Deprescribing should **not occur before assessing medication adherence**
3. Must involve **active patient participation within shared decision-making**
4. Prescribing, renewing, and deprescribing should be viewed as a **"fluid" process** with active oversight throughout
5. Clinicians must have greater recognition of **treatment endpoints**
6. The consensus is described as "an important milestone in psychopharmacology" reflecting "a growing understanding that the prescribing and deprescribing of psychotropic medications deserve more thoughtfulness and rigor"

### The Lancet Psychiatry Meta-Analysis (December 2025)

A major meta-analysis involving **17,000+ adults** found:
- **Slow tapering + psychological support** prevents depression relapse to a similar extent as remaining on antidepressants
- Slow tapering with therapy could **prevent one relapse in every five individuals** compared to abrupt stopping or fast tapering
- Continuation of antidepressants at standard dose reduces relapse risk by **up to 60%** compared to abrupt discontinuation
- However, therapy combined with gradual taper achieves **comparable protection**

### Why Patients Stay on SSRIs Too Long

Multiple factors contribute to excessive treatment duration:

1. **Relapse fear** — Both clinicians and patients fear discontinuation will trigger relapse, leading to indefinite continuation even when the original episode has fully resolved
2. **Withdrawal misinterpreted as relapse** — This is perhaps the most pernicious driver. When patients attempt to stop and experience withdrawal symptoms (anxiety, mood instability, insomnia), these are frequently interpreted by clinicians as "the depression coming back," leading to reinstatement
3. **Lack of deprescribing guidelines** — Until the ASCP consensus (Feb 2026), there was no structured guidance for when and how to discontinue
4. **System inertia** — Repeat prescriptions are easier than reassessment; 1 in 5 Scottish adults receives antidepressant prescriptions annually, many for years without review
5. **Undertrained primary care** — A 2025 scoping review (*Research in Social and Administrative Pharmacy*) found GPs face significant barriers: lack of time, confidence, training, and structured support for deprescribing
6. **Patient anxiety** — Many patients are understandably anxious about stopping a medication that "worked"

### The 2024 REDUCE Trial

A University of Liverpool study published in *JAMA Network Open* (2024) found that **more than 40% of long-term users** could successfully discontinue with GP support plus internet/telephone helplines, with lower rates of depression, fewer withdrawal symptoms, and better overall wellbeing.

### Best Practices for Tapering

Based on current evidence:
- **Slow, hyperbolic tapering** — Dose reductions should follow the hyperbolic binding curve of serotonin transporters, meaning progressively smaller dose reductions (e.g., 20→10→5→2.5→1.25 mg), not linear cuts
- **Individualized timelines** — Weeks to months depending on treatment duration and patient sensitivity
- **Liquid formulations** — Enable precise dose titration below available tablet sizes
- **Psychological support** — CBT or other therapy during tapering improves outcomes
- **Fluoxetine bridging** — Switching to fluoxetine before final discontinuation leverages its long half-life for a smoother taper
- **Monitor and distinguish** — Track symptoms carefully to differentiate withdrawal from relapse (withdrawal onset is typically within days; relapse typically takes weeks)

## 5. Epigenetic and Lasting Biological Changes

### Gene Expression Changes

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis in *Molecular Psychiatry* (Cooper et al.) mapped **gene expression signatures of fluoxetine treatment response**, identifying consistent transcriptomic changes across multiple studies.

Key findings from animal studies:

**Myelination gene changes (Kroeze et al., 2015, Translational Psychiatry):**
- Chronic fluoxetine exposure in adult rats altered hippocampal gene expression **40 days after treatment cessation**
- Upregulated genes were enriched for **myelination-related pathways**
- Two key genes—**Transferrin (Tf)** and **Ciliary neurotrophic factor (Cntf)**—showed opposite regulation depending on whether exposure occurred in adulthood vs neonatal period
- This suggests age-dependent epigenetic programming with lasting consequences

**CaMKIIα promoter modifications (Nestler lab, 2013, Neuropsychopharmacology):**
- Chronic fluoxetine **epigenetically altered the CaMKIIα promoter** in the nucleus accumbens
- This regulated ΔFosB binding—a transcription factor involved in both addiction and depression models
- The modification was specifically linked to antidepressant behavioral effects, suggesting the therapeutic mechanism itself involves lasting epigenetic change

**Multi-omics landscape (Arul Rayan et al., 2022, Molecular Psychiatry):**
- Comprehensive analysis across **27 brain regions** using 310 RNA-seq and H3K27 histone modification datasets
- Found **global increase in energy metabolism** and **region-specific chromatin remodeling**
- This is among the most detailed maps of SSRI-induced epigenomic changes ever produced

**HDAC4 and postnatal exposure (Vaidya lab, 2014, Neuropsychopharmacology):**
- Postnatal fluoxetine exposure in rats paradoxically induces **persistent depression- and anxiety-like behaviors** (opposite of adult treatment effects)
- Mediated by increased **histone deacetylase-4 (HDAC4)** recruitment in the hippocampus
- Key transcripts (HDAC4, mTOR, Gnai1, Prkcc, Hcn1) were **consistently dysregulated across the lifespan** and selectively influenced by postnatal but not adult exposure
- Demonstrates that timing of exposure critically determines whether epigenetic changes are adaptive or maladaptive

**Developmental exposure—zebrafish (Nozari et al., 2022, Frontiers in Endocrinology):**
- Brief developmental exposure to fluoxetine (54 µg/L) caused **life-long alteration of the brain transcriptome** in zebrafish
- Effects were measured not just during exposure but persisted into adulthood (6 months post-exposure)
- Disrupted the normal function of the stress axis, with lasting transcriptomic changes in cortisol-responsive pathways
- Demonstrates that early-life SSRI exposure can permanently reprogram brain gene expression across species

**Adolescent exposure—persistent anxiety in mice (Flores-Ramirez et al., 2021, Scientific Reports):**
- Adolescent fluoxetine treatment (PD 35–49) in female mice produced **persistent anxiety-like behavior** that lasted well beyond the washout period
- The phenotype was associated with altered serotonin transporter (5-HTT) and BDNF-related signaling markers (TrkB-ERK1/2-CREB) in hippocampus and prefrontal cortex
- Interestingly, fluoxetine re-exposure in adulthood **ameliorated** the anxiety phenotype, suggesting the changes, while persistent, are not necessarily irreversible

### HPA Axis Effects

- A February 2026 review (*Biology*, MDPI) on "Epigenetic Basis of Stress-Induced CNS Disorders" found that altered DNA methylation, reduced hippocampal histone acetylation, and shifted microRNA profiles are linked to **HPA axis dysregulation** and **limbic system remodeling**
- Some of these changes show **potential reversibility** after pharmacological interventions, but the degree and timeline of reversal remain poorly characterized
- SSRIs are known to modulate the HPA axis during treatment (generally reducing cortisol hyperactivity), but whether this normalization persists, reverses, or overshoots after discontinuation is an active area of investigation

### Clinical Implications

The epigenetic evidence creates an uncomfortable paradox:
- The **therapeutic effects** of fluoxetine may themselves depend on lasting epigenetic modifications (e.g., ΔFosB/CaMKIIα changes in reward circuitry)
- The **adverse persistent effects** (PSSD, emotional blunting) may represent the same type of modification in circuits governing sexual function and emotional processing
- This means you cannot cleanly separate "good persistence" (therapeutic) from "bad persistence" (side effects) at the epigenetic level

## 6. The Positive Case for Long-Term Use

### Relapse Prevention

The evidence for continued treatment preventing relapse is **robust and well-replicated**:

- A foundational 2003 *Lancet* systematic review (Geddes et al.) established that continuing antidepressants **reduces relapse risk by approximately 50–60%** compared to switching to placebo
- A 2024 *Frontiers in Psychiatry* meta-analysis (Voderholzer et al.) on "enduring effects of psychotherapy, antidepressants and their combination" confirmed antidepressants' relapse-prevention efficacy while noting that **psychotherapy has more enduring effects** after treatment ends
- A 2025 Cochrane review on long-term treatment for persistent depression confirmed efficacy of continued pharmacotherapy

### Who Genuinely Needs Indefinite Treatment

Clinical guidelines generally support indefinite treatment for:
- **Three or more depressive episodes** (recurrent depression)
- **Chronic/persistent depressive disorder** (lasting 2+ years)
- **Severe episodes** with suicidal ideation, psychotic features, or significant functional impairment
- **Incomplete remission** with residual symptoms
- **High-risk patients** where relapse carries extreme consequences (prior suicide attempts, loss of employment/relationships during episodes)

### The Risk of Undertreating

The ASCP consensus explicitly warns against reflexive deprescribing:
- Deprescribing should involve careful assessment, not be driven by ideology or media pressure
- Some patients **need indefinite pharmacotherapy**, and premature discontinuation in this group is dangerous
- The Lancet's 2025 editorial "50 years of SSRIs" emphasizes: "For many, SSRIs have been profoundly helpful in managing their health and continue to have an important place in care"

### NIHR Evidence (2022)

A UK National Institute for Health Research analysis found that **almost half of long-term antidepressant users can stop without relapsing**—but crucially, this means roughly half *cannot*. The clinical challenge is identifying which group a given patient belongs to, which current tools do poorly.

### The Nuanced Reality

The best evidence suggests a **bimodal distribution**:
1. **~40–50% of long-term users** are on antidepressants longer than necessary and can safely discontinue with proper support
2. **~50–60% of long-term users** either relapse upon discontinuation or have risk profiles warranting continued treatment
3. **Virtually no one** benefits from abrupt cessation or unsupported fast tapers
4. **Slow tapering with psychological support** offers the best risk-benefit profile for those attempting discontinuation

## Why You're So Tired

Your fatigue isn't one problem. It's at least four things stacking on top of each other.

### 1. Hyperprolactinemia → Hormonal Disruption → Energy Crash

Your prolactin is 121 ng/mL — nearly 4x the upper limit of normal. A 2023 study by Wahlberg et al. in *Hormone and Metabolic Research* specifically studied women with hyperprolactinemia and found they had **significantly increased fatigue, daytime sleepiness, and reduced quality of life** compared to healthy women. This isn't a vague association — elevated prolactin directly suppresses the hormones (estrogen, testosterone) that regulate energy, mood, and vitality.

Your FSH is already being suppressed (2.8, below the normal follicular range), which means your body is getting less of the hormonal fuel it needs. Your free testosterone is at the floor (1.0 pg/mL). Even though total hormone levels look "normal" on paper, the bioavailable amounts — the hormones actually doing work in your body — are depleted. This is a well-recognized pattern called functional hormone deficiency, and fatigue is its signature symptom.

### 2. Iron Stores Are Too Low

Your ferritin is 40 ng/mL. The lab says "normal" because their reference range starts at 15. But current evidence strongly suggests that **ferritin below 50 ng/mL causes fatigue in premenopausal women**, even without anemia. A randomized controlled trial (Vaucher et al., CMAJ 2012) found that iron supplementation reduced fatigue by 29% in non-anemic women with low ferritin — but only in women whose ferritin was below 50. A 2025 CMAJ review on iron deficiency in females reinforced that standard reference ranges dramatically underdiagnose iron deficiency in menstruating women.

You're being hit from three directions: monthly blood loss from menstruation, reduced dietary intake from Vyvanse's appetite suppression, and potentially altered eating patterns. Your BUN/creatinine ratio of 8 (below normal) confirms you're not getting enough protein, which also means you're probably not getting enough dietary iron.

### 3. Vyvanse Crash + Nutritional Deficit

Vyvanse creates a real energy curve throughout the day — functional and focused during active hours, then a dip as it wears off. When you combine that afternoon/evening crash with chronic under-eating (your low BUN proves this), you get a body that's running on fumes by the end of the day. The stimulant masks some of the fatigue during its active window while quietly making the underlying problem worse by suppressing your appetite.

### 4. Cannabis and Sleep Quality

Cannabis helps you fall asleep, but it alters sleep architecture — specifically suppressing REM sleep, the stage your brain needs for emotional processing and memory consolidation. Over time, chronic use means you're getting more hours of sleep but less *restorative* sleep. Needing 9–10 hours might partly reflect your body trying to compensate for lower sleep quality. Research shows that chronic cannabis users don't consistently get better sleep on objective measures (polysomnography) even when they subjectively feel it helps.

### How These Stack

These four factors don't just add up — they multiply. Low iron makes the hormonal fatigue worse. Poor nutrition makes everything worse. Disrupted sleep means you start the day behind. The Vyvanse helps you push through for a while, but it can't fix what's actually broken. You're running a marathon on flat tires, using a turbocharger to compensate instead of fixing the tires.

## The Medication Interaction Nobody Caught

This is the single most important finding across all the research, and it's something to bring to your psychiatrist right away.

**Fluoxetine (Prozac) is a potent inhibitor of CYP2D6** — the liver enzyme that breaks down risperidone. When you take both together, your body can't clear the risperidone efficiently. The result: risperidone builds up in your bloodstream to levels **roughly 75% higher** than what your prescribed dose would normally produce (Spina et al., 2002). In some people, levels quadruple.

This means you're functionally on a much higher dose of risperidone than what's written on the bottle. That directly explains why your prolactin hit 121 — you're getting amplified D2 receptor blockade from a drug that already raises prolactin more aggressively than any other common antipsychotic.

This interaction is well-documented in the pharmacology literature. It should ideally have been caught at the prescribing level, but it's a commonly missed interaction in clinical practice. The fix is straightforward — it just requires your psychiatrist to be aware of it.

## Cannabis: An Honest Look

Cannabis is doing several things that work against your goals:

**For fatigue specifically:**
- It disrupts REM sleep, contributing to the need for 9–10 hours
- Chronic use is independently associated with fatigue and daytime sleepiness
- It may be maintaining a cycle where you need it to sleep, but it's reducing the quality of that sleep

**For your medications:**
- Cannabis can reduce the effectiveness of risperidone. Research shows that patients with psychotic disorders who use cannabis have higher rates of treatment resistance and are prescribed more unique antipsychotics over time
- It adds sedation on top of risperidone's sedation
- The cardiovascular data is genuinely concerning — doubled cardiovascular mortality risk, and this applies to edibles too, not just smoking

**The withdrawal-as-treatment trap:**
One of the most important findings from the cannabis research: regular users who start using for anxiety or sleep often end up in a cycle where the cannabis is maintaining the very symptoms it's "treating." The anxiety that feels like it needs cannabis may be partly cannabis-withdrawal anxiety. The insomnia that needs cannabis may be partly cannabis-withdrawal insomnia. This doesn't mean your original symptoms weren't real — it means the cannabis may have become part of the problem rather than the solution.

**What this doesn't mean:** It doesn't mean you need to quit cold turkey tomorrow. But it's worth being honest about whether cannabis is serving you or whether you're serving it. The clearest test: if you've tried to take a break and found it very difficult, that tells you something.

This is the result most likely to cause anxiety, so let's put it to rest. Almost 18% of healthy young women test ANA-positive. At 1:80, roughly 1 in 4 positives is a false positive. Your ESR (inflammation marker) is 2 mm/hr — essentially zero. Active autoimmune diseases almost always produce elevated inflammation. Your immune system is quiet. This is almost certainly a normal variant, not a disease. No rheumatology workup needed unless you develop specific symptoms (joint pain, rashes, dry eyes/mouth, unusual hair loss).

## Recommendations

### Priority 1: Talk to Your Psychiatrist About the Drug Interaction (This Week)

This is the conversation that can change the most. Bring up the fluoxetine-risperidone CYP2D6 interaction. Your psychiatrist has several options:

| Option | What It Does | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Switch fluoxetine to sertraline or escitalopram** | Removes the CYP2D6 inhibition; risperidone levels normalize | Keeps current antipsychotic; one medication change | Need to transition carefully between SSRIs |
| **Switch risperidone to aripiprazole** | Aripiprazole is "prolactin-sparing" — actually lowers prolactin | Directly solves the prolactin problem; well-studied switch | Different side effect profile; might take adjustment |
| **Add low-dose aripiprazole (2–5 mg) alongside risperidone** | Aripiprazole's partial D2 agonism counteracts prolactin elevation | Doesn't require stopping anything; quick to try | Adds another medication; evidence supports this approach (RCTs show significant prolactin reduction) |
| **Reduce risperidone dose** | Compensates for the interaction; less D2 blockade | Simple; no new medications | May reduce therapeutic effectiveness |
| **Get CYP2D6 genotyping** | One-time test that reveals how you personally metabolize these drugs | Personalizes all future medication decisions | Results take time; cost varies by insurance |

**My read on the best path:** Switching fluoxetine to sertraline or escitalopram is probably the lowest-risk, highest-reward first move. It directly addresses the interaction without changing the antipsychotic. If prolactin doesn't normalize after that switch, then switching risperidone to aripiprazole becomes the next step. CYP2D6 genotyping is worth doing regardless — it's a one-time test that guides all future prescribing decisions.

### Priority 2: Start Nutritional Support (Today)

These are things you can start immediately, no prescription needed:

- **Iron supplementation:** Iron bisglycinate 25mg daily, taken with vitamin C (like a glass of orange juice). This form is gentler on the stomach than ferrous sulfate. Don't take it with coffee, tea, or calcium. Target: ferritin above 50–75 ng/mL. Recheck in 3 months.

- **Protein intake:** Aim for ~1.0–1.2 grams per kilogram of body weight per day. The Vyvanse appetite suppression makes this hard during the day, so:
  - Front-load a protein-rich breakfast *before* the Vyvanse kicks in
  - Use protein shakes or bars during the mid-day suppression window
  - Have a substantial dinner when the Vyvanse wears off in the evening

- **Vitamin D:** Bump up to 2,000–4,000 IU/day of D3. Your level (41) is adequate but not optimal, and given the bone density concerns from the prolactin situation, getting to 50–60 ng/mL matters more for you than average.

### Priority 3: Follow-Up Labs (2–4 Weeks After Any Medication Change)

- **Repeat prolactin** — fasting, morning draw, at least 1 hour after waking
- **Estradiol level** — to see how much the prolactin is actually suppressing your estrogen
- **Pregnancy test** — standard protocol for any reproductive-age woman with hyperprolactinemia
- **Clarify OCP status** — if you're on oral contraceptives, that likely explains the SHBG. If not, the investigation shifts to BMI and Vyvanse effects

### Priority 4: Longer-Term Follow-Up (1–3 Months)

- **MRI of the pituitary** — only if prolactin stays above 100 after medication adjustments. At 121, there's genuine overlap between drug-induced elevation and a microprolactinoma (small, benign pituitary tumor). The medication explanation is strong, but if the number doesn't come down with changes, imaging rules out the alternative
- **DEXA scan (bone density)** — worth considering given your age (24, pre-peak bone mass) and the likely duration of hyperprolactinemia. This establishes a baseline so you know where you stand
- **Recheck ferritin** — after 3 months of supplementation

### Priority 5: Cannabis — An Honest Conversation With Yourself

This isn't about judgment. It's about whether cannabis is actually serving your goals — better energy, better sleep, less reliance on medications. The evidence suggests it may be working against several of those goals simultaneously:

- **Experiment with reduction.** You don't have to go to zero. But trying a 2–4 week break would tell you a lot about what symptoms are "yours" versus what's being maintained by the cannabis cycle. If sleep gets worse initially, that's expected — it resolves in 4–6 weeks as REM rebounds
- **Track your sleep.** If you reduce or stop, notice whether your total sleep need decreases. If you can get through the day on 7–8 hours instead of 9–10, that's strong evidence the cannabis was affecting sleep quality
- **Consider the cardiovascular piece.** At 24, this is long-game thinking, but the doubled cardiovascular mortality risk from chronic use is real and applies even to edibles. Your future self will thank present-you for taking this seriously

### What NOT to Do

- **Don't stop risperidone abruptly.** Any changes should be gradual and supervised by your psychiatrist. Abrupt discontinuation can trigger rebound symptoms
- **Don't panic about the ANA.** It's almost certainly meaningless. Only follow up if you develop joint pain, facial rashes, dry eyes/mouth, Raynaud's (fingers turning white in cold), or unusual hair loss
- **Don't stop Vyvanse without a plan.** If the fatigue improves by fixing the prolactin and iron, you and your doctor can reassess whether you still need it. But don't pull the rug out from under yourself while fixing the other stuff
- **Don't over-test.** You don't need an autoimmune panel, rheumatology referral, or extensive DHEA workup. The current data doesn't support that

## The Optimistic Case

Here's what could realistically happen over the next 3–6 months if these recommendations are followed:

**Month 1:** Fluoxetine is switched to sertraline or escitalopram. Iron supplementation started. Protein intake improved. Within 2–4 weeks of the SSRI switch, the CYP2D6 inhibition clears (note: fluoxetine's active metabolite hangs around for 3–5 weeks, so patience is needed here).

**Month 2:** Prolactin begins dropping as risperidone levels normalize. FSH starts recovering. You may notice your energy improving before the labs even confirm it. Iron stores are building.

**Month 3:** Prolactin rechecked — if it's below 100 (and ideally normalizing), no MRI needed. Ferritin rechecked — targeting 50+. If you've experimented with reducing cannabis, you may notice better sleep quality and less total sleep needed.

**Month 6:** Hormonal axis largely recovered. Energy levels meaningfully improved. If iron stores are replenished, that adds another layer of improvement. The question of whether Vyvanse is still needed can be revisited — you might find that once the underlying fatigue causes are addressed, the need for a stimulant is reduced.

**The realistic expectation:** You're unlikely to feel like a completely different person overnight. But addressing the drug interaction alone could improve your energy meaningfully within weeks. Adding iron supplementation and better nutrition could add another noticeable improvement. And if cannabis reduction improves sleep quality, that's a third layer of recovery. These gains compound.

The body is remarkably good at bouncing back when you remove the things suppressing it. At 24, you have biology on your side. The goal isn't perfection — it's getting your baseline high enough that you don't need a stimulant just to get through the day.

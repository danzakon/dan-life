## 1. Compounded Peptides Beyond GLP-1s

### Market Overview

The broader peptide therapeutics market is projected to reach **$81-87B by 2034-2035**, growing at a **6.8% CAGR**. The peptide supplements sub-market alone is expected to hit **$5.89B by 2030**. GLP-1s opened the floodgates: peptides like BPC-157, TB-500, NAD+, CJC-1295, and Ipamorelin have crossed from biohacker niche into mainstream wellness consciousness.

New York Magazine ran a major feature in January 2026 declaring "Peptides Are the New Wellness Cure-All." Companies like Superpower are openly saying "when a peptide is legal, we are going to sell it." The category has gone fully mainstream.

### Regulatory Landscape

**This is the single biggest risk factor in this market.**

- **February 2026:** HHS announced 14 peptides would return to Category 1 compounding status, reversing some of the 2023-2024 restrictions.
- **March 2026:** FDA reclassification is formally underway but still in flux. BPC-157 and TB-500 status remains contested.
- **March 2026:** Peptide Sciences (the largest grey-market supplier) shut down amid regulatory crackdown. Federal criminal charges are being brought against clinics.
- **503A/503B pharmacy rules** are the key regulatory battleground. Legitimate compounding pharmacies operating under 503B can compound peptides with a prescription, but the line between "legitimate" and "grey market" is being aggressively policed.

**Window assessment:** The window is NARROWING, not opening. The February HHS announcement created temporary optimism, but federal enforcement is intensifying. This is a "move fast or get caught" market.

### Infrastructure Platforms

- **Compounding pharmacies** (Empower Pharmacy, Hallandale Pharmacy) serve as the supply backbone
- **Telehealth platforms** (AgelessRx, Superpower, various DTC peptide clinics) handle patient acquisition and prescriptions
- **OpenLoop Health** provides white-label clinician networks for any company wanting to add peptide prescriptions
- **Grey market** (research chemical suppliers) is being shut down aggressively

### Consumer Demand Signal

**Extremely strong.** Social media is saturated with peptide content. Instagram reels about BPC-157 for injury recovery and NAD+ for energy routinely hit millions of views. The consumer doesn't distinguish between "proven" and "promising" -- they want access.

### Barriers to Entry for Solo AI-Powered Operator

- Need a licensed prescriber in the loop (can use OpenLoop or similar)
- Pharmacy relationship required for fulfillment
- Regulatory risk is personal -- operators face criminal liability
- Content/marketing is where AI leverage shines (education, lead gen, protocol guidance)

### Key Risks

1. **Federal enforcement escalation** -- clinics are being raided, operators charged
2. **FDA could re-restrict** peptides that were just reclassified
3. **Liability exposure** -- these are injectable compounds with limited human trial data
4. **Reputational risk** -- the line between "longevity clinic" and "pill mill" is thin

### Verdict

**High reward, high risk.** The demand is undeniable but the regulatory ground is shifting under operators' feet. Best angle: content and education layer (no prescribing risk) or partner with an established 503B pharmacy as a marketing/patient acquisition channel.

## 2. Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy

### Market Overview

The psychedelic therapeutics market is projected to reach **$11B by 2034** (from ~$3-4B in 2025), growing at a **12-15% CAGR**. The ketamine clinic sub-market is the most mature segment and already generating real revenue.

**Ketamine clinics** are the proven business model today:
- IV ketamine clinic owner income ranges from **$34K to $52M** depending on scale
- Average clinic can break even within **2 months** with the right model
- Revenue per infusion: **$400-800** (not covered by insurance)
- At-home ketamine (sublingual tablets) is the scalable telehealth model -- companies like Mindbloom, Joyous, and Nue Life have proven this works

**Psilocybin therapy** is legal in Oregon and Colorado only:
- Oregon launched its program in 2023; Colorado is actively implementing
- Sessions cost **$1,500-3,500** per facilitated experience
- Supply of trained facilitators is the bottleneck, not demand

**MDMA therapy** remains stalled:
- FDA rejected Lykos Therapeutics' application in August 2024
- CRL (Complete Response Letter) published September 2025 citing safety concerns
- Additional clinical trials required -- **earliest possible approval is 2027-2028**
- This effectively removes MDMA as a near-term business opportunity

### Regulatory Landscape

- **Ketamine:** Already legal (Schedule III, used off-label for depression). DEA crackdowns on telehealth prescribing of controlled substances are the main risk.
- **Psilocybin:** State-by-state, Oregon and Colorado only. More states exploring but no imminent expansion.
- **MDMA:** Dead in the water for now. FDA rejection means years of delay.

**Window assessment:** Ketamine is the only reliable window, and it is **tightening** as DEA scrutinizes telehealth prescribing of controlled substances. At-home ketamine companies face real regulatory risk.

### Infrastructure Platforms

- **Mindbloom, Joyous, Nue Life** -- at-home ketamine platforms (competitors, not infrastructure)
- **Ketamine clinic franchise models** emerging
- No true "OpenLoop equivalent" for psychedelic therapy -- the integration therapy component is deeply human
- **Withfluence, Psychable** -- directories connecting patients with practitioners

### Consumer Demand Signal

**Strong but niche.** Mental health crisis is the demand driver. Treatment-resistant depression and PTSD create a population that has exhausted conventional options. But this isn't "wellness curiosity" like peptides -- it's medical necessity, which means a different customer profile (more desperate, less discretionary).

### Barriers to Entry for Solo AI-Powered Operator

- Ketamine requires DEA registration and prescriber involvement
- Psilocybin facilitation requires state-specific licensing (Oregon: 120+ hours of training)
- Integration therapy is inherently high-touch and human
- AI can assist with intake, screening, and follow-up but cannot replace the core service
- Capital requirements for a brick-and-mortar clinic are substantial (~$200K-500K)

### Key Risks

1. **DEA crackdown on telehealth controlled substance prescribing**
2. **Liability** -- psychedelic experiences can trigger psychiatric crises
3. **Stigma** still exists despite mainstream media coverage
4. **MDMA rejection** signals FDA conservatism on the whole category

### Verdict

**Not ideal for a solo AI-powered operator.** The highest-margin plays (ketamine clinics, psilocybin facilitation) require significant in-person infrastructure, specialized licensing, and deeply human therapeutic relationships. The telehealth ketamine model is the most "insertable" angle but faces increasing regulatory headwinds.

## 3. Testosterone/HRT Telehealth

### Market Overview

The HRT market is projected to reach **$47.29B by 2034**, growing at a **6.7% CAGR**. The testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) sub-market specifically is experiencing explosive growth in the telehealth channel.

**The competitive landscape is a duopoly plus a long tail:**
- **Hims & Hers (HIMS):** Public company, $2B+ revenue run rate. Expanded from ED/hair loss into TRT, GLP-1s, and broader hormone optimization. SEC investigating their compounded GLP-1 practices. Stock down 50% from ATH.
- **Ro:** Private, well-funded. Similar model -- telehealth consultation, prescription, home delivery.
- **Long tail:** Marek Health, Peter MD, Fountain TRT, TRT Nation, and dozens of others competing for the DTC male hormone optimization market.

**Women's hormone optimization** is the under-penetrated adjacent opportunity:
- Menopause/perimenopause telehealth is growing rapidly
- Companies like Alloy, Evernow, and Midi Health raised significant VC funding
- Less competition than men's TRT, higher lifetime value per patient

### Regulatory Landscape

- Testosterone is Schedule III (controlled substance) -- requires DEA involvement
- Telehealth prescribing of controlled substances was expanded during COVID; post-pandemic rules are being finalized
- **Ryan Haight Act** enforcement is the key risk for telehealth-prescribed controlled substances
- Some states require in-person visit before controlled substance prescription

**Window assessment:** The regulatory environment is **stable but watchful**. The telehealth prescribing expansion is likely permanent, but operators must stay within guardrails.

### Infrastructure Platforms

- **OpenLoop Health** -- white-label clinician networks (50-state licensed)
- **Wheel** -- telehealth clinician staffing
- **Truepill/Truepill** -- pharmacy fulfillment and telehealth API
- **Empower Pharmacy, Hallandale** -- compounding pharmacy fulfillment
- The infrastructure layer is **well-developed** -- you can assemble a TRT telehealth business from off-the-shelf components

### Consumer Demand Signal

**White-hot, especially for men 30-55.** "Low T" awareness has been mainstreamed by Joe Rogan, Andrew Huberman, and the broader men's health content ecosystem. Google Trends for "testosterone replacement therapy" and "TRT online" show sustained high interest. The demand is not hype-driven -- it is biologically real and the population is aging into it.

Women's HRT demand is also surging as menopause moves from taboo to mainstream wellness topic.

### Barriers to Entry for Solo AI-Powered Operator

- **Low barriers** when using infrastructure platforms (OpenLoop + compounding pharmacy)
- AI can handle: intake forms, symptom assessment, lab result interpretation, dosage protocol suggestions, follow-up scheduling, patient education content
- Prescriber still required in the loop but can be contracted via OpenLoop ($15-30 per consult)
- Lab work coordination (partnership with Quest/Labcorp or at-home kits)
- **Differentiation challenge:** Market is increasingly commoditized. You compete on brand, content, and customer experience.

### Key Risks

1. **Commoditization** -- Hims/Ro have massive scale advantages
2. **DEA enforcement** on telehealth controlled substance prescribing
3. **Insurance pressure** if payers start covering TRT telehealth (would undercut cash-pay model)
4. **Medical liability** -- hormone optimization has real side effects (cardiovascular, fertility)

### Verdict

**Strong opportunity for a lean operator, especially in women's HRT.** The infrastructure exists to assemble a full telehealth HRT offering without building anything from scratch. Men's TRT is commoditized but still growing. Women's HRT is under-penetrated and commands premium pricing. AI can add real value in the patient experience layer (personalized protocols, ongoing optimization coaching).

## 4. Ozempic-Adjacent Services

### Market Overview

This is arguably the **single best opportunity** on this list because it rides the GLP-1 wave without the regulatory complexity of prescribing the drugs themselves.

**The numbers are staggering:**
- ~15M Americans are on GLP-1 medications as of early 2026
- GLP-1 sales at aesthetics practices topped **$1.5B in 2025** alone
- The weight management market (broader category) is forecast to reach **$60.48B by 2030**
- Body contouring procedures are up **30-50%** year-over-year at many plastic surgery practices, driven almost entirely by GLP-1 weight loss patients

**Key sub-markets:**

| Sub-Market | Description | Maturity |
|------------|-------------|----------|
| Body contouring referrals | Connecting GLP-1 patients with plastic surgeons for loose skin treatment | Early growth |
| Nutrition coaching for GLP-1 users | Preventing muscle loss, optimizing macros while on medication | Early growth |
| GLP-1 adherence/coaching | Digital therapeutics helping patients stay on protocol | Emerging |
| Skin tightening (non-surgical) | Morpheus8, Renuvion, RF treatments for post-weight-loss skin | High growth |
| Fitness programming for GLP-1 users | Resistance training to combat muscle loss side effect | Emerging |
| Mental health support | Addressing identity shift, food relationship changes on GLP-1s | Emerging |

### Regulatory Landscape

**This is the beauty of this market: most of these services have minimal regulatory burden.**

- Nutrition coaching: No prescription required. Can operate as a wellness service.
- Body contouring referrals: Lead generation/marketplace model. No medical license needed.
- Fitness programming: Pure content/coaching play.
- Digital adherence tools: Software, not medicine.
- Non-surgical skin tightening: Requires a medical provider but can be a referral/marketplace model.

**Window assessment:** **WIDE OPEN.** Regulatory risk is minimal because you are not prescribing, compounding, or dispensing medications. You are building services around the medication.

### Infrastructure Platforms

- **No dominant platform exists yet** -- this is greenfield
- RealSelf (cosmetic procedure marketplace) is adjacent but not GLP-1-specific
- Telehealth Ally, Fitt Insider cover the space editorially
- **This is exactly the kind of market where a first-mover can build the platform**

### Consumer Demand Signal

**Off the charts.** Every plastic surgeon in America is seeing GLP-1 patients. "Ozempic butt" and "Ozempic face" are cultural phenomena. Patients are actively searching for what to do about loose skin, muscle loss, and nutritional needs while on these medications. The demand is not speculative -- it is a direct, predictable consequence of millions of people losing significant weight rapidly.

### Barriers to Entry for Solo AI-Powered Operator

- **Extremely low** for the coaching/content/referral angles
- AI-powered nutrition coaching for GLP-1 users could be built and launched in weeks
- Body contouring marketplace/referral network is a classic lead-gen business
- Content marketing (SEO, social) can capture enormous organic demand
- No prescriber relationship needed for most sub-markets
- Can start as content/affiliate and evolve into a platform

### Key Risks

1. **GLP-1 market contraction** if drugs become less popular (unlikely in the near term)
2. **Competition from Hims/Ro** expanding into adjacent services
3. **Low switching costs** -- coaching and content businesses are hard to defend
4. **Medical advice vs. wellness coaching** line must be carefully managed

### Verdict

**The #1 opportunity on this list.** Combines proven, massive demand with minimal regulatory burden and extremely low barriers to entry. A solo AI-powered operator could launch a GLP-1 nutrition coaching service, a body contouring referral marketplace, or a GLP-1 patient community/content platform with near-zero capital. The 15M+ Americans on GLP-1s are all asking the same questions and no dominant platform has emerged to serve them yet.

## 5. At-Home Diagnostics & Lab Testing

### Market Overview

The direct-to-consumer (DTC) lab testing market is projected to reach **$8.16B by 2034**, growing at a strong pace. The continuous glucose monitor (CGM) market alone is valued at **$13.6-47B** depending on scope (diabetes + wellness).

**Key segments:**

| Segment | Market State | Key Players |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| At-home blood panels | Growing rapidly | InsideTracker, Function Health, Hundred Health |
| CGMs for non-diabetics | Emerging | Dexcom Stelo, Abbott Lingo, Levels, Nutrisense |
| Gut microbiome testing | Niche but growing | Viome, Jona, ZOE |
| At-home hormone panels | Strong growth | Everlywell, LetsGetChecked |
| Genetic/pharmacogenomics | Mature | 23andMe, Nebula Genomics |

**Notable data points:**
- Abbott launched Lingo and Dexcom launched Stelo as OTC CGMs for non-diabetic wellness consumers
- Function Health (raised $110M) is building a comprehensive blood panel subscription ($499/year)
- InsideTracker's "Ultimate" plan is $546 -- consumers are willing to pay premium prices for health data

### Regulatory Landscape

- OTC CGMs received FDA clearance (Dexcom Stelo, Abbott Lingo) -- the regulatory door is open
- At-home blood collection kits operate under CLIA-waived testing or partner with CLIA-certified labs
- Lab ordering varies by state -- some require a physician order, others allow DTC
- **FDA is generally supportive** of consumer health data access

**Window assessment:** **Open and likely to remain open.** The trend is toward more consumer access to health data, not less. Bipartisan political support for health transparency.

### Infrastructure Platforms

- **Quest Diagnostics / Labcorp** -- lab fulfillment (blood draw networks, processing)
- **Getlabs, Scarlet** -- at-home phlebotomy services
- **True Health Diagnostics** -- reference lab partnerships
- **Vital** -- API for ordering and receiving lab results programmatically
- The infrastructure to build a DTC lab testing brand exists and is accessible via APIs

### Consumer Demand Signal

**Strong and growing.** The quantified-self movement has gone mainstream. Podcasters (Huberman, Attia) constantly reference biomarkers. CGMs on non-diabetics went from biohacker niche to mainstream wellness in 2024-2025. Consumers want data about their bodies and are willing to pay cash for it.

### Barriers to Entry for Solo AI-Powered Operator

- **Moderate.** The testing itself requires lab partnerships, but these are available via API.
- AI is the **core value proposition** here -- interpreting results, recommending actions, tracking trends over time
- The gap in the market is not more tests -- it is better interpretation and actionable guidance
- An AI-powered "health data concierge" that orders labs, interprets results, and recommends lifestyle changes could be built on top of existing lab infrastructure
- State-by-state physician ordering requirements add complexity

### Key Risks

1. **Commoditization** of basic blood panels (Quest/Labcorp offer DTC directly)
2. **Interpretation liability** -- AI giving medical advice crosses regulatory lines
3. **Consumer fatigue** with data that doesn't lead to action
4. **Margin pressure** -- lab costs are the floor, and testing is becoming cheaper

### Verdict

**Strong opportunity, especially in the interpretation/coaching layer.** The testing infrastructure is commoditized but the "so what do I do about these results?" layer is wide open. An AI-powered health data interpreter that sits on top of lab APIs could serve the growing population of health-conscious consumers who want personalized, actionable guidance -- not just numbers on a page.

## 6. Longevity Medicine

### Market Overview

The anti-aging market (broadest definition) is projected to reach **$1.137 TRILLION by 2035**. The anti-aging drugs sub-market specifically is headed toward **$101.87B by 2035**. The longevity clinic market is a fast-growing segment within this.

**Key treatments in the longevity medicine stack:**

| Treatment | Status | Price Point | Demand |
|-----------|--------|-------------|--------|
| Rapamycin (off-label) | Prescription, off-label | $100-300/month | Growing rapidly |
| Metformin (off-label) | Generic, cheap, easy to prescribe | $10-30/month | Mainstream |
| NAD+ IV therapy | Administered at clinics or via telehealth (subcutaneous) | $250-1000/session | High |
| Senolytics (dasatinib + quercetin) | Research-grade, off-label | $50-200/month | Niche but growing |
| Peptides (see section 1) | Regulatory flux | Varies | High |
| Epigenetic testing | Consumer testing | $300-500/test | Emerging |

**Key players:**
- **AgelessRx** -- longevity telehealth platform (rapamycin, NAD+, metformin, GLP-1s). Published a detailed 2026 platform overview with pricing transparency.
- **Hone Health** -- men's health + longevity telehealth
- **Function Health** -- comprehensive testing + longevity guidance
- **Longevity clinics** (brick-and-mortar) -- proliferating in major metros. Global market report published March 2026.

### Regulatory Landscape

- **Off-label prescribing is legal** -- physicians can prescribe rapamycin, metformin, etc. off-label for longevity
- No FDA-approved "longevity" indication for any drug -- all use is off-label
- Compounding pharmacies can prepare custom formulations (NAD+ injections, etc.)
- The regulatory environment is **permissive by default** for off-label use but could tighten if adverse events make headlines

**Window assessment:** **Open.** Off-label prescribing is a well-established legal framework. The main risk is cultural/political backlash against "rich people living longer" narratives.

### Infrastructure Platforms

- **OpenLoop Health** -- clinician staffing for telehealth longevity consultations
- **Empower Pharmacy, Tailor Made Compounding** -- compounding fulfillment
- **Vital, Getlabs** -- lab ordering infrastructure
- **AgelessRx model** is essentially a template: telehealth consult + compounding pharmacy fulfillment + ongoing monitoring

### Consumer Demand Signal

**Strong and accelerating.** Peter Attia's "Outlive" book (NYT bestseller) mainstreamed longevity medicine. Bryan Johnson's "Blueprint" protocol gets constant media coverage. The target demographic (affluent, 35-65, health-conscious) is large and willing to pay cash. Longevity medicine is currently a **luxury good** but showing signs of mass-market expansion.

### Barriers to Entry for Solo AI-Powered Operator

- **Moderate.** Need prescriber in the loop (OpenLoop) and pharmacy relationship.
- AI adds real value: personalized protocol design based on labs, ongoing optimization, literature synthesis
- The "longevity concierge" model is emerging -- AI-powered guidance on what to take, when, and how to monitor
- Content/education is a major acquisition channel (podcasts, newsletters)
- Differentiation through AI-powered personalization is defensible

### Key Risks

1. **Adverse events** with off-label longevity drugs (rapamycin immune suppression, etc.)
2. **Political/cultural backlash** against longevity medicine as elitist
3. **No proven outcomes** -- longevity medicine is largely theoretical in humans
4. **Liability** for off-label prescribing outcomes

### Verdict

**Strong opportunity with the right positioning.** The intersection of AI + longevity medicine is natural -- these protocols are data-intensive, require ongoing optimization, and benefit from personalization. A solo operator could build an AI-powered longevity concierge that handles protocol design, lab interpretation, and ongoing adjustment. The infrastructure (telehealth clinicians, compounding pharmacies, lab APIs) is all available off-the-shelf.

## 7. Pet Health/Telehealth

### Market Overview

The veterinary telehealth market is growing at **19.8% CAGR**, projected to reach **$2.4B by 2034**. The broader pet healthcare market is enormous ($35B+ annually in the US).

**Key sub-markets:**

| Sub-Market | Status | Opportunity |
|------------|--------|-------------|
| Veterinary telehealth consultations | Growing rapidly | Moderate (crowded) |
| Compounded pet medications | Established market | High |
| Pet GLP-1s / obesity treatment | Pre-market | Speculative but exciting |
| Pet wellness/diagnostics | Emerging | Moderate |

**Pet GLP-1s are the most interesting angle:**
- A cat-specific GLP-1 implant (OKV-119) is in clinical trials (MEOW-1 trial)
- ~60% of dogs and 61% of cats in the US are overweight or obese
- Pet parents are already asking vets about Ozempic for their pets
- Human GLP-1s CANNOT be safely used in animals -- species-specific formulations are needed
- This is a **pre-market opportunity** with massive demand signal

**Compounded pet medications** are the proven revenue opportunity:
- Veterinary compounding is a $1B+ market
- Pets often need custom dosage forms (flavored liquids, transdermal gels)
- Compounding pharmacies serve both human and veterinary markets

### Regulatory Landscape

- Veterinary telehealth regulations vary wildly by state
- VCPR (Veterinarian-Client-Patient Relationship) requirements differ -- some states require in-person exam before telehealth follow-ups
- **FDA regulates veterinary compounding** under different rules than human compounding
- Pet GLP-1s will need FDA-CVM (Center for Veterinary Medicine) approval -- years away

**Window assessment:** **Moderately open.** Veterinary telehealth rules are evolving. Compounded pet medications are a stable, regulated market. Pet GLP-1s are too early.

### Infrastructure Platforms

- **Vetster, PetDesk, Pawp** -- veterinary telehealth platforms
- **Wedgewood Pharmacy, Diamondback Drugs** -- veterinary compounding pharmacies
- **No "OpenLoop for vet" exists** -- this is a gap in the market
- Veterinary practice management systems (eVetPractice, Shepherd) are potential integration points

### Consumer Demand Signal

**Strong for general pet health, speculative for pet GLP-1s.** Pet humanization is a mega-trend -- Americans spend more on pets every year and treat them increasingly like family members. Willingness to pay for premium pet health services is high and growing. But the specific "pet GLP-1" demand, while generating social media buzz, is ahead of available products.

### Barriers to Entry for Solo AI-Powered Operator

- **Moderate to high.** Veterinary prescribing requires a licensed veterinarian (DVM).
- Veterinary compounding requires pharmacy relationships
- AI can add value in: pet health triage, symptom assessment, nutrition guidance, medication reminders
- A "pet health concierge" chatbot that triages issues and connects with vet telehealth is buildable
- Less infrastructure available than human healthcare -- more custom building required

### Key Risks

1. **Regulatory fragmentation** across states for vet telehealth
2. **Pet GLP-1s are years away** from market (don't build a business around a pre-market product)
3. **Veterinary profession resistance** to telehealth disruption
4. **Lower revenue per customer** than human health markets

### Verdict

**Interesting but not the best fit for a solo operator.** The market is growing fast (19.8% CAGR) and pet humanization is a durable trend, but the infrastructure layer is less developed than human healthcare, veterinary licensing adds complexity, and revenue per customer is lower. Best angle might be a pet nutrition/wellness content play that monetizes through affiliate (pet food, supplements) rather than services requiring veterinary licensure.

## Bottom Line

The next GLP-1-scale opportunity is not a single drug -- it is the **ecosystem of services that grows around proven-demand health interventions**. The pattern is:

1. A medical intervention creates massive, proven consumer demand (GLP-1s, TRT, CGMs, longevity drugs)
2. The intervention itself is increasingly commoditized (generics, compounding, telehealth access)
3. The **services layer around the intervention** -- coaching, interpretation, optimization, referrals -- is where margin and defensibility live
4. AI makes this services layer buildable by a solo operator

**Start with Ozempic-adjacent services.** The demand is undeniable, the regulatory burden is minimal, and no dominant platform exists yet. Build the AI-powered GLP-1 patient experience layer and expand into longevity medicine and HRT from there.

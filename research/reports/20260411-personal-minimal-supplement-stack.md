## The Thinking Framework

Before picking products, I asked three questions about every potential supplement:

### 1. Does it have strong human RCT evidence (not just rodent/in-vitro)?

This eliminates ~80% of what supplement companies sell. NMN, resveratrol, lion's mane, AG1, greens powders — all have either thin human evidence, contradictory results, or effects that can be achieved cheaper through diet.

### 2. Does it address a plausible gap in a modern diet?

Most adults in the US are suboptimal in:
- **Omega-3**: Unless you eat fatty fish 3-4x/week, you're almost certainly low
- **Vitamin D**: 42% of US adults are deficient; higher if you work indoors
- **Magnesium**: ~50% of Americans fall below the RDA from food alone
- **Creatine**: Only available from meat/fish; 1-2g/day from food vs 5g optimal dose

These aren't exotic longevity molecules. They're basic nutritional infrastructure that modern diets consistently fail to deliver.

### 3. Does it stack without redundancy with what you already take?

You're already on oral minoxidil (cardiovascular) and topical finasteride (DHT suppression). Any supplement that targets these same pathways is redundant. This is exactly why Nutrafol doesn't make sense for you — and why saw palmetto, biotin mega-doses, and branded hair supplements are out.

### 4. Creatine Monohydrate — The Most Underrated "Longevity" Supplement

**Why:** Creatine is the single most studied supplement in sports science history. But the reason it's in this stack isn't just muscle — it's **brain energy**. Your brain uses 20% of your body's energy, and creatine directly supports the phosphocreatine energy system in neurons. RCTs show improved cognitive performance under sleep deprivation and stress, and emerging evidence links it to neuroprotection as we age.

For your energy goal specifically: creatine doesn't give you a stimulant "buzz." It increases your cells' capacity to regenerate ATP — the actual energy currency. The result is sustained physical and mental performance, not a spike and crash.

**What to buy:** Creatine monohydrate is a commodity. The molecule is the molecule. Don't pay for "micronized," "buffered," or "HCl" variants — they have zero evidence of superiority.

## What I Deliberately Left Out

### Multivitamin — Skip It

Controversial take: for most people eating a reasonably varied diet, a multivitamin is expensive urine. The [USPSTF](https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/vitamin-supplementation-to-prevent-cvd-and-cancer-preventive-medication) found insufficient evidence that multivitamins prevent cardiovascular disease or cancer. The targeted approach (D3, Mg, omega-3) addresses the *specific* deficiencies that are actually common, at therapeutic doses. A multi gives you trace amounts of many things — enough of nothing.

If your diet is genuinely terrible (fast food daily, no vegetables), a basic multi is fine as insurance. But it's not in the core stack.

### AG1 / Athletic Greens — Hard No

$79/month for a greens powder with proprietary blends and no meaningful dose of anything. The omega-3 + D3 + Mg + creatine stack does more for $50-70/month with transparent dosing and third-party testing. AG1 is marketing, not medicine.

### NMN / NR (NAD+ Precursors) — Not Yet

The longevity potential is intriguing, but human evidence is immature. Rodent data is promising; human RCTs are small, short, and inconsistent. At $40-60/month, it's a poor first purchase when you haven't even optimized the basics. Revisit in 2-3 years when the evidence matures.

### Curcumin/Turmeric — Redundant

With high-dose omega-3 already in your stack, you've got the anti-inflammatory base covered by the most broadly supported intervention. Curcumin adds pills, cost, and complexity for marginal additional benefit. The bioavailability problem (you need special formulations to absorb it) makes it even less attractive.

### Separate Biotin — Absolutely Not

You don't need supplemental biotin. Deficiency is extremely rare in people eating a normal diet. High-dose biotin interferes with thyroid tests, troponin assays, and other bloodwork (FDA-warned). The small amount in food is sufficient. If anyone suggests biotin for hair, redirect them to the evidence: it only helps in true deficiency, which you almost certainly don't have.

### Saw Palmetto — Already Covered

You have topical finasteride. Saw palmetto is a weaker version of the same mechanism. Skip. (Full analysis in the Nutrafol report.)

### Probiotics — Too Generic

Probiotic benefits are strain-specific, not "take any probiotic and feel better." Unless you have a specific GI condition (IBS, post-antibiotic recovery), daily generic probiotics are a lottery ticket.

### Collagen — Overhyped

Collagen supplements break down into amino acids in your gut, just like any protein. If your total protein intake is adequate (0.7-1g per pound of body weight), you don't need supplemental collagen. Eat enough protein and save $30/month.

## Safety Notes for Your Specific Situation

### Oral Minoxidil Interactions

Oral minoxidil is a potent vasodilator originally developed for hypertension. At hair-loss doses (2.5-5mg), cardiovascular effects are typically mild but real.

| Supplement | Interaction | Risk Level | Action |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Magnesium | Mild additive BP-lowering | Low | Monitor for dizziness when standing; mention to prescriber |
| Omega-3 | Minimal BP effect at normal doses | Very Low | No concern at 2g/day |
| Ashwagandha | Mild hypotensive properties | Low-Moderate | Mention to prescriber; monitor |
| Creatine | No cardiovascular interaction | None | Safe to combine |
| D3+K2 | No direct interaction | None | Safe to combine |

### Blood Test Considerations

Before any bloodwork, inform your doctor/lab:

| Supplement | Lab Impact | Action |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Creatine | Raises serum creatinine (kidney marker) — artifact, not damage | Tell your doctor |
| Biotin (if any) | Distorts thyroid panels, troponin | Stop 48-72h before labs |
| Vitamin D3 | You're supplementing; report dose so they interpret 25-OH D correctly | Report dose |

### First-Month Protocol

Don't start everything at once. Stagger introduction so you can identify any issues:

```
Week 1:  Omega-3 + D3/K2 (lowest risk, start the foundation)
Week 2:  Add magnesium glycinate (evening)
Week 3:  Add creatine (powder)
Week 4+: Add ashwagandha if desired (with liver panel baseline)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **The Big 4 (omega-3, D3+K2, magnesium, creatine) is not a trend — it's the convergence point.** Every evidence-based practitioner arrives at these same 4 supplements because they address the most common nutritional gaps with the strongest human data.

2. **Targeted beats shotgun.** Four well-dosed, high-quality supplements outperform 15 underdosed ingredients in a proprietary blend. You know exactly what you're getting and at what dose.

3. **Bloodwork is the highest-leverage action.** A $50-150 blood panel tells you exactly what your body actually needs. Everything else is educated guessing. Get the test, then supplement what's actually low.

4. **Your pill count: 4 pills + drops + powder scoop.** That's it. Not 20. The D3/K2 liquid drops and creatine powder eliminate 3-4 capsules compared to pill-form alternatives.

5. **Ashwagandha is a personal risk-benefit call.** The stress data is real, the liver risk is rare but serious. Behavioral interventions (sleep, exercise, caffeine management) should come first. If you add it, cycle it and monitor liver enzymes.

6. **Everything expensive that I left out (AG1, NMN, collagen, branded hair supplements) is either redundant with what's in the stack, under-evidenced, or both.** Don't let marketing complexity convince you that more = better.

7. **Your hair stack is already near-maximal.** The supplement layer for hair is "fix deficiencies" — which this core stack largely does. Add microneedling for the strongest additional evidence-based intervention.

## Executive Summary

Europe's decline is not a mystery. It's the predictable result of policy choices made over two decades by a political class that prioritized ideology over outcomes, consensus over debate, and moral posturing over strategic thinking.

This report examines five interconnected policy failures that have left Europe economically stagnant, militarily vulnerable, energy-dependent, socially fractured, and technologically irrelevant:

1. **Energy Policy:** Germany shut down its nuclear plants, became dependent on Russian gas, and now has some of the highest electricity prices in the developed world. Its own chancellor now calls this a "serious strategic mistake."

2. **Climate Policy:** The Green Deal imposed costs that European industry couldn't bear while competitors in the US and China faced no such constraints. Farmers revolted. Industry fled. Emissions targets became economic suicide pacts.

3. **Defense Policy:** Decades of free-riding on American military protection left Europe unable to defend itself or adequately support Ukraine. NATO's 2% target was treated as a ceiling, not a floor—and most countries didn't even hit that.

4. **Regulatory Policy:** GDPR, the AI Act, and layers of compliance burden drove tech talent and companies to the US. Europe became a place that regulates innovation rather than creates it.

5. **Immigration Policy:** Mass migration without integration infrastructure created parallel societies, gang networks, and periodic riots. Sweden went from one of Europe's safest countries to having its highest rate of gun violence. Every major European country has now reversed course.

The common thread: policies designed to signal virtue rather than achieve results, implemented by elites who faced no consequences for failure, and insulated from democratic accountability until the damage was done.

## Energy Policy: The Self-Inflicted Crisis

### Germany's Nuclear Suicide

In 2011, following the Fukushima disaster in Japan, Germany decided to phase out nuclear power. By April 2023, the last three reactors were shut down.

The logic: Nuclear power was dangerous. Germany would transition to renewables. Everything would be fine.

The reality: Germany became dependent on Russian natural gas, then scrambled when Russia invaded Ukraine, and now has among the highest electricity prices in the developed world.

**January 2026:** Chancellor Friedrich Merz called the nuclear phase-out a ["serious strategic mistake"](https://www.aa.com.tr/en/energy/general/germanys-merz-calls-nuclear-phaseout-serious-strategic-mistake/54076), stating it led to "insufficient energy capacity, inflated costs, and an overly expensive energy transition reliant on subsidies and fossil fuels."

He's right. But it took a decade and an energy crisis to admit what critics said from the start.

### The Numbers Tell the Story

════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This isn't a minor disadvantage. Energy-intensive industries—chemicals, steel, aluminum, glass—cannot compete when their electricity costs are double or triple their competitors'.

### The Deindustrialization

The consequences are visible:

**BASF**, Germany's chemical giant and a pillar of German industry for 150 years, is [shifting investment to China](https://asiatimes.com/2024/11/germany-closing-factories-at-home-opening-them-in-china/). The company's €10 billion Zhanjiang complex in China is expanding while German operations contract.

According to [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/article/rust-belt-on-the-rhine-the-deindustrialization-of-germany/), Germany's biggest companies are "ditching the fatherland." Energy costs are the primary driver.

**German industrial production:** Down 10%+ from pre-pandemic levels. Manufacturing employment declining. Factory closures accelerating.

The Energiewende—Germany's celebrated energy transition—has produced:
- Higher emissions (coal replaced nuclear)
- Higher prices (consumers and industry pay for subsidies)
- Lower reliability (intermittent renewables require backup)
- Strategic vulnerability (dependence on Russian gas until 2022)

### Who Saw This Coming?

Everyone who looked honestly at the numbers.

In 2018, Donald Trump [warned Germany](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xGtK26vEM0s) at a NATO summit about dependence on Russian energy. German diplomats laughed. The clip has aged well.

Nuclear engineers, energy economists, and strategic analysts said for years that shutting down reliable baseload power while increasing dependence on Russian gas was dangerous. They were dismissed as industry shills or climate deniers.

The same political establishment that created the problem now claims to be solving it—by reversing the policies they championed.

## Climate Policy: Ideology Over Economics

### The Green Deal's Impossible Math

The European Green Deal, launched in 2019, aimed to make Europe "climate neutral" by 2050. It imposed binding emissions targets, mandated renewable energy quotas, and created a regulatory apparatus covering everything from car emissions to agricultural practices.

The ambition was genuine. The execution was disastrous.

**The fundamental problem:** Europe was imposing costs on its own industry that competitors didn't face.

```
Climate Policy Asymmetry
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

                    Carbon    Regulatory    Industrial
                    Price     Burden        Competitiveness
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
European Union      €80/ton   High          Declining
United States       ~$0       Low           Rising
China               ~$10      Low           Rising
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Result: European industry faces costs competitors don't pay
        Products become uncompetitive
        Production moves to jurisdictions with lower standards
        Global emissions unchanged or higher ("carbon leakage")
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

This isn't climate policy. It's economic unilateral disarmament dressed up as environmentalism.

### The Farmer Revolt

In early 2024, farmers across Europe [revolted](https://www.reuters.com/world/europe/angry-farmers-descend-brussels-take-protest-eu-summit-2024-02-01/). Tractors blocked Brussels. Highways were shut down in France, Germany, Poland, and the Netherlands. Manure was dumped on government buildings.

The proximate causes varied—fuel taxes, pesticide bans, environmental regulations—but the underlying complaint was consistent: EU climate and environmental policies were making farming economically unviable.

What happened next was instructive. Within days, the European Commission [caved](https://www.politico.eu/article/france-tractors-farmer-protests-gabriel-attal-emmanuel-macron/):

- Green farming rules reversed
- Pesticide reduction plans killed
- Ukrainian import restrictions imposed
- Environmental targets postponed

Years of work on the green agriculture agenda abandoned in a week because farmers had tractors and were willing to use them.

The lesson: These policies were never democratically sustainable. They were imposed by an elite consensus that collapsed the moment it faced organized resistance.

### The Greta Problem

Greta Thunberg became the face of European climate activism—a teenager lecturing adults about scientific consensus while offering no viable path forward.

The problem wasn't the science. The problem was the politics.

Climate activism of the Thunberg variety demanded immediate, radical action regardless of economic consequences. "How dare you" was the message to anyone who mentioned costs, tradeoffs, or feasibility.

This framing made climate policy impossible to discuss rationally. Any acknowledgment that the transition had costs was treated as climate denial. Any suggestion that Europe acting alone wouldn't solve a global problem was dismissed as defeatism.

The result: Policies that imposed massive costs on European citizens and industry while achieving minimal emissions reductions globally. Moral satisfaction substituted for actual impact.

China's emissions dwarf Europe's. India's are growing rapidly. The US withdrew from Paris and saw emissions decline anyway through market-driven shifts. Europe mortgaged its economic future for climate policies that, even if fully implemented, would have negligible global impact.

### The Climate Science Credibility Problem

Meanwhile, climate science itself suffered from overstated claims:

In December 2025, [Nature retracted](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/03/business/economy/study-climate-damage-retracted.html) a prominent 2024 study claiming climate change would cause catastrophic economic damage. The study, which had been cited by central banks and incorporated into risk management scenarios, was found to have significant data errors that skewed results.

This matters because policy depends on credible science. When high-profile studies are retracted, when predictions fail to materialize, when the most extreme claims prove unfounded—the entire enterprise loses credibility.

Reasonable people can support emissions reduction without endorsing apocalyptic predictions or economically suicidal policies. But the climate establishment's refusal to distinguish between "significant problem requiring action" and "existential emergency requiring immediate sacrifice" undermined public trust.

## Defense Policy: The Peace Dividend That Wasn't

### Thirty Years of Free-Riding

After the Cold War ended, Europe cashed in the "peace dividend." Defense budgets were slashed. Military capabilities atrophied. NATO's Article 5 guarantee was treated as a substitute for actual defense capacity.

The assumption: American military power would always be there. Russia was defeated. War in Europe was unthinkable.

This was strategic malpractice dressed up as enlightened post-nationalism.

### The 2% Fairy Tale

In 2014, after Russia annexed Crimea, NATO allies agreed to spend 2% of GDP on defense. A decade later, most still hadn't hit the target.

Even now, nearly a third of NATO members remain below target.

The new NATO target is 5% of GDP. Most European countries will struggle to reach 3%.

### The Ammunition Scandal

When Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, Europe discovered it had essentially disarmed.

**Artillery ammunition:** The EU [promised](https://www.rferl.org/a/ukraine-weapons-shells-european-union-eu-war-russia-investigation/33025300.html) 1 million 155mm shells to Ukraine. It delivered 650,000. European production capacity was revealed to be far below official claims—perhaps half what EU officials stated publicly.

According to [investigations by European journalists](https://www.ftm.eu/articles/who-pays-for-ukraine-s-155mm-grenade), actual shell production capacity may be 600,000 rounds annually—not the 1.4-1.7 million the Commission claimed.

Russia produces more ammunition than all of NATO combined.

**The production gap:**

European defense industries were optimized for peacetime procurement, not wartime production. When crisis came, they couldn't scale.

### The Strategic Dependency

Europe's defense dependency on the United States is now undeniable:

- **Air defense:** European countries lack sufficient air defense systems. They're dependent on American Patriots and waiting for systems that don't exist.
- **Strategic airlift:** European countries cannot move forces without American C-17s and C-5s.
- **Intelligence:** European countries rely on American satellite and signals intelligence.
- **Nuclear deterrent:** Only France and the UK have nuclear weapons. Everyone else depends on American extended deterrence.

President Zelenskyy [stated explicitly](https://sceeus.se/en/publications/can-europe-stand-alone-in-supporting-ukraine-scaling-up-the-defence-industry-and-funding-defence-production/) that "Europe cannot support Ukraine alone without the US."

This dependency was a choice. For thirty years, European governments chose to spend on social programs rather than defense, trusting America to provide security. Now that trust is being tested by a President who openly questions whether Europe deserves protection it won't pay for.

## Regulatory Policy: Strangling Innovation

### The GDPR Template

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), implemented in 2018, was supposed to protect European citizens' privacy while setting a global standard.

The reality: GDPR created massive compliance burdens, especially for small companies; generated cookie consent popups that everyone clicks through without reading; and did little to prevent the actual privacy violations people care about.

More importantly, GDPR established a template: Europe regulates first, asks questions later. Innovation must prove it's safe before being allowed. The precautionary principle trumps experimentation.

### The AI Act: Repeat Performance

The EU AI Act, taking effect in stages from 2024, applies the same logic to artificial intelligence:

- Extensive compliance requirements
- Risk categorization bureaucracies
- Documentation mandates
- Restrictions on "high-risk" systems

According to a [landmark survey](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-tech-scene-denounces-ai-data-rules-bad-for-growth-meta-google-survey-gdpr/) of European tech founders and investors, EU AI and data rules have "negatively" affected conditions for starting or scaling tech companies.

European AI companies like Mistral are [expanding in Silicon Valley](https://www.ft.com/content/71e8248d-b473-47fa-979d-172bfa75df3e)—not to access European markets, but to access American talent that doesn't want to work under European regulation.

Norwegian AI startups are [fleeing](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366632823/EU-regulation-sharpens-fear-of-Norwegian-AI-exodus) to avoid EU-aligned rules their government is adopting.

### The Brain Drain

The talent flow is one-directional: out of Europe, into the United States.

European founders [choose the US](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/charles-martin-280a41178_the-brain-drain-of-european-talent-to-the-activity-7323718270899933184-bt3r) for:
- Access to capital (US venture funding dwarfs Europe's)
- Regulatory freedom (can build first, comply later)
- Talent density (other ambitious people are already there)
- Market size (can scale domestically before going international)

The [precautionary principle](https://thedailyeconomy.org/article/europes-precautionary-principle-is-killing-the-next-big-thing/) that underlies European regulation—prove safety before deploying—is fundamentally incompatible with technological innovation, which requires experimentation, failure, and iteration.

- "Simplification" becoming the new buzzword

This is the familiar pattern: impose ideological regulations, discover they don't work, quietly reverse them while claiming credit for "reform."

## Immigration Policy: The Integration Myth

### The Experiment

For two decades, Western European governments ran an experiment: accept large-scale immigration from the developing world, provide housing and benefits, and assume integration would happen organically.

The results are in. The experiment failed.

**Germany (2015):** Chancellor Merkel unilaterally declared "Wir schaffen das" (we can do this), opened borders, and accepted over 1 million asylum seekers in a single year—with no plan for housing, integration, or security vetting. Germans who objected were told they were on the wrong side of history.

**Sweden:** Accepted more asylum seekers per capita than any EU country (1.6% of population in 2015 alone). Placed refugees in segregated housing blocks, provided welfare but few employment pathways, and made no serious demands for language acquisition or cultural adaptation.

**France:** Built massive public housing projects on urban peripheries, concentrated immigrant populations in these banlieues, and tolerated 30%+ unemployment for decades while pretending parallel societies didn't exist.

### The Consequences

**Sweden** went from one of Europe's safest countries to having the worst gang violence on the continent:

```
Sweden: From Safety to Crisis
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Gun homicides:     17 (2011) → 53 (2023)     Tripled in 12 years
Bombings:          ~40 (2016) → 257 (2019)   Now 30+/month
Criminal networks: 62,000 connected          In a country of 10M
"Vulnerable areas": 59 zones                 Where police struggle to operate

Sweden now has the HIGHEST gun homicide rate in the EU
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

Swedish police designate 59 "vulnerable areas" (utsatta områden)—neighborhoods where police have difficulty operating, emergency services require escorts, and criminal networks exercise de facto control. The government doesn't call them "no-go zones." Everyone else does.

The Swedish Prime Minister admitted in 2022: "Sweden has failed to integrate the vast numbers of immigrants it has taken in over the past two decades, leading to parallel societies and gang violence."

**France's** integration failures erupt periodically:
- **2005:** Three weeks of riots after two teenagers died fleeing police. 9,000 cars burned. State of emergency declared.
- **2023:** Six nights of nationwide riots after police shot 17-year-old Nahel M. 45,000 police deployed. Hundreds of buildings damaged.

These aren't spontaneous uprisings. They're the predictable result of concentrating disadvantaged populations in isolated suburbs with no economic prospects.

### The Integration Gap

| Employment rate | 80% | 55-60% |
| Unemployment | 4% | 15-18% |

The employment gap is 20-25 percentage points—larger than the black-white employment gap in the United States.

A growing economy can absorb newcomers. A stagnant economy cannot. Europe chose both: mass immigration AND economic stagnation. The combination was toxic.

### The Reversal

Every major European country has now tightened immigration policy:

| Country | Policy Shift |
|---------|-------------|
| **Sweden** | Asylum seekers: 163,000 (2015) → 9,000 (2023). Now paying migrants €34,000 to leave. |

| **Germany** | Border controls reinstated. Deportation acceleration. |
| **France** | 2024 immigration law with language requirements, tighter family reunification. |

The political mainstream spent 20 years calling these policies racist. Now they're implementing them.

## The Draghi Verdict

In September 2024, Mario Draghi—former ECB President, the man who "saved the euro"—delivered a [400-page report](https://commission.europa.eu/topics/competitiveness/draghi-report_en) on European competitiveness. Its conclusions were devastating.

### The Diagnosis

**Productivity:** EU productivity growth has been 1/3 of US levels since 2000. The gap is accelerating.

**Innovation:** Europe invests less in R&D, produces fewer patents, and creates fewer breakthrough companies than the US or China.

**Regulation:** "Regulatory burdens fall disproportionately on smaller companies," limiting scale-ups.

**Energy:** "Higher energy costs undermine competitiveness in energy-intensive sectors."

**Capital markets:** Fragmented European markets can't fund scale the way American markets can.

**Scale:** The single market is not actually single. Regulatory fragmentation creates 27 mini-markets.

### The Prescription

Draghi called for €800 billion in additional annual investment—roughly 5% of EU GDP—to close the gap with the US and China.

This isn't happening. According to [ECIPE](https://ecipe.org/insights/draghi-report-turns-one/), one year after the report, implementation "is still more crawling than walking."

### The Real Message

Draghi's report was polite, diplomatic, and framed in the language of EU institutions. But the underlying message was stark:

**Europe's economic model is failing.** The combination of high regulation, high energy costs, fragmented markets, and low investment is producing stagnation. Without fundamental change, decline will accelerate.

The political class that created these problems is unlikely to solve them. They're too invested in the existing system, too dependent on the interest groups that benefit from regulation, and too ideologically committed to the approaches that failed.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Energy policy was strategic suicide.** Germany shut down nuclear plants while becoming dependent on Russian gas. The result: the highest industrial electricity prices in the developed world and accelerating deindustrialization. Chancellor Merz now calls this a "serious strategic mistake." It was obvious at the time.

2. **Climate policy prioritized symbolism over impact.** The Green Deal imposed costs on European industry while competitors faced none. The result: carbon leakage (production moving to dirtier jurisdictions), farmer revolts, and policy reversals. Europe sacrificed economic competitiveness for minimal global emissions impact.

3. **Defense free-riding has ended.** Thirty years of underspending left Europe unable to defend itself or adequately support Ukraine. The ammunition shortage alone demonstrates how hollow European military capacity had become. The 5% NATO target will require cuts to social spending that European publics haven't agreed to.

4. **Regulatory policy strangled innovation.** GDPR and the AI Act created compliance burdens that favored large incumbents over startups. The brain drain to the US accelerated. No major European tech company has been created in the internet era. The EU now belatedly recognizes it over-regulated and is walking back rules.

5. **Immigration without integration created parallel societies.** Sweden went from Europe's safest country to having its highest gun violence rate. France's banlieues riot periodically. The employment gap between natives and immigrants exceeds 20 percentage points. Every major country has now reversed course—implementing the restrictive policies they spent two decades calling racist.

6. **The Draghi Report confirmed systemic failure.** Productivity growth 1/3 of US levels. Zero €100B+ companies created in 50 years. Energy costs 2-4x competitors. The diagnosis is in. The treatment is not being applied.

7. **Elite consensus overrode democratic accountability.** In every case—immigration, energy, climate, defense, regulation—policies were implemented by political elites who faced no consequences for failure. Voters were told their concerns were ignorant or bigoted. Reversal came only when crisis made continuation impossible.

## Predictions

Based on current trajectories:

1. **German deindustrialization will accelerate.** BASF's shift to China is a leading indicator. Energy-intensive industries will continue moving to the US (Inflation Reduction Act incentives) and Asia. Germany's manufacturing base will hollow out over the next decade.

2. **The Green Deal will be quietly gutted.** Farmer protests showed the political limits. Climate targets will be postponed, exemptions expanded, and enforcement weakened—while officials continue claiming commitment to the goals.

3. **European defense spending will rise but remain inadequate.** The 5% target will be missed by most countries. Social spending constituencies are too powerful. Europe will remain dependent on American protection while complaining about American leadership.

4. **Tech regulation will be reversed.** The competitive disadvantage is too obvious. GDPR will be hollowed out. The AI Act will be softened. But the damage—lost companies, departed talent—won't be recovered.

5. **Right-wing parties will continue gaining.** The political establishment that created these failures will continue losing credibility. Voters who were called racists for questioning immigration, deniers for questioning climate policy, and alarmists for questioning defense spending will continue shifting rightward.

6. **Immigration restrictions will tighten further.** Sweden paying migrants to leave is just the beginning. Deportation programs will expand. Integration requirements will become mandatory. The open-borders consensus of the 2010s is dead. The only question is whether reversal came in time to prevent permanent parallel societies.

7. **The US-Europe economic gap will widen.** American GDP per capita will exceed EU levels by 100% within a decade. Productivity divergence will accelerate. Europe will become a regulatory museum—well-preserved, pleasant to visit, economically irrelevant.

The policies have been tried. The results are in. The reckoning is underway.

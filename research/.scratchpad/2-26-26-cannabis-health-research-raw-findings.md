## A1. The UCSF CANDIDE Study (2025) — Blood Vessel Function Destroyed by Cannabis

### Study Details

- **Title:** "Association of Endothelial Dysfunction With Chronic Marijuana Smoking and THC-Edible Use"
- **Authors:** Leila Mohammadi, MD, PhD; Matthew L. Springer, PhD; Gregory M. Marcus, MD, MAS; Joseph C. Wu, MD; et al.
- **Published:** May 28, 2025 in *JAMA Cardiology* (10(8):851–855)
- **DOI:** 10.1001/jamacardio.2025.1399
- **Study Name:** CANnabis: Does It Damage Endothelium (CANDIDE)
- **URLs:**
  - UCSF press release: https://www.ucsf.edu/news/2025/05/430051/whether-its-smoking-or-edibles-marijuana-bad-your-heart
  - PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40434782/
  - Full text PDF: https://cdn.cannabis.ca.gov/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/02/2020-ucsf-association_endothelial_dysfunction.pdf

### Design & Participants

- Cross-sectional study of 55 sex- and age-matched healthy adults aged 18–50 in the San Francisco Bay Area
- 20 females, 35 males; mean age 31
- None used tobacco or vaped
- Three groups: marijuana smokers (≥3 smoking sessions/week for ≥1 year), THC-edible users (≥3 edibles/week for ≥1 year), and nonusers
- Recruited October 2021 – August 2024; analysis completed September 2024

### Key Results — The Numbers

- **Arterial flow-mediated dilation (FMD):**
  - Marijuana smokers: **6.0%**
  - THC-edible users: **4.6%**
  - Nonusers: **10.4%**
- FMD was **significantly lower** in both cannabis smoker and edible user groups compared to nonusers — **vascular function reduced roughly by half**
- In endothelial cells treated with participants' sera, VEGF-stimulated nitric oxide levels were significantly lower for the marijuana smoker group (mean 1.1 nmol/L) vs. nonuser group (mean 1.5 nmol/L)
- For THC-edible users, FMD was impaired but VEGF-stimulated NO was *not* significantly different from nonusers — suggesting a **different mechanism** for edible-related vascular damage
- **Inverse correlation** observed between FMD and smoking frequency, and between FMD and the amount of THC ingested

### Key Quotes

> "This study enhances the understanding of the potential risks to vascular health linked to cannabis use and provides more evidence that cannabis use is not benign." — Study authors

> "There's a misconception that marijuana, being more 'natural' than highly processed cigarettes from tobacco companies, produces smoke that is less toxic. But that's not the case." — Matthew Springer, PhD, UCSF professor of cardiology

### Why This Matters

- Decreased vascular function (endothelial dysfunction) is associated with greater risk of heart attack, hypertension, and other cardiovascular conditions
- This is the first study to show that **edible cannabis** — not just smoked — produces comparable vascular damage to tobacco smoking
- Previously, edibles were recommended by some clinicians as a "safer" route of administration for cardiovascular health

## A2. The BMJ Heart Meta-Analysis (2025) — Cannabis Doubles Cardiovascular Death Risk

### Study Details

- **Title:** "Cardiovascular risk associated with the use of cannabis and cannabinoids: a systematic review and meta-analysis"
- **Authors:** Wilhelm Storck, PhD; Meyer Elbaz; Cécile Vindis; Amélia Déguilhem; Maryse Lapeyre-Mestre; Emilie Jouanjus, PhD, PharmD (all University of Toulouse / INSERM, France)
- **Published:** June 17, 2025 in *Heart* (BMJ), 111:1047–1056
- **DOI:** 10.1136/heartjnl-2024-325429
- **URLs:**
  - BMJ Heart: https://heart.bmj.com/content/early/2025/06/10/heartjnl-2024-325429
  - BMJ Group press release: https://bmjgroup.com/cannabis-use-linked-to-doubling-in-risk-of-cardiovascular-disease-death/
  - Full text PDF: https://realmofcaring.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Cardiovascular-risk-associated-with-the-use-of-cannabis-and-cannabinoids-a-systematic-review-and-meta-analysis.pdf

### Design

- Systematic review and meta-analysis
- Included pharmacoepidemiological studies from January 2016 to January 2023 (PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus; last search September 2023)
- 24 studies included
- Random-effect model with inverse variance weighting
- Study quality assessed with ROBINS-E

### Key Results — The Numbers

- **Cardiovascular death:** Adjusted risk ratio (aRR) **2.10** (95% CI, 1.29–3.42)
- **Acute coronary syndrome (including MI):** aRR **1.29** (95% CI, 1.05–1.59)
- **Stroke:** aRR **1.20** (95% CI, 1.13–1.26)
- No significant association for the composite outcome of ACS + stroke combined
- Focused analysis restricted to cohort studies yielded **comparable results**

### Key Quotes

> "What we bring with this meta-analysis is we quantify the associations. There are implications for both prevention and management of disease." — Emilie Jouanjus, PhD, PharmD, senior author

> "The idea with this study is not to demonize cannabis, but just to make people aware there is a safety signal." — Emilie Jouanjus

### Linked Editorial — "Treat It Like Tobacco"

- An accompanying editorial in *Heart* (DOI: 10.1136/heartjnl-2025-326169) called for cannabis to be treated like tobacco: **not criminalized, but actively discouraged, with protection of bystanders from secondhand vapour inhalation**
- URL: https://heart.bmj.com/content/early/2025/06/10/heartjnl-2025-326169

## A3. The AHA / Keyhani Study (2024) — 434,104 Americans, Daily Use Linked to MI and Stroke

### Study Details

- **Title:** "Association of Cannabis Use With Cardiovascular Outcomes Among US Adults"
- **Lead Author:** Abra Jeffers (Massachusetts General Hospital); senior author Salomeh Keyhani
- **Published:** February 28, 2024 in *Journal of the American Heart Association* (JAHA)
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38415581/
- **AHA News:** https://www.heart.org/en/news/2024/02/28/marijuana-use-linked-to-higher-risk-of-heart-attack-and-stroke

### Design

- Population-based cross-sectional study
- Data from Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS), 2016–2020
- **N = 434,104** US adults aged 18–74 from 27 states
- Daily use scored as 1 (30/30 days), 15 days/month scored as 0.5, nonuse scored as 0
- Multivariable logistic regression controlling for tobacco use and other risk factors

### Key Results — The Numbers

- **Daily cannabis use** associated with:
  - Myocardial infarction: **aOR 1.25** (95% CI, 1.07–1.46)
  - Stroke: **aOR 1.42** (95% CI, 1.20–1.68)
  - Composite cardiovascular outcomes: **aOR 1.28** (95% CI, 1.13–1.44)
- **4% of respondents** who reported daily cannabis use had a **49% increased risk** for MI and a **twofold increased risk** for stroke
- Associations remained **significant among never-smokers** and younger adults (18–44)
- Cardiovascular risk increased as usage level increased (dose-response relationship)

### Key Quotes

> "Despite common use, little is known about the risks of cannabis use and, in particular, the cardiovascular disease risks. The perceptions of the harmfulness of cannabis are decreasing faster than the science can keep up." — Dr. Abra Jeffers

## A4. Nature Reviews Cardiology (2025) — Comprehensive Review of Cannabis CV Damage

### Study Details

- **Title:** "The relationship between cannabis and cardiovascular disease: clearing the haze"
- **Authors:** Mark Chandy (Western University), Nerea Jiménez Téllez (Stanford), Joseph C. Wu (Stanford)
- **Published:** January 23, 2025 in *Nature Reviews Cardiology*, 22(Suppl. 1)
- **DOI:** 10.1038/s41569-025-01121-6
- **URLs:**
  - Nature: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41569-025-01121-6
  - PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39849111/
  - ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/388322713

### Key Points from the Review

- Cannabinoids adversely affect the cardiovascular system, causing:
  - **Myocardial infarction**
  - **Cerebrovascular accidents (stroke)**
  - **Arrhythmia**
  - **Heart failure / cardiomyopathy**
- Cannabis, via **cannabinoid receptor 1 (CB1)-mediated oxidative stress and inflammation**, is linked to these adverse outcomes
- The link between cannabinoids and CV disease has moved from limited preclinical/case report evidence to now being **evident in epidemiological studies**
- **Synthetic cannabinoids** have the potential to cause even more substantial harm than traditional cannabinoids
- The effects of novel cannabinoids are unknown

### Key Points on Mechanisms

- CB1 antagonists and CB2 agonists are promising novel treatments for cardiovascular risk factors
- The endocannabinoid system regulates mood, satiety, and memory, **and modulates the cardiovascular system**

## A5. Route of Administration: Does It Matter?

### The UCSF CANDIDE Finding — The Bombshell

The CANDIDE study is the first to demonstrate that **the route of administration does NOT protect against cardiovascular damage**:

- Both **smoked cannabis** (FMD: 6.0%) and **THC edibles** (FMD: 4.6%) produced endothelial dysfunction comparable to tobacco smokers
- This directly challenges the longstanding clinical assumption that edibles avoid the cardiovascular harms of smoking

### Different Mechanisms, Same Outcome

- For **smokers**: Both FMD reduction AND reduced nitric oxide production were observed — consistent with smoke/particulate-mediated damage
- For **edible users**: FMD was reduced but nitric oxide production was NOT affected — suggesting THC itself (via systemic absorption) is the culprit through a **different biological pathway**
- This means it's not just the smoke — **the cannabinoid compound itself damages blood vessels**

### Stanton Glantz Commentary (May 2025)

> "Because most people smoked cannabis in [the] US population at the time, we could not distinguish the effects of inhaled cannabis from other forms of consumption, particularly edibles. [This new] paper shows that both smoked cannabis and edibles adversely affect blood vessel function which predicts later heart disease and heart attacks."
- Source: https://profglantz.com/2025/05/29/smoked-and-edible-cannabis-both-inhibit-normal-blood-vessel-function-in-people/

### Vaping — Emerging Evidence

- A 2025 *Heart Rhythm O2* review on arrhythmias and cannabis noted that approximately 219 million people use cannabis globally, with increasing consumption via vaping devices
- Vaping cannabis delivers THC systemically (similar to smoking) and is expected to carry similar cardiovascular risks, though dedicated studies comparing vaping-specific outcomes are still limited
- Source: https://scholar.rochesterregional.org/rrhpubs/2758/ (Paulraj et al., Heart Rhythm O2, 2025)

## A6. Mechanisms: How Cannabinoids Damage the Cardiovascular System

### CB1 Receptor-Mediated Vascular Inflammation (Stanford, *Cell*, 2022)

- **Title:** "Cannabinoid receptor 1 antagonist genistein attenuates marijuana-induced vascular inflammation"
- **Published in:** *Cell*, April 2022 (185(10):1676–1693)
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.cell.2022.04.005
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9400797/

Key findings from the Stanford iPSC study:
- Δ9-THC causes **cytotoxicity in endothelial cells** and is associated with oxidative stress
- THC causes **inflammation in human iPSC-derived endothelial cells**, including TNF-α release and monocyte adhesion
- THC acts through the **CB1 receptor**; the CB1 antagonist genistein was found to attenuate marijuana-induced vascular inflammation
- Analysis of the UK Biobank dataset confirmed the relationship between cannabis use and cardiovascular events in a large human cohort

### Nature Reviews Cardiology Summary of Mechanisms (Chandy, Wu et al., 2025)

From the comprehensive review:
1. **CB1 receptor activation → oxidative stress → endothelial damage**: THC activates CB1 receptors on endothelial cells, leading to reactive oxygen species production and inflammation
2. **Inflammation cascade**: TNF-α release, monocyte adhesion to vessel walls, promoting atherosclerosis
3. **Arrhythmias**: THC can alter cardiac conduction pathways; cannabis use is associated with atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, and other rhythm disturbances
4. **Sympathetic activation**: Cannabis acutely increases heart rate (tachycardia) and can raise blood pressure, increasing myocardial oxygen demand
5. **Vasospasm**: THC may trigger coronary artery vasospasm, reducing blood flow to heart tissue
6. **Platelet activation**: Some evidence suggests cannabinoids may increase platelet aggregation, raising thrombotic risk

### Endocannabinoid System Overview

- The endocannabinoid system (ECS) has two main receptors: **CB1** (predominantly in brain and cardiovascular system) and **CB2** (predominantly in immune cells)
- CB1 activation in the heart and blood vessels → generally harmful effects (vasoconstriction, inflammation, oxidative stress)
- CB2 activation → generally protective/anti-inflammatory effects
- THC is a partial agonist at both CB1 and CB2, but its cardiovascular harm is primarily CB1-mediated
- Sources:
  - PMC review: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2791499/ (Pacher, 2009)
  - J Pharmacol Exp Ther: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10658922/ (Rorabaugh, 2023)

### JACC: Advances Multicenter Retrospective Study (2025)

- **Title:** "Myocardial Infarction and Cardiovascular Risks Associated With Cannabis Use"
- **Published:** May 2025, *JACC: Advances*, Volume 4, Issue 5
- Multicenter retrospective study confirming associations between cannabis use, MI, heart failure, ventricular arrhythmias, and stroke
- URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2772963X25001152

## B1. THC Potency Trends: From ~4% to 15–25%+

### The Frontiers in Public Health 10-Year Trend Study (2024)

- **Title:** "A 10-year trend in cannabis potency (2013–2022) in different geographical regions of the United States of America"
- **Authors:** Mahmoud A. ElSohly, Chandrani G. Majumdar, Suman Chandra, Mohammed M. Radwan (University of Mississippi / NIDA)
- **Published:** October 3, 2024 in *Frontiers in Public Health*, Volume 12
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fpubh.2024.1442522
- **URLs:**
  - Frontiers: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/public-health/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2024.1442522/full
  - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11484017/
  - ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/384601836

### Key Data Points

- Cannabis has been analyzed by ElSohly's lab at the University of Mississippi under a NIDA contract for decades
- **1990s:** Average THC content in herbal cannabis was **~4%** or less
- **By 2022:** Average THC had **quadrupled** — reaching approximately **15–16%** in seized samples
- State-legal dispensary products routinely test **20–25%+ THC**
- The trend has been **consistently upward** over the past three decades, with an acceleration in the 2010s–2020s
- Geographic variation: potency trends varied by US region, but the overall upward trajectory was universal

### Boston University / NIDA Summary (May 2025)

> "Whereas in the 1990s, cannabis averaged less than 4 percent THC — the main psychoactive ingredient of the cannabis plant — that level quadrupled by 2022, according to the National Institute on Drug Abuse."
- Source: https://www.bu.edu/articles/2025/thc-content-in-cannabis-has-surged/

### Earlier ElSohly Review (2021, *Biological Psychiatry: Cognitive Neuroscience and Neuroimaging*)

- **Title:** "A Comprehensive Review of Cannabis Potency in the United States in the Last Decade"
- Published June 2021, Volume 6, Issue 6, Pages 603-606
- DOI: 10.1016/j.bpsc.2020.12.016
- Examined DEA-seized cannabis samples; confirmed sustained potency increase
- URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2451902221000227

### THC:CBD Ratio Shift

- Not only has THC increased, but **CBD levels have declined** in modern cannabis
- In the 1990s, the THC:CBD ratio was roughly 14:1
- By 2017, it was approximately **80:1** in some markets
- This means users get more intoxication and less of the potentially protective/moderating effects of CBD

## B2. Concentrates and Dabs: 60–90%+ THC

### What Are Concentrates?

- Cannabis concentrates (dabs, wax, shatter, budder, honeycomb, BHO) are highly concentrated extracts containing **60–90%+ THC**
- Traditional marijuana flower typically contains 10–30% THC
- Concentrates are created through extraction processes, often using volatile solvents like butane (butane hash oil / BHO)
- Consumed by heating on specialized equipment ("dab rigs") and inhaling the resulting vapor

### Potency Categories (from Am J Psychiatry Systematic Review)

The Lake et al. (2025) systematic review in *American Journal of Psychiatry* developed exposure-comparison categories:
- **1–9% THC** (historical/low-grade)
- **10–19% THC** (standard modern flower)
- **20–30% THC** (high-grade flower)
- **Kief/resin: ~30–50% THC**
- **Concentrates: ≥60% THC**

### Health Risks Specific to Concentrates

- **Immediate and intense psychoactive effects** — massive THC doses delivered instantaneously
- Short-term effects: anxiety, paranoia, nausea, dizziness, increased heart rate, hallucinations, impaired thinking
- Long-term effects: memory loss, cognitive impairment, structural brain changes, decreased motivation, increased risk of dependency/addiction
- **Respiratory risks**: inhaling hot vapor plus potential exposure to residual solvents or contaminants
- **Burns and injuries** from handling extremely hot equipment
- Sources:
  - DEA/Get Smart About Drugs: https://www.getsmartaboutdrugs.gov/news-statistics/2025/01/16/many-risks-cannabis-and-high-dose-thc
  - The Cabin Chiang Mai review: https://www.thecabinchiangmai.com/marijuana-dabbing-the-dangers-and-effects/
  - Partnership to End Addiction: https://drugfree.org/article/what-is-dabbing/

## B3. King's College London Epigenetics Study (October 2024) — High-Potency Cannabis Leaves DNA Signatures

### Study Details

- **Title:** "Methylomic signature of current cannabis use in two first-episode psychosis cohorts"
- **Lead Authors:** Emma L. Dempster (University of Exeter), Chloe C. Y. Wong, Marta Di Forti (King's College London)
- **Published:** October 16, 2024 in *Molecular Psychiatry*
- **DOI:** 10.1038/s41380-024-02689-0
- **URLs:**
  - Nature/Molecular Psychiatry: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-024-02689-0
  - King's College London press release: https://www.kcl.ac.uk/news/first-study-to-show-high-potency-cannabis-use-leaves-unique-signature-on-dna
  - ScienceDaily: https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2024/10/241016120649.htm
  - Medical Xpress: https://medicalxpress.com/news/2024-10-high-potency-cannabis-epigenetic.html

### Key Findings

- **First study** to show that high-potency cannabis (≥10% THC) leaves a **distinct mark on DNA** through epigenetic changes (DNA methylation)
- The study examined DNA methylation patterns in two independent first-episode psychosis cohorts
- **Critical finding:** The effect of cannabis on DNA was **different** in people experiencing their first episode of psychosis compared to cannabis users who had never experienced psychosis
- This suggests potential for **DNA blood tests to identify cannabis users at risk of developing psychosis** — informing preventative approaches
- The epigenetic changes were specific to **current** high-potency cannabis use (not just any cannabis use)
- Funded by the Medical Research Council, National Institute for Health and Care Research (NIHR)

### Key Quote

> "This is the first study to suggest that the use of high potency cannabis leaves a distinct mark on DNA, providing valuable insights into the biological impact of cannabis use." — King's College London press release

### Implications

- Provides a potential **biomarker** for identifying at-risk cannabis users before psychosis onset
- Demonstrates that cannabis effects are not purely transient — they can leave lasting biological signatures
- Distinguishes between low-potency and high-potency effects at the molecular level

## B4. How Higher Potency Changes the Risk Profile

### Psychosis Risk — The Di Forti EU-GEI Study (*Lancet Psychiatry*, 2019)

- **Title:** "The contribution of cannabis use to variation in the incidence of psychotic disorder across Europe (EU-GEI): a multicentre case-control study"
- **Published:** March 2019, *The Lancet Psychiatry*, Volume 6, Issue 5, Pages 427–436
- **URL:** https://www.thelancet.com/article/S2215-0366(19)30048-3/fulltext
- **Key finding:** Daily use of **high-potency cannabis** (≥10% THC) was associated with **roughly five-fold increased odds** of psychotic disorder compared to never-users
- In cities where high-potency cannabis predominated (London, Amsterdam), the **population-attributable fraction** (cases that could be prevented by eliminating high-potency daily use) was estimated at **~30% in London and ~50% in Amsterdam**

### The Lancet Psychiatry Systematic Review on Potency (2022)

- **Title:** "Association of cannabis potency with mental ill health and addiction: a systematic review"
- **Authors:** Kat Petrilli, Shelan Ofori, Lindsey Hines, Gemma Taylor, Sally Adams, Tom P. Freeman
- **Published:** July 25, 2022 in *The Lancet Psychiatry*, Volume 9, Issue 9, Pages 736–750
- **DOI:** 10.1016/S2215-0366(22)00161-4
- **URLs:**
  - Lancet Psychiatry: https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(22)00161-4/abstract
  - Full text PDF: https://smilodon-elephant-ygzl.squarespace.com/s/Lancet-meta-analysis.pdf

**Key Findings:**
- First systematic review of cannabis potency and health outcomes
- 20 eligible studies from 4,171 screened articles
- **Higher-potency cannabis** (vs. lower-potency) was associated with **increased risk of psychosis and cannabis use disorder (CUD)**
- Evidence **varied** for depression and anxiety
- Calls for standardization of exposure measures and longitudinal designs

### Adolescent Cannabis Use — New JAMA Health Forum Study (Feb 2026)

- **Title:** New Kaiser Permanente Northern California study
- **Published:** February 2026 in *JAMA Health Forum*
- **N = 463,598 adolescents** aged 13–17
- **Key finding:** Past-year cannabis use was associated with **more than double the risk** of psychotic disorders (adjusted HR 2.19) and bipolar disorder in early adulthood
- Also increased risks for depressive and anxiety disorders
- Sources:
  - Medscape: https://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/teen-cannabis-use-tied-increase-serious-mental-illness-2026a10005qn
  - Leafie: https://www.leafie.co.uk/news/cannabis-use-adolescence-linked-risk-psychotic-bipolar-disorders/

### Addiction / Cannabis Use Disorder

- From Murray et al. (2016), *World Psychiatry*: "Traditional marijuana, high-potency cannabis and synthetic cannabinoids: increasing risk for psychosis"
- Higher potency → faster development of tolerance and dependence
- Users of high-potency products may need to consume more to achieve desired effects, accelerating the trajectory toward cannabis use disorder
- URL: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/wps.20341

## B5. American Journal of Psychiatry Systematic Review (2025)

### Study Details

- **Title:** "High-Potency Cannabis Use and Health: A Systematic Review of Observational and Experimental Studies"
- **Authors:** Stephanie Lake, PhD; Conor H. Murray, PhD; Brittany Henry; Liza Strong; et al. (UCLA Center for Cannabis and Cannabinoids)
- **Published:** March 26, 2025 in *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 182(7):616–638
- **DOI:** 10.1176/appi.ajp.20240269
- **URLs:**
  - PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40134269/
  - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12549548/
  - ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/390203854

### Key Methods

- Systematic search of 5 biomedical databases
- Developed **ecologically relevant potency exposure-comparison categories**: 1–9%, 10–19%, 20–30%, kief/resin (~30–50%), concentrates (≥60%)
- Quality assessed using GRADE framework
- 4,545 records screened → **42 eligible studies**
- Two reviewers independently conducted screening, extraction, and quality assessment

### Key Findings

- Most studies addressed outcomes in **mental health**, **"problem" cannabis use**, and **other substance use** domains
- Findings in the "problem cannabis use" domain (CUD) showed the **strongest and most consistent** evidence of harm from high-potency products
- Evidence for mental health outcomes (psychosis, anxiety, depression) was **suggestive but of lower certainty** due to methodological limitations
- The review highlighted a **critical gap** in research on cardiovascular and physical health effects of high-potency products specifically
- Called for better standardization of how "potency" is defined and measured across studies

- **Published:** September 2024 by the National Academies Press

### Colorado — Policy Approaches to High-Potency THC (2024)

- **Title:** "Policy Approaches to High Concentration Cannabis and THC Concentrates"
- **Authors:** Cannabis Research & Policy Project Team, Colorado School of Public Health, University of Colorado Anschutz Medical Campus
- **Published:** March 27, 2024
- **URL:** https://coloradosph.cuanschutz.edu/docs/librariesprovider151/default-document-library/policy-approaches-to-high-concentration-cannabis-and-thc-concentrates---march-27-2024.pdf

Key context:
- House Bill 21-1317 mandated the Colorado School of Public Health to review evidence on health effects of high-potency marijuana and THC concentrates
- Motivated by concern that changes in the cannabis marketplace would lead to exposures to **potentially risky doses of THC**, particularly for youth and young adults
- Colorado considered but has not yet implemented specific concentration limits

### Vermont — THC Cap Debates

- Vermont has a **statutory cap of 30% THC** on cannabis flower
- The Vermont Medical Society recommended banning products >15% THC — the Cannabis Control Board rejected this
- Vermont health officials warned against lifting potency caps, citing data from Youth Risk Behavior Survey connecting higher-potency concentrates with adverse effects on developing adolescent minds
- In 2024, the Department of Health advised against a bill to eliminate THC caps on flower and concentrates

> "The council had serious concerns about [lifting caps], of the adverse effects that high-potency cannabis products can have." — Kelly Dougherty, Deputy Commissioner, Vermont Department of Health

- Sources:
  - Vermont CCB: https://ccb.vermont.gov/response-15-thc-cap-recommendation
  - WCAX: https://www.wcax.com/2024/01/24/vt-health-officials-tell-lawmakers-reject-cannabis-potency-proposal/

### The Policy Tension

The fundamental tension in potency regulation:
1. **Public health argument FOR caps:** Higher potency = higher risk for psychosis, CUD, cardiovascular damage, and adolescent harm. Limiting potency reduces the maximum potential harm per dose.
2. **Market argument AGAINST caps:** Setting caps too low pushes consumers to the illegal market, which has no quality controls, testing, or consumer protections. Vermont's CCB specifically cited this: "the stated purpose of creating Vermont's adult-use cannabis marketplace is to move as much of the illegal cannabis market as possible into the regulated market."
3. **The gap:** No jurisdiction has implemented a potency cap below ~15% for flower or below ~60% for concentrates, partly because the evidence base — while growing — has been considered insufficient to justify such aggressive regulation.

- **URL:** https://www.eatg.org/hiv-news/cannabis-is-interfering-with-the-heart/
- Summarized the Chandy/Wu review: "A large-scale US study from 2024 relied on survey data from more than 430,000 respondents and found the 4% of respondents who reported using cannabis daily had a 49% increased risk for myocardial infarction and a twofold increased risk for stroke."

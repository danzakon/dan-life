# The Neuroscience Paradox — Cannabis Effects on the Brain Across the Lifespan

**Raw Research Findings — February 26, 2026**

---

## 1. The Adolescent/Developing Brain (Ages 13–25): Structural Damage

### Cortical Thinning and Dendritic Shrinkage

A landmark October 2024 study from the Université de Montréal, led by **Graciela Piñeyro** and **Tomáš Paus** (researchers at CHU Sainte-Justine), demonstrated that THC causes **shrinkage of dendritic arborization** — neurons' "network of antennae" critical for communication between neurons. This results in **atrophy of certain regions of the cerebral cortex** during adolescence, when the brain is still maturing.

> "If we take the analogy of the brain as a computer, the neurons would be the central processor, receiving all information via the synapses through the dendritic network. So a decrease in the data input to the central processor by dendrites makes it harder for the brain to learn new things, interact with people, cope with new situations." — **Tomáš Paus**, professor of psychiatry and neuroscience, Université de Montréal

The study used a three-step approach:
1. Exposed adolescent male mice to THC and synthetic cannabinoid WIN 55,212-2; assessed differentially expressed genes (DEGs), spine numbers, and dendritic complexity in frontal cortex
2. In human male adolescents, examined cortical thickness differences (MRI) between those who used cannabis before age 16 (n=140) vs. non-users (n=327)
3. Found that 13 THC-related human homologs of DEGs correlated with cannabis-related variations in cortical thickness

The study found that THC modifies the expression of certain genes affecting the structure and function of synapses and dendrites, with coexpression patterns involving astrocytes, microglia, and a type of pyramidal cell enriched in dendrite-regulating genes.

- **Source**: Navarri et al., "Cells and Molecules Underpinning Cannabis-Related Variations in Cortical Thickness during Adolescence," *Journal of Neuroscience*, 2024
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39214708/
- **Press**: https://nouvelles.umontreal.ca/en/article/2024/10/23/cannabis-use-in-adolescence-visible-effects-on-brain-structure

### White Matter Integrity

A systematic review of diffusion-weighted MRI studies (Robinson et al., 2023, Frontiers in Neuroimaging) examining **1,457 cannabis users and 1,441 controls** synthesized evidence on white matter microstructure. Cannabis users showed disruptions in white matter tracts.

Longitudinal DTI data from Becker et al. found that cannabis users (onset before age 17) displayed **reduced longitudinal growth in fractional anisotropy (FA)** in:
- Central and parietal regions of the right and left superior longitudinal fasciculus
- White matter adjacent to the left superior frontal gyrus
- Left corticospinal tract
- Right anterior thalamic radiation lateral to the genu of the corpus callosum

Greater amounts of cannabis use correlated with reduced longitudinal growth in FA and impaired performance on verbal learning.

- **Source**: Robinson et al., "Measuring white matter microstructure in 1,457 cannabis users and 1,441 controls: A systematic review," *Frontiers in Neuroimaging*, 2023
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fnimg.2023.1129587/full
- **Source**: Becker et al., "Longitudinal changes in white matter microstructure after heavy cannabis use," *NeuroImage*, 2015
- **URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1878929315000948

### Scoping Review: Overall Brain Effects in Adolescence

A September 2025 scoping review (Nosko, Crocker & Tibbo, Frontiers in Psychiatry) summarized the state of evidence: cannabis use in adolescence and young adulthood is associated with effects on both brain structure and function, though the field is complicated by pre-existing differences that may predispose some individuals to both cannabis use and altered brain development.

- **Source**: Nosko et al., "Cannabis use in adolescence and young adulthood and its effects on brain structure and function: a scoping review," *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 2025
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1644105

### Sex-Moderated Cortical Signatures

A November 2025 study (Watts, Navarri & Conrod, Neuropsychopharmacology) found that cortical signatures of risk for adolescent cannabis use and consequences of use are **moderated by sex** — indicating distinct vulnerability patterns for male and female adolescents.

- **Source**: Watts et al., "Independent brain cortical signatures of risk for adolescent cannabis use and consequences of such use are moderated by sex," *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 2025
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41386-025-02249-2

### Adolescent vs. Young Adult Initiation

An ABCD/IMAGEN study (Albaugh et al., 2023, Molecular Psychiatry) showed **differential associations of adolescent versus young adult cannabis initiation with longitudinal brain change** — with earlier initiation associated with greater neurodevelopmental impact.

- **Source**: Albaugh et al., "Differential associations of adolescent versus young adult cannabis initiation with longitudinal brain change and behavior," *Molecular Psychiatry*, 2023
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-023-02148-2

## 2. The Surprising Finding: Moderate Cannabis Use in Older Adults (40–77) — Larger Brain Volumes

### CU Anschutz / UK Biobank Study (February 2026)

A new study from **CU Anschutz researchers**, published February 2026, used UK Biobank data from **26,000+ participants** aged 40–77 and found that cannabis use was **generally associated with larger volumes in several brain regions and better cognitive function**.

Key researcher **Anika Guha, PhD** (clinical psychologist and faculty research associate in the Department of Psychiatry at CU Anschutz):

> "More older adults are using cannabis. It's more widely available and is being used for different reasons than in younger folks — such as for sleep and chronic pain. Plus, people are living longer. We have to be asking, 'What are the long-term effects of cannabis use as we continue to age?'"

Guha said she was "surprised by the extent of the positive findings" but emphasized that cannabis use studies have unknown variables (constituents and potency of products were unknown).

**Key findings:**
- Cannabis usage in older adults was associated with **larger brain volumes**, particularly in regions critical for memory and learning such as the **hippocampus**
- **Moderate use** cases showed the strongest associations
- Long-term cannabis users demonstrated **faster processing speed** compared to non-users in the same age group
- The findings suggest a **potential neuroprotective effect in aging**, though the relationship is correlational, not causal

- **Source**: Guha et al., "Lifetime Cannabis Use Is Associated with Brain Volume and Cognitive Function in Middle-Aged and Older Adults," *PubMed*, 2026
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41379083/
- **Press**: https://news.cuanschutz.edu/news-stories/study-finds-cannabis-usage-in-middle-aged-and-older-adults-associated-with-larger-brain-volume-better-cognitive-function
- **Coverage**: https://neurosciencenews.com/cannabis-brain-volume-aging-30120/

### The BMJ Mental Health / Oxford Study: A Counterpoint

Importantly, an October 2024 study from **Oxford University** (Ishrat, Levey, Gelernter, Ebmeier & Topiwala) published in *BMJ Mental Health* examined **3,641 lifetime cannabis users (mean age 61.0 years) and 12,255 controls (mean age 64.5 years)** from UK Biobank — and found more concerning results:

- Cannabis users had **poorer white matter integrity** (lower fractional anisotropy and higher mean diffusivity in the genu of the corpus callosum)
- **Weaker resting-state functional connectivity** in brain regions underlying the default mode and central executive networks
- However, **Mendelian randomisation analyses found no support for causal relationships** — suggesting associations are "probably not causal in nature and might represent residual confounding"

> "Associations between lifetime cannabis use and brain structure and function in later life are probably not causal in nature and might represent residual confounding."

This is the **largest observational study of relationships between cannabis use and brain structure and function to date** and the first to use genetic data (Mendelian randomisation) to determine whether cannabis causes brain changes.

- **Source**: Ishrat et al., "Association between cannabis use and brain structure and function: an observational and Mendelian randomisation study," *BMJ Mental Health*, 2024
- **URL**: https://mentalhealth.bmj.com/content/27/1/e301065
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39477366/
- **Press**: https://www.psych.ox.ac.uk/news/brain-changes-seen-in-lifetime-cannabis-users-but-may-not-be-causal

### The Paradox Explained

Two UK Biobank studies reach seemingly contradictory conclusions:
- **CU Anschutz (Feb 2026)**: Larger brain volumes, better cognitive function in cannabis users aged 40–77
- **Oxford (Oct 2024)**: Poorer white matter integrity, weaker functional connectivity — but probably not causal

The paradox may be partially explained by:
1. **Different measures**: Gray matter volume vs. white matter integrity vs. functional connectivity are distinct metrics
2. **Confounding**: The Oxford team's Mendelian randomisation suggests shared genetic/environmental factors
3. **Survivorship bias**: Older cannabis users in population studies may represent a self-selected, healthier subset
4. **Endocannabinoid system dynamics in aging**: Declining ECS tone in older adults may mean exogenous cannabinoids serve a different function

## 3. THC vs. CBD: Different Effects on the Endocannabinoid System

### Core Pharmacological Differences

A comprehensive review by **Nephi Stella** (2023, *Neuron*) — "THC and CBD: Similarities and differences between siblings" — provides the definitive comparison:

**THC (Δ9-tetrahydrocannabinol):**
- **Partial agonist** at CB1 receptors (primary psychoactive mechanism)
- Directly binds to CB1 receptors concentrated in the brain (cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, cerebellum)
- Produces psychoactive effects: euphoria, altered perception, impaired memory
- Exhibits **biased signaling** at CB1R — different downstream effects depending on cellular context
- Also acts on CB2 receptors (immune/peripheral)
- Therapeutic: antiemetic, appetite stimulation, pain relief, muscle relaxation

**CBD (Cannabidiol):**
- **Does NOT directly activate CB1 or CB2 receptors** at physiologically relevant concentrations
- Acts as a **negative allosteric modulator** of CB1 — subtly altering how THC and endocannabinoids bind
- Modulates multiple non-cannabinoid targets: 5-HT1A (serotonin) receptors, TRPV1 channels, GPR55, PPARγ
- No psychoactive effects
- May **counteract** some effects of THC (especially anxiety, paranoia)
- Therapeutic: anticonvulsant (FDA-approved Epidiolex), anti-inflammatory, anxiolytic

> "Both THC and CBD exhibit promising therapeutic properties; however, impairment of brain functions by THC and adverse effects of CBD limit their medical utility." — Stella, 2023

- **Source**: Stella, "THC and CBD: Similarities and differences between siblings," *Neuron*, 2023
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9898277/
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2022.12.022

### The Endocannabinoid System (ECS) Overview

The ECS consists of:
- **Endocannabinoids**: Anandamide (AEA) and 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG) — the body's own cannabinoids
- **Receptors**: CB1 (primarily CNS — densest in hippocampus, cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum) and CB2 (primarily immune/peripheral, but also in brain)
- **Enzymes**: FAAH (breaks down AEA) and MAGL (breaks down 2-AG)

Italian scientist Vincenzo DiMarzo described the ECS role as helping us "eat, sleep, relax, protect, and forget."

THC mimics endocannabinoids but with far greater potency and duration, flooding CB1 receptors. With chronic heavy use, CB1 receptors **downregulate** (decrease in number/sensitivity) — a key mechanism of tolerance.

- **Source**: Zou & Kumar, "Cannabinoid Receptors and the Endocannabinoid System: Signaling and Function in the Central Nervous System," *Int J Mol Sci*, 2018
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5877694/

### CBD and Brain Function

A January 2024 review (Schouten et al., Frontiers in Pharmacology) on "Cannabidiol and brain function: current knowledge and future perspectives" notes that CBD has gained attention for potential health benefits including anxiolytic, antipsychotic, and neuroprotective properties — but its mechanisms are complex and not fully understood.

- **Source**: Schouten et al., "Cannabidiol and brain function: current knowledge and future perspectives," *Frontiers in Pharmacology*, 2024
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pharmacology/articles/10.3389/fphar.2023.1328885/full

## 4. Working Memory: The Largest Study Ever (CU Anschutz)

### JAMA Network Open Study (January 2025)

The **largest brain imaging study ever done on cannabis and brain function** was published in *JAMA Network Open* in January 2025 by CU Anschutz researchers (lead author: **Joshua Gowin, PhD**).

**Study details:**
- **1,000+ young adults** aged 22–36 from the Human Connectome Project
- Used brain imaging technology (fMRI) during cognitive tasks
- Data collected between August 2012 and 2015; analyzed January–July 2024

**Key findings:**
- **63% of heavy lifetime cannabis users** (>1,000 uses) exhibited **reduced brain activity during a working memory task**
- **68% of recent users** (positive urine toxicology) also demonstrated similar reduced activity
- The decline in brain activity was associated with **worse performance on working memory** — the ability to retain and use information to perform tasks
- Reduced activity was concentrated in the **prefrontal cortex**, the center for working memory and executive control

**Usage definitions:**
- **Heavy**: >1,000 lifetime uses
- **Moderate**: 10–999 lifetime uses
- **Non-user**: <10 lifetime uses

> "Working memory allows a person to follow instructions they've just been given or to mentally visualize and manipulate information, like solving a math problem."

The study notes that while heavy use was clearly associated with deficits, the picture for **moderate users** was less clear-cut.

- **Source**: Gowin et al., "Brain Function Outcomes of Recent and Lifetime Cannabis Use," *JAMA Network Open*, 2025
- **URL**: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2829657
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39874032/
- **Press**: https://news.cuanschutz.edu/news-stories/largest-study-ever-done-on-cannabis-and-brain-function-finds-impact-on-working-memory
- **Coverage**: https://neurosciencenews.com/cannabis-working-memory-neuroscience-30126/

## 5. The JAMA Health Forum Study (February 2026): Adolescent Cannabis Doubles Psychosis/Bipolar Risk

### Study Details

Published **February 20, 2026** in *JAMA Health Forum*. Conducted by researchers from **Kaiser Permanente**, the **Public Health Institute**, **UCSF**, and **USC**. Funded by NIH's National Institute on Drug Abuse (R01DA0531920).

**Sample**: **463,396 adolescents** ages 13–17 from Kaiser Permanente Northern California, followed through age 26.

**Method**: Analyzed electronic health record data from routine pediatric visits (2016–2023). Used the **Teen Well-Check Questionnaire (TWCQ)** to identify past-year cannabis use. Critically, the study **excluded any teen who already had mental health symptoms before using cannabis** — addressing the "chicken-and-egg" problem.

**Lead author**: **Kelly Young-Wolff, PhD** (Kaiser Permanente Division of Research)

> "We designed the study to follow adolescents into young adulthood so we could better understand the timing of cannabis use relative to the onset of mental health disorders. Our findings show that adolescents who report cannabis use have a significantly higher risk of developing multiple psychiatric disorders, particularly psychotic and bipolar disorders."

**Key findings:**
- **Psychotic disorders**: Adjusted hazard ratio (aHR) **2.19** — more than double the risk
- **Bipolar disorder**: Approximately **double** the risk (aHR ~2.0)
- **Depressive disorders**: Risk rose ~33%
- **Anxiety disorders**: Risk rose ~25%
- Cannabis use typically occurred **1.7 to 2.3 years before** a psychiatric diagnosis
- **The younger the user, the steeper the risk increase**
- More than **10% of 12- to 17-year-olds** in the U.S. have used cannabis within the past year
- By senior year of high school, ~**26%** of U.S. teenagers have tried it

**Expert reactions:**

> "Very, very, very worrying." — **Dr. Ryan Sultan**, Columbia University psychiatrist (not part of the study)

> "As cannabis becomes more potent and aggressively marketed, this study indicates that adolescent cannabis use is associated with double the risk of incident psychotic and bipolar disorders, two of the most serious mental health conditions." — **Dr. Lynn Silver**, Public Health Institute

Silver noted that **schizophrenia costs American society an estimated $350 billion annually**, making prevention of cannabis-related psychosis a significant public health priority.

The study authors called for:
- More prevention efforts and policy strategies
- Further research with nuanced measurement (frequency, mode of use, product strength)

**Caveat**: The study could not definitively determine whether cannabis use *causes* psychiatric disorders — or whether teens use cannabis to address emerging mental health symptoms that haven't yet been diagnosed.

- **Source**: Young-Wolff et al., "Adolescent Cannabis Use and Incident Psychiatric Disorders," *JAMA Health Forum*, February 20, 2026
- **Kaiser Permanente coverage**: https://divisionofresearch.kaiserpermanente.org/teen-cannabis-use-mental-health/
- **PHI Press Release**: https://www.phi.org/press/study-adolescent-cannabis-use-linked-to-doubling-risk-of-psychotic-and-bipolar-disorders/
- **NPR Coverage**: https://www.npr.org/2026/02/21/nx-s1-5719338/cannabis-marijuana-weed-teens-psychosis-jama
- **News-Medical**: https://www.news-medical.net/news/20260220/Cannabis-use-in-adolescence-associated-with-higher-rates-of-psychiatric-disorders.aspx
- **Medscape**: https://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/teen-cannabis-use-tied-increase-serious-mental-illness-2026a10005qn

## 6. Brain Connectivity and Resting-State Networks

### Cannabis Users Show Altered Connectivity

A study by Amico et al. (2022, *Scientific Reports*) using ultra-high field (7T) resting-state scans of cannabis users (N=26) found:

- **Chronic cannabis users** showed a broad state of **hyperconnectivity** within the entire range of major brain networks compared to occasional users
- This may reflect **adaptive network reorganization** following prolonged cannabis exposure
- Acute cannabis intoxication also altered functional connectomes

- **Source**: Amico et al., "Functional brain connectomes reflect acute and chronic cannabis use," *Scientific Reports*, 2022
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-06509-9

### Adolescent vs. Adult Connectivity Differences

A study by Ertl et al. (2023, published in *Biological Psychiatry*) examined **resting-state functional connectivity** in matched groups of regular cannabis users (N=70: 35 adolescents aged 16–17, 35 adults aged 26–29) vs. controls (N=70):

- Regular cannabis users showed **localized increases in connectivity** in the **executive control network (ECN)**
- Adolescents generally showed **weaker connectivity than adults**, consistent with developmental effects
- All seven major networks examined (default mode, executive control, salience, hippocampal, three striatal) showed age-based connectivity differences

- **Source**: Ertl et al., "Associations between regular cannabis use and brain resting-state functional connectivity in adolescents and adults," *PubMed*, 2023
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37515469/

### Default Mode Network Disruption

Ritchay et al. (2021, *NeuroImage: Clinical*) found relationships between cannabis use, gender, and cognition in the **default mode network (DMN)** in adolescents and young adults. Cannabis is the most commonly used illicit substance, with nearly 1 in 4 young adults as current users.

- **Source**: Ritchay et al., "Resting state functional connectivity in the default mode network: Relationships between cannabis use, gender, and cognition," *NeuroImage: Clinical*, 2021
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33872994/

### Acute Effects: Different Cannabis Types in Young Adults vs. Adolescents

Ertl et al. (2024, *Neuropsychopharmacology*) examined **acute effects of different types of cannabis** on young adult and adolescent resting-state brain networks — finding that the type of cannabis (THC-dominant vs. CBD-containing) produces different acute network-level effects.

- **Source**: Ertl et al., "Acute effects of different types of cannabis on young adult and adolescent resting-state brain networks," *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 2024
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41386-024-01891-6

### Aging Brain Connectivity

Watson et al. (2022, *Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience*) examined cannabis use and resting-state functional connectivity in the aging brain, noting that older adults (65+) have sharply increased cannabis use, highlighting a need to understand effects in this age group. Pre-clinical evidence suggests potential neuroprotective effects of cannabinoids.

- **Source**: Watson et al., "Cannabis Use and Resting State Functional Connectivity in the Aging Brain," *Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience*, 2022
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/aging-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnagi.2022.804890/full

### Structural and Functional Connectivity Combined

A 2023 study (Scientific Reports) measured alterations in both structural and functional connectivity in 73 cannabis users (age 22–36) vs. 73 controls from the Human Connectome Project. Significant differences were observed in:
- **Local structural/functional network measures** (degree and clustering coefficient)
- Prominent in the **insular and frontal opercular cortex** and **lateral/medial temporal cortex**
- Rich-club organization of structural networks showed normal trends but minor differences in **superior and inferior temporal gyri**

- **Source**: "Altered brain structural and functional connectivity in cannabis users," *Scientific Reports*, 2023
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-32521-8

## 7. Reversibility: Are Cognitive Effects Permanent?

### The Meta-Analysis: Effects Largely Reversible After Abstinence

A meta-analysis by **Schreiner & Dunn (2012)** in *Experimental and Clinical Psychopharmacology* — one of the most cited papers on this topic — examined residual effects of cannabis use on neurocognitive performance after prolonged abstinence:

> Key conclusion: "Lasting neurocognitive changes or deficits as a result of use are frequently noted despite a lack of clarity in the scientific literature."

However, the weight of evidence suggests most effects resolve with sustained abstinence, with the exception of very early onset (<16) and heavy, prolonged use.

- **Source**: Schreiner & Dunn, "Residual effects of cannabis use on neurocognitive performance after prolonged abstinence: A meta-analysis," *Experimental and Clinical Psychopharmacology*, 2012
- **URL**: https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding?doi=10.1037%2Fa0029117

### CCSA Report: Recovery Timeline

The Canadian Centre on Substance Abuse (CCSA) produced a comprehensive report "Clearing the Smoke on Cannabis" which found:

- **Regular cannabis use** (weekly or more) is associated with **mild cognitive difficulties**, which are **typically not apparent following about one month of abstinence**
- **Heavy (daily) and long-term** cannabis use is related to **more noticeable cognitive impairment**
- Cannabis use **beginning prior to age 16 or 17** is one of the **strongest predictors of cognitive impairment** — and it's unclear whether impairment leads to early use or early use causes progressive decline
- Regular cannabis use is associated with **altered brain structure and function**, though causality is unclear
- Regular use is related to **alterations in the brain's natural reward pathways**

- **Source**: CCSA, "Clearing the Smoke on Cannabis: Regular Use and Cognitive Functioning," 2019
- **URL**: https://www.ccsa.ca/sites/default/files/2019-09/CCSA-Cannabis-Use-Cognitive-Effects-Report-2019-en.pdf

### Adolescent Abstinence Trial (December 2025)

A randomized clinical trial (Frontiers in Psychiatry, December 2025) examined neurocognitive outcomes in adolescents with and without four weeks of cannabis abstinence — the first such trial using contingency management to encourage abstinence in teens.

- **Source**: "Neurocognitive outcomes in adolescents with and without four weeks of cannabis abstinence: a randomized clinical trial," *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 2025
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1723633/full

### Two-Week Abstinence in Adolescents

An earlier study (PMC, 2020) found that **two weeks of monitored abstinence** showed some improvement in cognition in adolescent and young adult cannabis users, but full recovery may require longer periods.

- **Source**: "Impact of Two-Weeks of Monitored Abstinence on Cognition in Adolescent and Young Adult Cannabis Users," *J Int Neuropsychol Soc*, 2020
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7483189/

### Multiple Sclerosis + Cannabis Abstinence

A 2024 study in *NeuroImage: Clinical* found that in MS patients, **28 days of cannabis abstinence** led to significant cognitive improvements accompanied by task-specific changes in brain activation, and that cannabis-related cognitive impairment may disrupt the **default mode network**.

- **Source**: Meza et al., "The effects of cannabis abstinence on cognition and resting state network activity in people with multiple sclerosis," *NeuroImage: Clinical*, 2024
- **URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2213158224000615

### India Rehabilitation Study

A case-control follow-up study from a rehabilitation center in Andhra Pradesh found that cannabis abstinence led to improved cognitive functioning in young people, supporting the reversibility thesis.

- **Source**: Goud et al., "Cannabis use and effect of cannabis abstinence on cognitive functioning in young people," *Middle East Current Psychiatry*, 2022
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43045-022-00228-7

## 8. Dose-Response: Frequency, Potency, and the Escalation Problem

### THC Potency Has Quadrupled

THC levels in cannabis have increased dramatically:
- **1960s–1970s**: Less than **2%** THC
- **1990s**: ~**4%** THC average
- **2014**: ~**12%** THC (ElSohly et al., *Biological Psychiatry*, 2016)
- **2020s**: Average potency **17–28%** THC, with highest levels around **37%**
- **Dabs and concentrates**: Can reach nearly **100%** THC

> "The trend toward increasing potency of THC is definitely an issue and is well-documented." — **Matthew Lally**, staff psychiatrist, Boston University Student Health Services

- **Source**: ElSohly et al., "Changes in Cannabis Potency over the Last Two Decades (1995-2014)," *Biological Psychiatry*, 2016
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4987131/
- **Updated data**: ElSohly et al., "A 10-year trend in cannabis potency (2013–2022) in different geographical regions of the United States," *Frontiers in Public Health*, 2024
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/public-health/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2024.1442522/full
- **BU coverage**: https://www.bu.edu/articles/2025/thc-content-in-cannabis-has-surged/

### Heavy Use Definitions and Thresholds

From the CU Anschutz working memory study:
- **Heavy**: >1,000 lifetime uses (~3+ years of daily use)
- **Moderate**: 10–999 lifetime uses
- **Non-user**: <10 lifetime uses

Heavy use was consistently associated with the most pronounced deficits. **63% of heavy users** showed reduced brain activity during working memory tasks vs. similar rates in recent users regardless of lifetime total.

### The CCSA Findings on Frequency

Key thresholds from the Canadian report:
- **Weekly or more frequent** use over months to years = "regular use" → mild cognitive difficulties
- **Daily use** = most noticeable cognitive impairment
- **Onset before age 16–17** = strongest predictor of lasting impairment
- Most cognitive effects **resolve after ~1 month of abstinence** for regular users
- Heavy/long-term users may require longer recovery

### Clinical Perspective on Potency

Addiction psychiatrist **Elizabeth Stuyt, MD** (University of Colorado) warned about high-potency THC:

> The increasing potency raises "serious concerns about health risks" — particularly for developing brains and those with predisposition to psychotic disorders.

- **Source**: Stuyt, "The Problem with the Current High Potency THC Marijuana from the Perspective of an Addiction Psychiatrist," *Missouri Medicine*, 2018
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6312155/

## 9. Bonus: The Endocannabinoid System and Aging — Why Cannabis Might Help Older Brains

### Age-Related ECS Decline

A January 2026 study in *Scientific Reports* (N=142 adults; younger 21–24, midlife 25–54, older 55–71) found:

- **Older adults exhibited lower baseline endocannabinoid levels** (AEA and DEA) than younger adults
- Acute cannabis use **increased** AEA, DEA, LEA, PEA, SEA, and OEA **across all age groups** (all p < .001)
- For AEA and DEA, **increases were larger in older adults** (significant Time×Age interaction)
- This suggests cannabis may help **restore declining endocannabinoid tone** in older adults

> "An age-related decline in endocannabinoid system (ECS) activity may contribute to conditions such as chronic pain and Alzheimer's disease."

- **Source**: "Age differences in endocannabinoid tone are ameliorated after recent cannabis use," *Scientific Reports*, 2026
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27618-1

### ECS in Alzheimer's Disease

A January 2026 review (Frontiers in Psychiatry) explored the role of the endocannabinoid system in managing neuropsychiatric symptoms in Alzheimer's disease:

- ECS influences **neuronal and glial communications, neurotransmitter regulation, neuroinflammation, and behavioral alterations**
- Up to **97% of AD patients** exhibit one or more neuropsychiatric symptoms
- Emerging evidence suggests ECS is both a **contributor to and a potential therapeutic target** for managing these symptoms

- **Source**: Rao et al., "The role of the endocannabinoid system in managing neuropsychiatric symptoms in Alzheimer's disease," *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 2026
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1709266/full

### Cannabis Use and Aging: Molecule to Mind

A comprehensive review (Yoo, DiMuzio & Filbey, 2020, *Journal of Dual Diagnosis*) — "Interaction of Cannabis Use and Aging: From Molecule to Mind" — synthesized evidence on how cannabis and aging interact at molecular, cellular, and systems levels.

- **Source**: Yoo et al., "Interaction of Cannabis Use and Aging: From Molecule to Mind," *J Dual Diagn*, 2020
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8177073/

## 10. The Clinical Paradox: Cannabis Use Disorder

From Margaret Haney's clinical perspective (*American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2022):

- A significant subset (**10%–30%**) of those using cannabis will develop **cannabis use disorder**
- Withdrawal symptoms include: sleep disruption, irritability, anxiety, anorexia, craving
- Cannabis use disorder is "both distressing to the individual and not easy to overcome"
- The consequences are "rarely as destructive to individual lives as other drugs of abuse," which may lead to perception that it's a minor concern

> "Societal attitudes toward cannabis, both as a drug of abuse and as a potential therapeutic, have undergone a marked shift in the past 25 years."

- **Source**: Haney, "Cannabis Use and the Endocannabinoid System: A Clinical Perspective," *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2022

## Summary: The Core Paradox

| Factor | Adolescent Brain (13–25) | Older Adult Brain (40–77) |
|--------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Gray matter** | Cortical thinning, reduced volume | Larger volumes (hippocampus, etc.) |
| **White matter** | Reduced integrity, impaired growth | Poorer integrity (Oxford), but complex |
| **Cognition** | Working memory deficits, processing speed impairment | Faster processing speed, better function |
| **Psychiatric risk** | 2x psychosis, 2x bipolar, ↑ depression/anxiety | Less studied; some evidence of benefit |
| **Mechanism** | Disrupts ongoing development during critical period | May compensate for declining ECS tone |
| **Reversibility** | Partially reversible; onset <16 may be lasting | N/A (usually ongoing use) |
| **Dose-response** | Earlier onset + higher frequency = worse outcomes | Moderate use shows strongest positive associations |

The neuroscience paradox is this: **the same substance that appears to damage the developing brain may offer neuroprotective benefits to the aging brain** — likely because the endocannabinoid system serves fundamentally different roles at different life stages. During adolescence, ECS signaling guides critical neurodevelopmental processes (synaptic pruning, myelination, cortical maturation). Flooding this system with exogenous THC disrupts these processes. In aging, ECS tone naturally declines, and exogenous cannabinoids may partially restore homeostatic function.

The critical unanswered question: **Is the neuroprotective association in older adults causal, or does it reflect confounding** (healthier people are more likely to use cannabis recreationally, survivorship bias, lifestyle factors)?

# Medical Benefits — Where Cannabis Science Actually Holds Up (and Where It Doesn't)

**Research compiled: February 26, 2026**

## 1. Chronic Pain

### The AHRQ Living Systematic Review (2025 Update)

The most comprehensive U.S. government review of cannabis for pain is the **AHRQ Living Systematic Review on Cannabis and Other Plant-Based Treatments for Chronic Pain**, Comparative Effectiveness Review No. 250. The fourth and final annual update was published **July 2025**, conducted by the Pacific Northwest Evidence-based Practice Center (Roger Chou, M.D. et al.).

**Key findings:**
- The review now encompasses **29 RCTs (in 30 publications) and 15 observational studies** — accumulated over four annual updates since October 2021
- Cannabis products are grouped by THC:CBD ratio: comparable THC to CBD, high THC to CBD (including THC only), and low THC to CBD (including CBD only)
- The 2025 update added 3 new RCTs and 3 new observational studies, including novel product types (topical hemp seed oil for knee osteoarthritis, topical THC:CBD:CBN for diabetic neuropathy, inhaled cannabis vs. oxycodone)
- Evidence remains largely **low to moderate quality** across most pain conditions
- Products with **high THC:CBD ratios (≥2:1)** were associated with **small improvements in pain** but came with medium-to-high risks for dizziness, nausea, and sedation
- Products with **low THC:CBD ratios (<1:2) did not improve pain** and still carried medium-to-high risks for adverse effects

> Source: [AHRQ Living Systematic Review — 2025 Update Executive Summary (PDF)](https://effectivehealthcare.ahrq.gov/sites/default/files/related_files/cer-250-cannabis-2025-surv-rep-update-executive-sum.pdf)
> Full report: [AHRQ CER-250 Full Report (PDF)](https://effectivehealthcare.ahrq.gov/sites/default/files/related_files/cer-250-cannabis-2025-surv-rep-update.pdf)
> PubMed: [PMID 40956916](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40956916/)

### American College of Physicians Best Practice Advice (2025)

Published in *Annals of Internal Medicine* on April 4, 2025, the ACP issued formal best practice advice on cannabis for chronic noncancer pain (Kansagara D et al.). Based primarily on 26 randomized trials and 12 observational studies, mostly focused on neuropathic pain:

**Key points:**
- High THC:CBD products (≥2:1): **small improvements in pain** with medium-to-high risks for dizziness, nausea, sedation
- Low THC:CBD products (<1:2): **did not improve pain** — still had side effects
- Potential long-term harms: **cannabis use disorder, cognition changes, cannabis hyperemesis syndrome, psychosis**
- **Best Practice Advice 1b** explicitly warns that harms likely outweigh benefits for: young adults/adolescents, patients with substance use disorders, patients with serious mental illness, and frail patients
- Clinicians should counsel patients about benefits AND harms; should **advise against use** during pregnancy or breastfeeding

> Source: Kansagara D et al., "Cannabis or Cannabinoids for the Management of Chronic Noncancer Pain: Best Practice Advice From the American College of Physicians," *Annals of Internal Medicine*, April 2025. [ACP Press Release](https://www.acponline.org/acp-newsroom/acps-best-practice-advice-addresses-use-of-cannabis-cannabinoids-for-chronic-noncancer-pain) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40183677/) | [JWATCH summary](https://www.jwatch.org/na58651/2025/04/17/cannabis-and-cannabinoids-chronic-noncancer-pain-guidance)

### University of Pittsburgh PAIN Journal Study (2025)

Published in the journal *PAIN* (January/July 2025), Wasan et al. from the University of Pittsburgh conducted an observational comparative effectiveness study — the type of study IASP identified as a key missing evidence type.

**Study details:**
- **440 patients** certified for medical marijuana by pain specialists in a single healthcare system
- Outcomes tracked via the University of Pittsburgh Patient Outcomes Repository for Treatment (PORT)
- At **3 months, 38.6% were responders**, based on clinically meaningful improvements in pain, function, or global impression of change — response maintained at 6 months
- Among **157 patients co-prescribed opioids**: mean **39.3% decrease in morphine milligram equivalents** at 6 months
- Logistic regression revealed an **odds ratio of 2.6 in favor of medical marijuana vs. medication treatment** (P < 0.01)
- **Important caveat**: This is an observational study, not an RCT. The populations compared were "slightly different"

> Source: Wasan AD et al., "The comparative effectiveness of medicinal cannabis for chronic pain versus prescription medication treatment," *PAIN*, 2025. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39878633/) | [Journal](https://journals.lww.com/pain/fulltext/2025/07000/the_comparative_effectiveness_of_medicinal.18.aspx) | [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/388496249)

### Evidence Quality Assessment

The chronic pain evidence is a mixed bag:
- **RCT evidence**: 29 trials available, but many are small, short-duration, use heterogeneous products, and show only **small effect sizes**
- **Observational evidence**: More encouraging (like the Pitt study), but subject to confounders and selection bias
- **The gap**: High THC products show modest benefit for pain; CBD-only products show essentially no benefit for pain
- **Neuropathic pain** has the strongest signal among pain subtypes
- **Bottom line**: Cannabis may help some chronic pain patients modestly, but the evidence is far from the certainty that public perception suggests

## 2. Epilepsy and Seizures — The Gold Standard

### Epidiolex (CBD) — The Strongest Cannabis-Derived Medical Evidence

Epidiolex (cannabidiol) is the **first and only FDA-approved prescription CBD**, approved June 25, 2018 for seizures associated with Lennox-Gastaut syndrome (LGS), Dravet syndrome, and later tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC) in patients 1 year of age and older.

**Clinical trial results (from the largest placebo-controlled clinical program for cannabidiol):**

**Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome (LGS):**
- **37% reduction** in monthly drop seizures at 10 mg/kg/day dose
- **42% reduction** in monthly drop seizures at 20 mg/kg/day dose
- vs. **17% reduction** in placebo group
- In a second study: **44% reduction** at 20 mg/kg/day vs. 22% reduction with placebo
- **Freedom from drop seizures**: 4-7% of EPIDIOLEX patients (vs. 0-1% placebo)
- **75%+ seizure reduction**: 3x more patients on EPIDIOLEX 10 mg/kg/day (11%) vs. placebo (3%)

**Dravet Syndrome:**
- The first pivotal Phase 3 trial showed a **significant reduction in convulsive seizures** vs. placebo (p=0.01)
- More than 500 people with LGS and Dravet syndrome participated across 3 clinical studies — the largest placebo-controlled clinical program for any cannabinoid

**Tuberous Sclerosis Complex (TSC):**
- Significant reduction in TSC-associated seizures vs. placebo
- The EpiCom trial (2025 interim results) also suggests improvements in **non-seizure outcomes** (behavioral)

**Commercial success as validation:**
- Epidiolex achieved **$1.1 billion in revenue** in 2025, with **9% year-over-year growth** (Jazz Pharmaceuticals Q4 2025 earnings)
- AES 2025 Annual Meeting: 8 abstracts including 4 late-breaking abstracts on Epidiolex

**Post hoc analysis (2025):**
- A post hoc analysis of Phase 3 trials published in *CNS Drugs* (August 2025) examined clinically meaningful reduction in drop seizures in LGS patients treated with cannabidiol

> Source: [Epidiolex Clinical Study Results](https://www.epidiolex.com/epidiolex-results) | [FDA Drug Trials Snapshot](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots-epidiolex) | [Jazz Pharmaceuticals AES 2025](https://investor.jazzpharma.com/news-releases/news-release-details/jazz-pharmaceuticals-present-extensive-new-data-and-real-world) | [Jazz Q4 2025 Earnings](https://www.prnewswire.co.uk/news-releases/jazz-pharmaceuticals-announces-full-year-and-fourth-quarter-2025-financial-results-and-provides-2026-financial-guidance-302696199.html) | PMC post hoc: [PMC12423233](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12423233/)

### Why This Is the Gold Standard

- **Multiple large, randomized, placebo-controlled trials** — the highest level of clinical evidence
- **FDA approval** through the standard drug approval process (not a state medical marijuana program)
- **Pharmaceutical-grade, standardized product** — not the variable dispensary products used for most other conditions
- **Replicated across multiple studies and indications**
- This is what cannabis medicine evidence *should* look like — and it's the exception, not the rule

## 3. Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting (CINV)

### FDA-Approved Synthetic Cannabinoids

Two synthetic cannabinoid medications are FDA-approved for CINV:
- **Dronabinol (Marinol/Syndros)**: Synthetic THC, approved for CINV and HIV/AIDS-related anorexia
- **Nabilone (Cesamet)**: Synthetic THC analog, approved for CINV after failure of first-line antiemetics

### Evidence Strength

**Cochrane Review (2015, still the benchmark):**
- 23 randomized controlled trials reviewed
- Cannabis-based medicines resulted in **fewer patients experiencing nausea and vomiting** compared to placebo
- Cannabis-based medicines were comparable to conventional antiemetics in efficacy
- But: **higher rates of adverse effects** including dizziness, dysphoria, euphoria, sedation, and "feeling high"

**Systematic Review — Kemmner et al. (2025):**
- Published in *Journal of Cancer Survivorship* (December 2025)
- Reviewed cannabinoids specifically for prevention of CINV in oncological therapy
- Over 144 cannabinoids identified in cannabis; THC is the primary antiemetic compound
- Cannabinoids exert antiemetic effect by **inhibiting 5-HT3 (serotonin) receptors** in the area postrema and activating CB1 receptors in the GI tract
- Evidence confirms prior findings: cannabinoids are effective for CINV, particularly as **rescue therapy after failure of standard antiemetics**

**Cannabis Evidence Project Summary (2023):**
- Cochrane and systematic reviews show cancer patients are more likely to have **complete absence of nausea or vomiting** with synthetic cannabinoids vs. placebo
- Nausea affects 75%+ of chemo patients; vomiting affects ~30%
- Despite advances in modern antiemetics (5-HT3 antagonists, NK1 antagonists, corticosteroids), CINV remains significantly distressing for many

**Key limitation**: Most evidence uses **synthetic, standardized pharmaceutical cannabinoids** (dronabinol, nabilone), not the plant-based or dispensary products that most medical cannabis users access. Extrapolating from pharma-grade synthetics to dispensary flower is a leap.

> Source: [Cochrane Review](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD009464_cannabis-based-medicine-nausea-and-vomiting-people-treated-chemotherapy-cancer) | [Kemmner et al. 2025, PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40797063/) | [Cannabis Evidence Project (PDF)](https://www.cannabisevidence.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/12/Cannabis-for-CINV-Dec-2023.pdf) | [StatPearls: Cannabinoid Antiemetic Therapy](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK535430/)

## 4. Mental Health Claims

### The Psychiatry Podcast — Episode 246 (August 2025)

"Cannabis and Depression, Anxiety, PTSD, Sleep, and Cognitive Function Update and Review" — David Puder, MD et al. A CME-accredited (1.25 credits) comprehensive review of the 2025 literature.

**Key framing from the episode:**
> "At a time when cannabis is not only legalized, but marketed as therapeutic, it is essential for healthcare professionals to be well-versed on what the research truly reveals about the reality of cannabis and its effect on psychiatric outcomes."

The episode describes a patient case: a man whose family encourages his cannabis use because without it he becomes "a different person — far more irritable and anxious and completely unable to function." The authors note: *"This is not a picture of a patient for whom cannabis is healing, but destructive"* — illustrating withdrawal/dependence masquerading as therapeutic benefit.

> Source: [Psychiatry Podcast Episode 246](https://www.psychiatrypodcast.com/psychiatry-psychotherapy-podcast/episode-246-cannabis-and-depression-anxiety-ptsd-sleep-and-cognitive-function-update-and-review) | [PDF companion](https://www.psychiatrypodcast.com/s/246_Puder_Cannabis-and-Depression-Anxiety-PTSD-Sleep-and-Cognitive-Function-Update-and-Review.pdf) | [Apple Podcasts](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/246-cannabis-and-depression-anxiety-ptsd-sleep/id1335892956?i=1000724085179)

### Depression

**Updated Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (Churchill et al., 2025):**
- Published in *Psychological Medicine* (February 2025)
- Found a significant association between cannabis use and depression
- This updates and reinforces earlier meta-analyses

**JAMA Psychiatry Meta-Analysis (Gobbi et al., 2019, still widely cited):**
- Adolescent cannabis use associated with increased risk of depression (OR ~1.37) and suicidality in young adulthood
- This remains one of the most-cited studies in the field

**Cannabis and Suicidality (Maffre Maviel et al., 2025):**
- Published in *Drug and Alcohol Dependence* (August 2025)
- Found a **significant association between cannabis use and suicidality, independent of depression**
- Existing research is inconsistent on whether the association differs between individuals with and without depression

**Bottom line on depression**: Cannabis does not treat depression. The weight of evidence suggests it is associated with **worsening depression outcomes**, particularly with heavy or chronic use, and especially in adolescents. The acute mood elevation is not therapeutic — it's followed by withdrawal-related mood deterioration.

> Sources: [Churchill et al. 2025, PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39936870/) | [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12055028/) | [Maffre Maviel et al. 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S037687162500167X)

### Anxiety

**Systematic Review — Blyth et al. (2025):**
- "A Systematic Review of Cannabis and Anxiety: Chicken or Egg?" published in *Current Addiction Reports* (April 2025)
- Reviewed studies from 2004-2024 examining cannabis use and anxiety
- Found **"promising potential for cannabis in the clinical treatment of anxiety"** — BUT also **"substantial evidence linked chronic cannabis use to the subsequent development of anxiety disorders"**
- Acute effects: cannabis can reduce anxiety in the short term
- Chronic effects: regular use is associated with **increased anxiety over time**
- Cannabis cessation can also worsen anxiety temporarily (withdrawal)

**Colorado Cannabis Users Health Cohort (Steeger et al., 2025):**
- Published in *Journal of Cannabis Research* (October 2025)
- Cross-sectional analysis with validated mental health scales AND biological cannabinoid markers
- Examined association between frequency of cannabis use and symptoms of anxiety/depression
- Cannabis is commonly used as a **self-prescribed treatment** for anxiety and depression, but the study raised questions about whether this is effective or counterproductive

**JAMA Health Forum (2026):**
- Study of **463,598 adolescents** aged 13-17
- Past-year cannabis use associated with **more than double the risk of psychotic disorders** (adjusted HR 2.19) and **bipolar disorder** by early adulthood
- Also identified increased risks for depressive and anxiety disorders

**Bottom line on anxiety**: The acute anxiolytic effect of cannabis is real but misleading. It creates a pattern where cannabis relieves the very anxiety that chronic cannabis use helped create — a self-perpetuating cycle that patients and families mistake for treatment.

> Sources: [Blyth et al. 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40429-025-00650-x) | [Steeger et al. 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s42238-025-00327-2) | [JAMA Health Forum 2026](https://www.leafie.co.uk/news/cannabis-use-adolescence-linked-risk-psychotic-bipolar-disorders/)

### PTSD

**RCT Evidence — Scoping Review (Cooling et al., 2025):**
- Published in *Clinical Drug Investigation* (December 2025)
- Scoped all RCT evidence on medicinal cannabis for mental health and substance use disorders
- Found that while emerging evidence supports cannabis for conditions like MS and epilepsy, **"its efficacy and safety profile for the treatment of mental health conditions remains controversial and under-explored"**
- Previous reviews found **inconclusive evidence** due to heterogeneity in study design and quality

**Project Twenty21 — Real-World Evidence (Lynskey et al., 2024):**
- Published in *BJPsych Open* (March 2024)
- One of the only real-world evidence studies on cannabis for PTSD with comorbid depression
- Showed improvements in PTSD symptoms, but this is **observational, uncontrolled** evidence

**Cannabis Use and Trauma-Focused Treatment (Hill et al., 2024):**
- Meta-analysis of individual patient data in *Journal of Anxiety Disorders* (March 2024)
- Found treatment outcomes for PTSD+SUD are **similar for those with and without cannabis use**
- Trauma-focused treatment outperforms non-trauma-focused treatment regardless of cannabis use
- Key finding: cannabis use doesn't necessarily interfere with PTSD treatment, but it also **doesn't replace evidence-based PTSD therapies**

**Bottom line on PTSD**: PTSD is one of the most common qualifying conditions for medical cannabis programs, but the RCT evidence remains thin. Observational data shows symptom improvements, but without controlled trials, it's impossible to separate cannabis effects from placebo, expectation, and natural symptom fluctuation.

> Sources: [Cooling et al. 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40261-025-01501-3) | [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12815972/) | [Lynskey et al. 2024, BJPsych Open](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/bjpsych-open/article/medicinal-cannabis-for-treating-posttraumatic-stress-disorder-and-comorbid-depression-realworld-evidence/F88B15F3E00576E757ADF4915F9874A6) | [Hill et al. 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0887618524000033)

## 5. Cancer Treatment Potential

### The 2026 MDPI Study — THC and CBD vs. Skin Cancer Cells

Published February 26, 2026 in *Antioxidants* (MDPI), this preclinical study found:

- **THC and CBD induce heme oxygenase-1 (HO-1)-dependent cell death** and trigger mitochondrial dysfunction in human melanoma and cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma cells
- Cannabinoids exert anticancer effects by **inhibiting tumor growth, proliferation, invasion, and angiogenesis**, and inducing cell death
- The enzyme HO-1 appears to be a **promising molecular target** linked to induction of apoptotic and ferroptotic cell death
- The authors suggest cannabinoids could potentially be used as **add-on therapy** to existing chemotherapy for skin cancers and potentially other tumor entities

**But the critical context**: This is all **in vitro** (cell lines in a lab). The molecular mechanisms of cannabinoid-induced cancer cell death have not been fully clarified. No human clinical trials exist for this application.

> Source: [MDPI Antioxidants, Feb 2026](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3921/15/3/286)

### Other Preclinical Cancer Research

**CBD and Glioblastoma (Baban et al., January 2026):**
- Published in *International Journal of Molecular Sciences*
- CBD investigated as a **prophylactic agent** against glioblastoma growth — preclinical only

**CBD Cytotoxicity in Cancer Cell Lines (Montes-de-Oca-Saucedo et al., December 2025):**
- CBD showed apoptotic cytotoxicity in HeLa (cervical), MDA-MB-231 (breast), and CaCo-2 (colon) cancer cell lines
- Non-cancerous cell lines (HaCaT, HUVEC) showed **less cytotoxicity** — suggesting some selectivity

**Cannabis Extract PHEC-66 and Melanoma (Bachari et al., January 2024):**
- Cannabis extract PHEC-66 showed anticancer effects on multiple melanoma cell lines
- Triggers apoptosis via multiple pathways including ROS generation and cell cycle arrest

**CBD and Pancreatic Cancer (Khaleghi et al., November 2025):**
- Published in *Cancer Cell International*
- Review of preclinical evidence for CBD in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC)
- Multiple mechanisms: ceramide accumulation, ER stress, GPR55 modulation
- Pancreatic tumor tissues express CB1 and CB2 receptors — a potential molecular target
- Still entirely preclinical

### How Far Are We From Actual Cancer Treatment?

**Very far.** The gap between "kills cancer cells in a petri dish" and "treats cancer in humans" is enormous:

1. **No human clinical trials** have demonstrated cannabinoid-based cancer treatment efficacy
2. Many substances kill cancer cells in vitro (bleach does too) — the challenge is doing it selectively, at tolerable doses, in living humans
3. The preclinical research is **promising for understanding mechanisms** but provides zero basis for clinical treatment claims
4. The most optimistic framing from researchers themselves is "add-on therapy" and "further research needed"
5. Patients who forgo conventional cancer treatment in favor of cannabis-based approaches are making a decision unsupported by evidence

## 6. Quality of Life

### PMC 2025 Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Published February 2026 in *Quality of Life Research* (Tait et al.):

**Study details:**
- "Health-related quality of life in patients receiving medicinal cannabis: systematic review and meta-analysis of primary research findings 2015–2025"
- Searched 7 databases (AMED, Medline, Web of Science, Scopus, Embase, Cinahl, PsycINFO) from January 2015 to April 2025
- Included studies using **validated HRQL measures** pre- and post-medicinal cannabis treatment for any chronic health condition
- Aimed to determine how studies justify, measure, and report HRQL, and assess current evidence

> Source: [PMC12862010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12862010/) | [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400337839)

### QUEST Initiative — 12-Month Follow-Up (2025)

Published in *PLOS ONE* (April 2025), Tait et al.:
- Australian observational study of patients prescribed medicinal cannabis
- Found improvements in health-related quality of life are **maintained long-term** (12-month follow-up)

> Source: [PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0320756)

### Project Twenty21 — Real-World Evidence from the UK

The UK's largest medical cannabis registry (launched August 2020), run by Drug Science:

**Preliminary findings (Sakal et al., 2021):**
- Described characteristics of first patients enrolled in the registry
- UK legalized medical cannabis in November 2018, but NHS prescriptions have been extremely rare — most patients access through private clinics

**3-Month Outcomes (Lynskey et al., 2023):**
- **2,833 patients** seeking medicinal cannabis for any indication (baseline data)
- **1,410 patients** at 3-month follow-up (anxiety disorders, chronic pain, or PTSD)
- **Consistent improvements** in disease-specific symptomatology and general health/quality of life at 3 months
- Effect sizes (Cohen's d): **0.50 to 1.17** — medium to large
- Among chronic pain patients using opioids: **moderate reduction in morphine equivalents** from 22.5 to 8.3 mg/day (Cohen's d = 0.29)
- Adverse events were rare

**Chronic Pain with Comorbid Depression (Athanasiou-Fragkouli et al., 2024):**
- 1,816 chronic pain patients at baseline; 1,058 at 3-month follow-up
- Prescribed cannabis associated with **marked reductions in pain severity and interference** and improvements in quality of life
- Patients with comorbid depression derived benefit, though the study examined whether they benefited equally

**Limitations**: Project Twenty21 is **observational and uncontrolled**. There is no placebo group, no randomization, and significant selection bias (patients who seek and pay for private medical cannabis are a self-selected group). Dropout between baseline and follow-up (2,833 → 1,410) may introduce survivorship bias.

> Sources: [Sakal et al. 2021](https://scispace.com/papers/developing-a-real-world-evidence-base-for-prescribed-4ktcklyj7x) | [Lynskey et al. 2023](https://www.cannabisclinicians.org/2023/05/06/characteristics-of-and-3-month-health-outcomes-for-people-seeking-treatment-with-prescribed-cannabis-real-world-evidence-from-project-twenty21/) | [Drug Science — Chronic Pain + Depression](https://www.drugscience.org.uk/chronicpain-depression-cannabis-t21)

## 7. The Gap Between Hype and Evidence

### The UCLA/JAMA Comprehensive Review (December 2025)

The most important single document for understanding the hype-evidence gap:

**Study**: Led by Dr. Michael Hsu (UCLA Health), published in *JAMA* (December 2025)
- Reviewed **more than 2,500 articles** from January 2010 through September 2025
- Included RCTs, meta-analyses, and clinical guidelines
- More than 120 studies prioritized by sample size, recency, and relevance

**Key findings:**

> "Our review highlights significant gaps between public perception and scientific evidence regarding its effectiveness for most medical conditions." — Dr. Michael Hsu

- **Only specific FDA-approved cannabinoid medications** have demonstrated clear benefits: HIV/AIDS-related appetite loss, chemotherapy-induced nausea, and certain pediatric seizure disorders
- For **most other conditions** — including chronic pain, anxiety, and insomnia — **the scientific support remains weak**
- **Cannabis use disorder**: Nearly **30% of medical cannabis users** meet the criteria for cannabis use disorder
- Cannabis is legal for medical use in **40 U.S. states**, fueling a **$32 billion industry** — but most products sold at dispensaries are NOT FDA-approved medications
- The review raised particular safety concerns about **high-potency cannabis use among young people**, linked to higher rates of mental health issues

> "The evidence does not support the use of cannabis or cannabinoids at this point for most of the indications that folks are using it for." — Dr. Michael Hsu, *The New York Times*

> Source: [UCLA Health Press Release](https://www.uclahealth.org/news/release/evidence-lacking-medical-cannabis-most-conditions) | [iHeart/JAMA coverage](https://waco100.iheart.com/content/2025-12-17-large-cannabis-study-warns-that-many-medical-claims-lack-scientific-proof/) | [Powers Health summary](https://www.powershealth.org/about-us/newsroom/health-library/2025/12/15/large-review-finds-limited-proof-behind-many-medical-cannabis-uses)

### Cannabis Use Disorder Risk

**Prevalence in medical cannabis users:**
- Systematic review and meta-analysis (Sciencedirect, 2024) found **22% prevalence** (95% CI: 18-26%) of cannabis use disorders among recreational users
- The UCLA JAMA review cites **~30% of medical cannabis users** meeting CUD criteria
- CDC: "Some people who use cannabis will develop cannabis use disorder, meaning that they are unable to stop using cannabis even though it's causing health and social problems in their lives"
- Psychology Today summary: People who use cannabis have a **1 in 5 risk** of either abuse or dependence; among weekly/daily users, **over a third** are dependent
- The oft-cited "9.1%" figure from the 1990s National Comorbidity Survey is outdated — products are now far more potent

> Sources: [CDC — Cannabis Use Disorder](https://www.cdc.gov/cannabis/health-effects/cannabis-use-disorder.html) | [ScienceDirect meta-analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0376871624001844) | [Yale Medicine](https://www.yalemedicine.org/conditions/marijuana-use-disorder)

### The Australian Regulatory Reckoning (February 2026)

The Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP) submitted to the TGA calling for **major reforms**:
- Requirement for all medicinal cannabis products to be approved on the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods
- Concern about "lack of ethical guidance and professionalism among some prescribers"
- High rate of prescribing of **THC-dominant products**
- Concern about unethical telehealth prescribing
- The TGA consultation was launched in response to **public concern over safety risks**, particularly for high-strength THC products

> Source: [RACGP submission, Feb 2026](https://www1.racgp.org.au/newsgp/professional/major-public-health-risk-racgp-backs-medicinal-can)

### The Wellness Industry Problem

The $32 billion cannabis industry benefits from a regulatory gray zone:
- Dispensary products are **not FDA-approved medications** — they don't go through clinical trials
- Products vary enormously in potency, composition, and contaminants
- Marketing claims often reference the legitimate medical evidence (epilepsy, nausea) to justify unproven uses
- The "natural" framing bypasses the rigor expected of pharmaceuticals
- CBD wellness products (tinctures, gummies, topicals) represent a massive market built on extrapolation from Epidiolex's epilepsy data to general "wellness" — a leap the evidence does not support
- 27% of U.S./Canadian adults report using cannabis for medical purposes — far outstripping the conditions where strong evidence exists

## Summary: What We Actually Know

**Where the evidence holds up:**
1. **CBD for rare pediatric epilepsies** (Dravet, LGS, TSC) — pharmaceutical-grade Epidiolex, multiple RCTs, FDA approved
2. **Synthetic cannabinoids for chemo nausea** — dronabinol, nabilone, FDA approved, moderate-strong evidence
3. **Dronabinol for HIV/AIDS appetite stimulation** — FDA approved
4. **Nabiximols for MS spasticity** — approved in several countries (not US)

**Where the evidence is suggestive but limited:**
5. **Chronic neuropathic pain** — small benefits from high-THC products, weighed against significant side effects
6. **Quality of life improvements** — consistent in observational studies, but no RCT evidence

**Where the evidence says no or is actively harmful:**
7. **Depression** — cannabis is associated with worse outcomes, not better
8. **Anxiety** — acute relief masks chronic worsening; creates dependence cycle
9. **Cancer treatment** — entirely preclinical; no basis for therapeutic claims
10. **General wellness/CBD products** — no meaningful evidence for the vast majority of marketed claims

**The uncomfortable truth**: The conditions with strong evidence (epilepsy, chemo nausea) account for a tiny fraction of medical cannabis use. The conditions that drive the $32 billion industry (pain, anxiety, sleep, depression) have weak, mixed, or negative evidence.

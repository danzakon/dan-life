# Vyvanse (Lisdexamfetamine): Cardiovascular & Long-term Physical Health Risks

## Raw Research Findings — February 2026

---

## Summary

Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) and other ADHD stimulants carry a modest but real long-term cardiovascular risk profile that is dose- and duration-dependent. The largest population studies (Swedish registers, n=278,027; Danish registers, n=73,264) consistently show that cumulative ADHD medication use beyond 2-3 years is associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk, particularly hypertension and arterial disease. Short-term hemodynamic effects are small (systolic BP +1-3 mmHg, heart rate +3-6 bpm per RCT meta-analysis), but these compound over years. Stimulant-associated cardiomyopathy is an emerging concern in young adults, though incidence data is lacking. Sudden cardiac death risk at therapeutic doses appears extremely low in structurally normal hearts. Growth suppression in children is well-documented (1-2 cm height deficit), with incomplete rebound by adulthood. The neurotoxicity question at therapeutic doses remains largely preclinical, with no established evidence of neuronal damage in humans at prescribed doses. All-cause mortality data actually favors treatment — ADHD medication initiation is associated with reduced all-cause and unnatural-cause mortality.

---

## Key Findings

- **Cardiovascular disease risk increases with cumulative duration**: Each additional year of ADHD medication use is associated with ~4% increased CVD risk (AOR 1.04, 95% CI 1.03–1.05), with steeper increases in the first 3 years (~8% per year) ([Zhang et al., JAMA Psychiatry 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/2811812))
- **Hypertension is the dominant long-term CV risk**: AOR 1.80 (95% CI 1.55–2.08) after >5 years of cumulative use vs. nonuse
- **10-year stroke risk elevated at higher doses**: 2.1% vs 1.7% in high-dose (≥1 DDD/day) vs. prior users; heart failure risk ratio 1.7 (95% CI 1.3–2.2) ([Holt et al., JACC 2024](https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2024.03.375))
- **Short-term BP/HR effects are small**: Most ADHD medications increase SBP by ~1-3 mmHg and pulse by ~3-6 bpm in RCTs ([Farhat et al., Lancet Psychiatry 2025](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(25)00062-8/abstract))
- **Stimulant-associated cardiomyopathy**: Emerging case reports of dilated cardiomyopathy in young adults on long-term amphetamine prescriptions; often reversible with discontinuation
- **Mortality**: ADHD medication *reduces* all-cause mortality (HR 0.79 for all-cause, 0.76 for unnatural-cause) ([Li et al., JAMA 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2816084))
- **Growth suppression**: ~1-2 cm adult height deficit with sustained stimulant use through childhood; partial but incomplete rebound
- **No established human neurotoxicity at therapeutic doses**: Preclinical animal data at supertherapeutic doses only

## 1. Cardiovascular Effects

### 1a. Blood Pressure and Heart Rate (Short-Term RCT Data)

**The 2025 Lancet Psychiatry Network Meta-Analysis** (Farhat, Cortese et al.)
- Largest and most comprehensive RCT-based analysis of CV effects of ADHD medications
- 102 RCTs, 22,702 participants (13,315 children/adolescents + 9,387 adults)
- Median follow-up: 7 weeks (IQR 5–9)
- **All ADHD medications** (except guanfacine) were associated with small increases in BP and heart rate
- Effects were generally small and comparable across stimulants and non-stimulants (except guanfacine, which lowered both)
- Researchers noted they "cannot exclude that a subgroup of individuals may have a higher risk of more substantial cardiovascular alterations"
- **Clinical implication**: Short-term hemodynamic effects alone seem minor, but the concern is cumulative long-term consequences

**Typical ranges reported across studies:**
- Systolic BP increase: +1 to +4 mmHg
- Diastolic BP increase: +1 to +3 mmHg  
- Heart rate increase: +3 to +6 bpm
- These are averages — individual responses vary considerably

**Source**: [Farhat et al., Lancet Psychiatry, April 2025](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(25)00062-8/abstract); [Physicians Weekly summary](https://www.physiciansweekly.com/post/how-adhd-medications-impact-heart-health-the-risk-benefit-equation)

### 1b. Long-Term Cardiovascular Disease Risk

**The Zhang et al. Karolinska Study (JAMA Psychiatry, Feb 2024)**
- **Design**: Nested case-control study using Swedish national registers
- **Population**: 278,027 individuals aged 6–64 with incident ADHD diagnosis or medication dispensation, 2007–2020
- **Cases**: 10,388 with new CVD diagnosis; matched to 51,672 controls
- **Key finding**: Longer cumulative ADHD medication use was associated with dose-dependent increased CVD risk

**Specific Adjusted Odds Ratios (AOR) for CVD vs. nonuse:**

| Cumulative Duration | AOR (95% CI) |
|---|---|
| 0 to ≤1 year | 0.99 (0.93–1.06) |
| 1 to ≤2 years | 1.09 (1.01–1.18) |
| 2 to ≤3 years | 1.15 (1.05–1.25) |
| 3 to ≤5 years | **1.27 (1.17–1.39)** |
| >5 years | **1.23 (1.12–1.36)** |

- **Non-linear pattern**: Risk increases most steeply in first 3 years of cumulative use (8% per year), then stabilizes
- **Per-year increase over full follow-up**: AOR 1.04 (95% CI 1.03–1.05) per additional year
- **Higher doses = higher risk**: Mean DDD >2 (e.g., methylphenidate >60mg) → AOR 1.05 per year; mean DDD 1.5–2 → AOR 1.04 per year

**CVD subtype analysis:**

| CVD Type | AOR at 3–5 years | AOR at >5 years |
|---|---|---|
| **Hypertension** | **1.72 (1.51–1.97)** | **1.80 (1.55–2.08)** |
| **Arterial disease** | **1.65 (1.11–2.45)** | 1.49 (0.96–2.32) |
| Arrhythmias | Not significant | Not significant |
| Heart failure | Not significant | Not significant |
| Ischemic heart disease | Not significant | Not significant |
| Cerebrovascular disease | Not significant | Not significant |
| Thromboembolic disease | Not significant | Not significant |

- **Lisdexamfetamine specifically**: Associated with increased CVD risk, with results adjusting for other ADHD medication use. Methylphenidate showed AOR 1.20 (1.10–1.31) at 3–5 years and AOR 1.19 (1.08–1.31) at >5 years.

**Source**: [Zhang et al., JAMA Psychiatry 2024;81(2):178-187](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10851097/); [Full PDF via ePrints Soton](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/488997/1/jamapsychiatry_zhang_2023_oi_230086_1706192516.35527.pdf)

### 1c. The Danish Registers Study (JACC, May 2024)

**Holt et al. — "Long-Term Cardiovascular Risk Associated With Treatment of ADHD in Adults"**
- **Design**: Population-based cohort study using Danish nationwide registers
- **Population**: 73,264 adults first-time initiated on ADHD treatment, 1998–2020
- **Exposure groups**: Prior users, <1 DDD/day, ≥1 DDD/day
- **Follow-up**: Up to 10 years

**Key 10-year absolute risk comparisons (≥1 DDD/day vs. prior users):**

| Outcome | ≥1 DDD/day (10-yr risk) | Prior Users (10-yr risk) | Risk Ratio (95% CI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stroke** | 2.1% (1.8–2.4%) | 1.7% (1.5–1.9%) | **1.2 (1.0–1.5)** |
| **Heart failure** | 1.2% (0.9–1.4%) | 0.7% (0.6–0.8%) | **1.7 (1.3–2.2)** |
| **Composite CV outcome** | 3.9% (3.4–4.3%) | 3.0% (2.8–3.2%) | **1.3 (1.1–1.5)** |
| Acute coronary syndrome | 1.0% (0.8–1.2%) | 0.9% (0.8–1.0%) | Not significant |

- Higher dosage (≥1 DDD/day) associated with greater risk, supporting a dose-response relationship
- **No significant association found for acute coronary syndrome**
- This was the first large study to show long-term stroke and heart failure risk specifically

**Source**: [Holt et al., JACC 2024;83(19):1870-1882](https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2024.03.375)

### 1d. Karolinska Thesis (February 2026)

Honghui Yao's PhD thesis from Karolinska Institutet found that ADHD medication is linked to heart disease risk, that people with ADHD are more likely to smoke and use snus, and that young adults are the most likely to discontinue medication. This builds on the earlier Zhang et al. findings from the same institution.

**Source**: [Mirage News, Feb 24 2026](https://www.miragenews.com/adhd-medications-linked-to-heart-disease-risk-1625359/)

## 2. Cardiomyopathy Risk

### Stimulant-Associated Cardiomyopathy — Emerging Evidence

**What we know:**
- Multiple case reports and case series have described **dilated cardiomyopathy** in young adults taking prescribed amphetamine-based ADHD medications (Adderall, Vyvanse) at therapeutic or near-therapeutic doses
- Distinct from methamphetamine-associated cardiomyopathy (which occurs at much higher doses and with illicit use), though mechanisms overlap

**Key publications:**

1. **Habeb et al. (Cureus, March 2025)** — "Prolonged Amphetamine-Dextroamphetamine Use: An Unrecognized Cause of Cardiomyopathy"
   - Case report of cardiomyopathy in a patient on long-term prescribed amphetamine-dextroamphetamine
   - Authors argue this is an *underrecognized* cause of heart failure in young patients
   - Published: [PMC 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11993926/)

2. **Cureus, June 2023** — "Amphetamine-Dextroamphetamine-Induced Cardiomyopathy, an Emerging Cause of Heart Failure in Young Patient Populations"
   - Case study describing cardiomyopathy from the study drug
   - [PMC 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10368484/)

3. **ACC/JACC 2025 presentations**:
   - "Speedy Heart, Risky Business: A Case of Adderall-Induced Cardiomyopathy in a Young Female" (Nugooru et al.)
   - "Long Term High Dose Adderall Use Resulting in Non-Ischemic Cardiomyopathy" (McAuliffe et al.)
   - Both presented at the American College of Cardiology 2025 Scientific Sessions
   - [JACC 2025, Vol. 85 No. 12 Supplement](https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/S0735-1097(25)03712-X)

**Mechanism:**
- Chronic sympathomimetic stimulation → increased catecholamine levels → direct myocardial toxicity
- Sustained tachycardia → tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy
- Catecholamine-mediated oxidative stress and myocyte apoptosis
- Peripheral vasoconstriction increasing afterload

**Reversibility:**
- **Often reversible** with drug discontinuation and standard heart failure therapy
- Methamphetamine-associated cardiomyopathy studies show reverse remodeling is possible with cessation and guideline-directed medical therapy ([Richards et al., Med Res Arch, June 2024](https://esmed.org/MRA/mra/article/view/5458))
- Recovery timeline: weeks to months after discontinuation
- Some patients may have permanent damage, especially with prolonged exposure

**How common?**
- **Unknown** — no population-level incidence data exist
- Currently described only in case reports/series
- Likely underrecognized because clinicians may not consider prescribed stimulants as a cause
- The Zhang et al. JAMA Psychiatry study notably did **not** find a statistically significant increase in heart failure with long-term ADHD medication use (though JACC Danish data did show heart failure risk ratio of 1.7)

**Certainty level**: ⚠️ **Emerging/uncertain** — individual case reports and ACC presentations, no controlled epidemiological data on incidence

## 3. Sudden Cardiac Death

### Evidence For and Against

**FDA position:**
- The FDA black box warning on amphetamines (including Vyvanse, as of October 2023 label update) addresses **abuse, misuse, and addiction** — not sudden cardiac death directly
- The warning states: "Misuse of amphetamines may cause sudden death and serious cardiovascular adverse events"
- Separate warnings in the prescribing information note: "Sudden death has been reported in association with CNS stimulant treatment at recommended doses in pediatric patients with structural cardiac abnormalities or other serious heart problems"
- For adults: "Sudden deaths, stroke, and myocardial infarction have been reported in adults taking stimulant drugs at recommended doses for ADHD"

**FDA 2011 Safety Review Update:**
- FDA conducted a comprehensive safety review of ADHD medications in adults
- Conclusion: The data did not show an increased risk of serious cardiovascular events with ADHD medication use in adults without pre-existing conditions
- However, FDA noted limitations and maintained warnings

**Key epidemiological evidence:**
- Multiple large population studies (including a meta-analysis of observational studies) have **not** found a statistically significant association between ADHD medication use at therapeutic doses and sudden cardiac death in individuals without pre-existing structural heart disease
- The absolute risk is estimated to be **extremely low** in structurally normal hearts

**Who is at highest risk:**
- Patients with **pre-existing structural cardiac abnormalities** (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, congenital heart disease)
- Patients with **channelopathies** (long QT syndrome, Brugada syndrome, Wolff-Parkinson-White)
- Patients with **known arrhythmias**
- Those using at **supratherapeutic doses** or combining with other sympathomimetic agents
- Pre-existing cardiomyopathy

**Clinical recommendations:**
- Pre-treatment cardiovascular screening (personal and family cardiac history) is standard of care
- Routine ECG screening before starting stimulants is **not** universally recommended (varies by guideline)
- Ongoing heart rate and blood pressure monitoring is recommended throughout treatment
- Canadian guidelines advise stimulants "should not be used if a patient has symptomatic cardiac disease, moderate to severe hypertension, advanced arteriosclerosis, or hyperthyroidism"

**Source**: [FDA Safety Communication](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-safety-and-availability/fda-drug-safety-communication-safety-review-update-medications-used-treat-attention-0); [Vyvanse Prescribing Information 2023](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2023/021977s050,208510s007lbl.pdf); [CADDRA cardiac risks PDF](https://caddra.ca/pdfs/cardiac.pdf)

**Certainty level**: ✅ **Well-established** — sudden cardiac death risk is extremely low at therapeutic doses in those without structural heart disease; appropriately elevated concern in those with pre-existing conditions

## 4. Growth Effects

### Height and Weight Suppression in Children/Adolescents

**The MTA Study — Landmark longitudinal data:**

- **Swanson et al. (JAACAP, 2007)**: Across 3 years of MTA follow-up, consistent stimulant use was associated with growth rate suppression
  - Height growth suppression: ~1 cm/year less than expected during first 1-2 years
  - Weight growth suppression: ~1-3 kg less than expected
  - Effect was most pronounced in the first year of treatment

- **Greenhill, Swanson et al. (JAACAP, 2020)**: Long-term MTA trajectories through adolescence
  - Continued medication use associated with persistent but modest height deficit
  - Growth velocity partially recovered after initial suppression
  - The deficit stabilized rather than continuing to widen

**Wojnowski et al. (J Pediatr Endocrinol Metab, 2022)** — "Effect of stimulants on final adult height"
- Literature review of studies measuring final or near-final adult height
- **Key conclusion**: Multiple clinical trials indicated a reduction in adult height potential for children with long-term stimulant use
- **Estimated adult height deficit**: approximately **1–2 cm** (some studies show up to 2.5 cm)
- However, other studies showed no significant impact on final adult height
- **The evidence is conflicting** but leans toward a small net deficit

**Mechanism:**
- Appetite suppression → caloric deficit → reduced growth
- Possible direct effects on growth hormone pulsatility (some evidence of altered GH/IGF-1 axis)
- Sympathomimetic effects may affect bone metabolism

**Rebound growth after discontinuation:**
- Partial rebound in growth velocity is observed when stimulants are stopped
- "Drug holidays" (e.g., summers off) may partially mitigate growth effects
- However, complete catch-up to predicted adult height appears **incomplete** in most long-term studies

**Vyvanse prescribing label note:**
- "Pediatric patients with ADHD younger than 6 years of age experienced more long-term weight loss than patients 6 years and older"
- Growth monitoring is recommended during treatment

**Source**: [Goldman, Can Fam Physician 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2821235/); [Wojnowski et al., JPEM 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9627528/); [Greenhill et al., JAACAP 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7021562/); [Richardson et al., Transl Pediatr 2017](https://tp.amegroups.org/article/view/16972/html)

**Certainty level**: ✅ **Well-established** — growth suppression during treatment is clear; impact on final adult height is probable (~1-2 cm) but with conflicting data

## 5. Other Organ Systems

### 5a. Peripheral Vascular Effects (Raynaud's Phenomenon)

- **Case report (2025)**: Raynaud's phenomenon during treatment with lisdexamfetamine, with discussion of risk of cerebral vasospasm
  - Published in Einstein (São Paulo), October 2025
  - Authors raised concern that peripheral vasospasm (Raynaud's) could be a sentinel sign of broader vascular reactivity, including potential cerebral vasospasm risk
  - [Pitliuk & Fucci, Einstein 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12539819/)
- Raynaud's phenomenon is listed as a known adverse effect of amphetamines in the prescribing information
- Mechanism: amphetamine-induced peripheral vasoconstriction via norepinephrine release
- FDA Vyvanse label includes "peripheral vasculopathy, including Raynaud's phenomenon" under Warnings and Precautions

### 5b. Sexual Dysfunction

- **ADHD itself** is associated with higher rates of sexual dysfunction (both hyper- and hypo-sexual presentations)
- **Stimulant effects on sexual function are bidirectional**:
  - Some patients report *improved* sexual function (potentially via improved focus, reduced impulsivity, dopaminergic effects)
  - Others report erectile dysfunction, decreased libido, or delayed orgasm
  - Amphetamines can cause priapism (rare but listed in Vyvanse label)
- **Bieś et al. (Pharmaceuticals, May 2025)**: Systematic review of methylphenidate and sexual function — found both beneficial and adverse sexual effects depending on the individual, suggesting the relationship is complex and likely mediated by the underlying ADHD
- **Soldati et al. (J Sex Med, 2020)**: Systematic review finding ADHD itself is associated with sexual dysfunctions, and stimulant treatment may improve some aspects while worsening others
- **No large-scale data specifically for lisdexamfetamine and sexual dysfunction**

**Source**: [Bieś et al., Pharmaceuticals 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12114644/); [Soldati et al., J Sex Med 2020](https://www.ovid.com/journals/jsmed/pdf/10.1016/j.jsxm.2020.03.019)

### 5c. Liver Effects

- Lisdexamfetamine is a prodrug converted to d-amphetamine in the blood (via red blood cell hydrolysis), not requiring hepatic activation
- **No significant hepatotoxicity** has been reported with lisdexamfetamine at therapeutic doses
- This contrasts with atomoxetine, which has rare reports of severe liver injury
- Vyvanse prescribing information does not include liver-specific warnings

### 5d. Kidney Effects

- **No established nephrotoxicity** at therapeutic doses
- Amphetamines can cause rhabdomyolysis in overdose or extreme exertion, which can secondarily damage kidneys
- Chronic hypertension (a known long-term risk per the Zhang et al. data) can of course lead to hypertensive nephropathy over decades
- No specific renal warnings in the Vyvanse label

### 5e. Bone Density

- **Very limited data**
- Theoretical concern: chronic appetite suppression → reduced calcium/nutritional intake → impaired bone accrual in children
- Growth suppression could theoretically affect bone mineral density
- No controlled studies have specifically examined bone density in long-term stimulant users
- Not addressed in the prescribing information

**Certainty level**: ⚠️ Raynaud's is well-documented but uncommon; sexual effects are complex and bidirectional; liver/kidney/bone effects are minimal or theoretical

## 6. Neurotoxicity

### The 2025 Neurotoxicity Paper

**Hasanzadeh, Beirami, Bayat & Aliaghaei — "Neurotoxicity of lisdexamfetamine: Implications for neuronal health and central nervous system function"**
- Published in *Advanced Neurology*, 2025 (AccScience Publishing)
- DOI: [10.36922/AN025080017](https://accscience.com/journal/AN/4/3/10.36922/AN025080017)
- Authors affiliated with Shahid Beheshti University of Medical Sciences, Iran

**What the paper covers:**
- Comprehensive review of LDX (lisdexamfetamine) as a d-amphetamine prodrug
- Reviews evidence for neurotoxic potential across preclinical literature
- Examines mechanisms: oxidative stress, dopaminergic neurotoxicity, neuroinflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction

**What the evidence actually shows:**

1. **At supertherapeutic/abuse-level doses** (animal models):
   - Amphetamines are well-established neurotoxins at high doses
   - Mechanism: excessive dopamine release → oxidative stress → dopaminergic terminal degeneration
   - Can damage serotonergic and dopaminergic neurons
   - Effects include neuroinflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and apoptosis

2. **At therapeutic doses** (the critical distinction):
   - **No established evidence of neurotoxicity in humans at prescribed therapeutic doses**
   - The prodrug mechanism of LDX limits peak plasma concentrations, theoretically providing a wider safety margin
   - LDX produces a slower, more gradual rise in d-amphetamine levels compared to immediate-release formulations
   - Animal studies at therapeutic-equivalent doses generally do not show the same patterns of neuronal damage seen at high doses

3. **Important caveats:**
   - Long-term (decades-long) human data at therapeutic doses is limited
   - Individual variability in metabolism could create higher-than-expected brain concentrations in some individuals
   - The developing brain (children/adolescents) may have different vulnerability thresholds
   - Most neurotoxicity research is on methamphetamine or high-dose d-amphetamine, not LDX specifically

**Context on the journal:**
- *Advanced Neurology* (AccScience Publishing) — relatively new, open-access journal
- Not a top-tier neurology journal
- The paper is a narrative review, not original research
- Should be interpreted as a literature synthesis, not new evidence

**Source**: [Hasanzadeh et al., Advanced Neurology 2025](https://www.academia.edu/129524267/Neurotoxicity_of_lisdexamfetamine_Implications_for_neuronal_health_and_central_nervous_system_function); [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/392012139)

**Also relevant:** [Multidimensional Evaluation of Lisdexamfetamine: Pharmacology, Therapeutic Use, Toxicity and Forensic Implications](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12464896/) — Basic Clin Pharmacol Toxicol, September 2025. Broader review covering pharmacology, therapeutic use, toxicity, and forensic aspects.

**Certainty level**: ✅ **Well-established** that high-dose amphetamines are neurotoxic in animals; ⚠️ **Uncertain/unlikely** that therapeutic-dose LDX causes neurotoxicity in humans

## 7. Mortality Data

### All-Cause Mortality: Treatment Is Protective

**Li et al. (JAMA, March 2024)** — "ADHD Pharmacotherapy and Mortality in Individuals With ADHD"
- **Design**: Population-based cohort study, Swedish national registers, 2007–2018
- **Population**: Individuals aged 6–64 with new ADHD diagnosis, no prior ADHD medication
- **Follow-up**: 2-year period after medication initiation
- **Key findings**:
  - ADHD medication initiation **significantly decreased** all-cause mortality risk
  - Also significantly decreased **unnatural-cause mortality** (accidents, suicide, etc.)
  - **Did NOT decrease natural-cause mortality** (cancer, cardiovascular disease, etc.)
  - The protective effect was primarily driven by reduction in unnatural deaths

**Source**: [Li et al., JAMA 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2816084); [Psychiatry Advisor summary](https://www.psychiatryadvisor.com/news/adhd-medication-reduces-all-cause-unnatural-cause-mortality-risk/)

**Vasiliadis et al. (Transl Psychiatry, Feb 2024)** — "ADHD medications use and risk of mortality and unintentional injuries: a population-based cohort study"
- Complementary findings from a different population
- ADHD medication use associated with reduced mortality and unintentional injuries
- [Nature 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-024-02825-y)

**Thapar (Psychology Today, Feb 2026)** — Summary perspective:
- People with ADHD have elevated physical health risks (cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cancers, neurological conditions)
- Many risks are modifiable
- "ADHD medication treatment reduces some health risks and mortality"

**The paradox:**
- ADHD medication may modestly increase cardiovascular risk markers (BP, HR, hypertension)
- But it substantially reduces overall mortality, primarily through reduction in accidental deaths, self-harm, and risky behaviors
- This suggests the net benefit of treatment likely outweighs the cardiovascular risk for most patients

**Certainty level**: ✅ **Well-established** — ADHD medication reduces all-cause mortality, primarily via unnatural-cause reduction

## Distinction: Well-Established vs. Emerging/Uncertain

### Well-Established Findings
- Short-term hemodynamic effects (small BP/HR increases)
- Long-term hypertension risk with cumulative use
- Growth suppression during treatment in children
- No sudden cardiac death signal at therapeutic doses in structurally normal hearts
- All-cause mortality reduction with treatment
- Raynaud's phenomenon as a recognized (uncommon) side effect

### Emerging/Uncertain Findings
- Stimulant-associated cardiomyopathy in prescribed-dose users (case reports only)
- Magnitude of final adult height impact (conflicting data, likely ~1-2 cm)
- Long-term stroke and heart failure risk (single large study, dose-dependent)
- Whether the cardiovascular risk plateau after ~3 years of cumulative use is real or an artifact
- Cerebral vasospasm risk associated with peripheral Raynaud's
- Any neurotoxicity at therapeutic doses in humans
- Precise bone density effects
- Long-term kidney effects via chronic hypertension pathway
